# Historical Reasons

**November 11, 2025**

[![Untitled](https://live.staticflickr.com/65535/54905274610_7b1ac67de2_b.jpg)](https://www.flickr.com/photos/mhoye/54905274610/ "Untitled")

In 2011 after [Dennis Ritchie died](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dennis_Ritchie), I wondered if we should start retiring usernames on unix systems as an honorific, in the same way sports teams will retire the numbers of great players. I’d proposed a patch to useradd.c at the time that was rejected; understandable, I suppose, but I ran the modified version on my own machine for a while, to no effect and for no reasons but my own. Those few lines of code might have run a dozen times, inspecting the names of service accounts that they’d never object to, but I knew they were there.

I frequently wonder what building a deeper cultural history into the functioning of the common codebase would look like, and what a shared heritage might mean and maybe gain over time. How history might invisibly accrete, not in load-bearing bugs or temporary-permanence, but in the touchstones of the human history.

You can’t wear [99](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_National_Hockey_League_retired_numbers) in the NHL now, or [6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_NBA_retired_numbers) in the NBA. Maybe you shouldn’t be able to log in as [dmr](https://www.nokia.com/bell-labs/about/dennis-m-ritchie/) for the same reasons.

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Detail_of_Wanamaker_Organ.jpg/250px-Detail_of_Wanamaker_Organ.jpg)](/wiki/File:Detail_of_Wanamaker_Organ.jpg)

The display pipes of the Wanamaker Organ. These pipes are decorative only. The pipes that sound are behind and above them. Store architect [Daniel Hudson Burnham](/wiki/Daniel_Hudson_Burnham "Daniel Hudson Burnham") designed the organ casework.[\[1\]](#cite_note-1)[\[2\]](#cite_note-2)

The **Wanamaker Grand Court Organ**, located in [Philadelphia](/wiki/Philadelphia "Philadelphia"), [Pennsylvania](/wiki/Pennsylvania "Pennsylvania"), is the largest fully functioning [pipe organ](/wiki/Pipe_organ "Pipe organ") in the world, based on the number of playing pipes, the number of ranks and its weight. The Wanamaker Organ is located within a spacious 7-story Grand Court at the [John Wanamaker Store](/wiki/John_Wanamaker_Store "John Wanamaker Store") (originally [Wanamaker's](/wiki/Wanamaker%27s "Wanamaker's") and most recently [Macy's](/wiki/Macy%27s "Macy's")) and was played twice a day Monday through Saturday. The organ was featured at several special concerts held throughout the year, including events featuring the Friends of the Wanamaker Organ Festival Chorus and Brass Ensemble.

## Notable characteristics

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Wanamaker_Organ&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Notable characteristics")\]

The Wanamaker Organ is the largest [pipe organ](/wiki/Pipe_organ "Pipe organ") in the world when measured by the number of ranks and its weight.[\[3\]](#cite_note-3)[\[4\]](#cite_note-sacredclassics-4) It is a concert organ of the American Symphonic school of design, which combines traditional organ tone with the sonic colors of the symphony orchestra. In its present configuration, the instrument has 28,762 [pipes](/wiki/Organ_pipe "Organ pipe") in 465 ranks.[\[5\]](#cite_note-biswanger-5)

The [organ console](/wiki/Organ_console "Organ console") consists of six [manuals](/wiki/Manual_\(music\) "Manual (music)") with an array of [stops](/wiki/Organ_stop "Organ stop") and controls that command the organ. The organ's _String Division_ fills the largest single organ chamber in the world. The division features eighty-eight ranks of [string pipes](/wiki/String_\(pipe_organ\) "String (pipe organ)") built to Wanamaker specifications by the [W.W. Kimball Company](/wiki/W.W._Kimball_Company "W.W. Kimball Company") of Chicago.[\[5\]](#cite_note-biswanger-5)

The organ is famed for its [orchestra](/wiki/Orchestra "Orchestra")\-like sound, coming from pipes that are voiced softer than usual, allowing an unusually rich build-up because of the massing of pipe-[tone](/wiki/Timbre "Timbre") families. The organ was also built and enlarged as an "art organ", using exceptional craftsmanship and lavish application of materials to create a luxury product beyond what is generally commercially available.

There is a minimum of borrowing and unification in its disciplined design, except in the Pedal and Orchestral divisions, where it adds genuine value, and duplexing is reserved for when valuable solo voices can be separated from their divisions without tying up the remaining tonal resources of said division. Choruses (16', 8', 4') are true choruses of three ranks, each with their own personality, rather than a single rank electrically "tapped" at three pitches, with the resulting weakening of the octaves and sameness of tone between the voices as found in unification.[\[6\]](#cite_note-6)

The [Wanamaker Store](/wiki/Wanamaker%27s "Wanamaker's") maintained its own organ factory to ensure an ultra-high-grade result. The artistic obligation entailed by the creation of this instrument has always been honored, with two [curators](/wiki/Curator "Curator") employed in its constant and scrupulous care (which leads to its status as one of the best maintained organs in the world). This dedication was enhanced when corporate parentage shifted from the Wanamaker family to [Carter Hawley Hale Stores](/wiki/Carter_Hawley_Hale_Stores "Carter Hawley Hale Stores") followed by [Woodward & Lothrop](/wiki/Woodward_%26_Lothrop "Woodward & Lothrop"), [The May Department Stores Company](/wiki/The_May_Department_Stores_Company "The May Department Stores Company"), and [Lord & Taylor](/wiki/Lord_%26_Taylor "Lord & Taylor"). When the space was occupied by [Macy's](/wiki/Macy%27s "Macy's") and with the founding of the Friends of the Wanamaker Organ with its input of outside capital, an aggressive restoration schedule developed and is yet maintained.

## History

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Wanamaker_Organ&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/WanamakerBuilding2.jpg/250px-WanamakerBuilding2.jpg)](/wiki/File:WanamakerBuilding2.jpg)

The Wanamaker Organ centennial plaque

The Wanamaker Organ was originally built by the [Los Angeles Art Organ Company](/wiki/Los_Angeles_Art_Organ_Company "Los Angeles Art Organ Company"), successors to the [Murray M. Harris](/wiki/Murray_M._Harris "Murray M. Harris") Organ Co., for the Kansas City Convention Hall. Before it was placed there, it was exhibited at the [1904 St. Louis World's Fair](/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase_Exposition "Louisiana Purchase Exposition"). It was designed to be the largest organ in the world, an imitation of a full-size orchestra with particularly complete resources of full organ tone including mixtures. In addition to its console, the organ was originally equipped with an automatic player that used punched rolls of paper, according to the _[Los Angeles Times](/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times "Los Angeles Times")_ of 1904.[\[7\]](#cite_note-7) It was built to a specification by renowned organ theorist and architect [George Ashdown Audsley](/wiki/George_Ashdown_Audsley "George Ashdown Audsley"). Wild cost overruns plagued the project, with the result that Harris was ousted from his own company. With capital from stockholder [Eben Smith](/wiki/Eben_Smith "Eben Smith"), it was reorganized as the Los Angeles Art Organ Company, and finished at a cost of $105,000 (equal to $3,674,611 today), $40,000 over budget, equal to $1,399,852 today. The Fair began (in late April 1904) before the organ was fully installed in its temporary home, Festival Hall. Although the organ's debut was scheduled for May 1, official fair organist and St. Louis local [Charles Henry Galloway](/wiki/Charles_Henry_Galloway "Charles Henry Galloway") did not give his opening recital until June 9. The organ was still not entirely finished in September of that year, when [Alexandre Guilmant](/wiki/Alexandre_Guilmant "Alexandre Guilmant"), one of the most famous organists of the day, presented 40 very well-attended recitals on the organ.

Following the Fair, the organ was intended for permanent installation at the [Kansas City Convention Center](/wiki/Kansas_City_Convention_Center "Kansas City Convention Center"). Indeed, the original console had a prominent "KC" on its music rack. This venture failed, saddling the L. A. Art Organ company with debt after the Fair closed. The firm was reorganized as the Electrolian Company under principal shareholder [Eben Smith](/wiki/Eben_Smith "Eben Smith").There was a plan to exhibit the organ at [Coney Island](/wiki/Coney_Island "Coney Island") in New York City, but nothing came of this.

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Wanamaker_Organ_1904.jpg/250px-Wanamaker_Organ_1904.jpg)](/wiki/File:Wanamaker_Organ_1904.jpg)

The organ in its original place of exhibition, the [1904 World's Fair](/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase_Exposition "Louisiana Purchase Exposition"). This facade was formerly installed at [Macy's](/wiki/Macy%27s "Macy's"), it used to be behind the current facade.

The organ languished in storage at the Handlan warehouse in [St. Louis](/wiki/St._Louis "St. Louis") until 1909, when it was bought by [John Wanamaker](/wiki/John_Wanamaker "John Wanamaker") for his new department store at 13th and Market Streets in [Center City, Philadelphia](/wiki/Center_City,_Philadelphia "Center City, Philadelphia"). It took thirteen [freight cars](/wiki/Rail_freight_transport "Rail freight transport") to move it to its new home, and two years for installation. It was first played on June 22, 1911, at the exact moment when British [King George V](/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom "George V of the United Kingdom") was [crowned](/wiki/Coronation_of_King_George_V_and_Queen_Mary "Coronation of King George V and Queen Mary"). It was also featured later that year when U.S. President [William Howard Taft](/wiki/William_Howard_Taft "William Howard Taft") dedicated the store.

Despite its then-unprecedented size (more than 10,000 pipes), it was judged inadequate to fill the seven-story Grand Court in which it was located, so Wanamaker's opened a private organ factory in the store attic, which was charged with enlarging the organ. The first project to enlarge the organ was the addition of 8,000 pipes between 1911 and 1917.

Wanamaker's sponsored many historic after-business-hours concerts on the Wanamaker Organ. The first, in 1919, featured [Leopold Stokowski](/wiki/Leopold_Stokowski "Leopold Stokowski") and the [Philadelphia Orchestra](/wiki/Philadelphia_Orchestra "Philadelphia Orchestra") with organist [Charles M. Courboin](/wiki/Charles_M._Courboin "Charles M. Courboin").[\[5\]](#cite_note-biswanger-5) Every sales counter and fixture was removed for the free after-hours event, which attracted an audience of 15,000 from across the [United States](/wiki/United_States "United States"). Subsequently, more of these "Musicians' Assemblies" were held, as were private recitals. For these events Wanamaker's opened a Concert Bureau under [Alexander Russell](/wiki/Alexander_Russell_\(organ_impresario\) "Alexander Russell (organ impresario)") and brought to America master organists [Marcel Dupré](/wiki/Marcel_Dupr%C3%A9 "Marcel Dupré") and [Louis Vierne](/wiki/Louis_Vierne "Louis Vierne"), [Nadia Boulanger](/wiki/Nadia_Boulanger "Nadia Boulanger"), [Marco Enrico Bossi](/wiki/Marco_Enrico_Bossi "Marco Enrico Bossi"), [Alfred Hollins](/wiki/Alfred_Hollins "Alfred Hollins"), and several others. (This agency, which worked in partnership with Canadian Bernard R. LaBerge, evolved into the Karen McFarlane Concert Agency of the present day.) During his first recital on the organ, Dupré was so impressed with the instrument that he was inspired to improvise a musical depiction of the life of Jesus Christ. This was later published as his _Symphonie-Passion_.

From April 24, 1922, to 1928 the store had its own radio station, [WOO](/wiki/WOO_\(Philadelphia\) "WOO (Philadelphia)"), and music from the organ was a major feature of the broadcasts.[\[8\]](#cite_note-bcpp-8)

In 1924, a new project to enlarge the organ began. Marcel Dupré and [Charles M. Courboin](/wiki/Charles_M._Courboin "Charles M. Courboin") were among those asked by [Rodman Wanamaker](/wiki/Rodman_Wanamaker "Rodman Wanamaker"), John Wanamaker's son, to "Work together to draw up a plan for the instrument. Use everything you have ever dreamed about." They were told there was no limit to the budget. This project resulted in, among other things, the celebrated String Division, which occupies the largest organ chamber ever constructed, 67 feet long, 26 feet deep, and 16 feet high (22 by 9 by 5 m). During this project, the organ's current console was constructed in Wanamaker's private in-house pipe-organ factory, with six [manuals](/wiki/Manual_\(music\) "Manual (music)") and several hundred controls. By 1930, when work on expanding the organ finally stopped, the organ had 28,482 pipes, and, if Rodman Wanamaker had not died in 1928, the organ would probably be even bigger.[\[9\]](#cite_note-whitney-9)

Plans were made for, among others, a [Stentor](/wiki/Stentor_\(disambiguation\) "Stentor (disambiguation)") division, a section of high-pressure [diapasons](/wiki/Diapason_\(pipe_organ\) "Diapason (pipe organ)") and [reeds](/wiki/Reed_pipe "Reed pipe"). It was to be installed on the fifth floor, above the String Division, and would be playable from the sixth manual. However, it was never funded, and the sixth manual is now used to couple other divisions or play various solo voices from other divisions that are duplexed to this keyboard.[\[10\]](#cite_note-10)

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/ConsoleOrgueWanamaker.jpg/500px-ConsoleOrgueWanamaker.jpg)](/wiki/File:ConsoleOrgueWanamaker.jpg)

The organ's six-[manual](/wiki/Manual_\(music\) "Manual (music)") console

[Rodman Wanamaker](/wiki/Rodman_Wanamaker "Rodman Wanamaker") was not interested in mere size, however, but in artistic organ-building with finely crafted pipes and chests using the best materials and careful artistic consideration. The Wanamaker Organ console, built in the store organ shop by William Boone Fleming, is a work of art in its own right with heavy, durable construction, an ingenious layout of its pneumatic stop action and many unique features and conveniences. Wanamaker also had a collection of 60 rare [stringed instruments](/wiki/String_instrument "String instrument"), the Wanamaker Cappella, that were used in conjunction with the store organs in Philadelphia and [New York](/wiki/New_York_City "New York City"), and went on tour. They were dispersed after his death.

Following the sale of the store to [The May Department Stores Company](/wiki/The_May_Department_Stores_Company "The May Department Stores Company"), in 1995, the Wanamaker's name was removed from the store (first as Wanamaker-Hecht's) in favor of [Hecht's](/wiki/Hecht%27s "Hecht's"), but the organ and its concerts were retained. During the local renaming of the Hecht's stores to [Strawbridge's](/wiki/Strawbridge%27s "Strawbridge's"), the historic Wanamaker Store briefly took the name of its longtime rival Strawbridge's. The May Company began a complete restoration of the organ in 1997, as part of the store's final May Co. conversion into a [Lord & Taylor](/wiki/Lord_%26_Taylor "Lord & Taylor"). At that time the store area was reduced to three floors and additional panes of glass were put around the Grand Court on floors four and five, greatly enhancing the reverberation of the room.

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Wanamakers_Organ_at_Macys_Philadelphia_in_2023.jpg/250px-Wanamakers_Organ_at_Macys_Philadelphia_in_2023.jpg)](/wiki/File:Wanamakers_Organ_at_Macys_Philadelphia_in_2023.jpg)

The Wanamaker Organ in the Grand Court

The [Philadelphia Orchestra](/wiki/Philadelphia_Orchestra "Philadelphia Orchestra") returned to the Grand Court on September 27, 2008, for the premiere performance of [Joseph Jongen](/wiki/Joseph_Jongen "Joseph Jongen")'s _Symphonie Concertante_ (1926) on the organ for which it was written. The ticketed event, featuring soloist Peter Richard Conte, also included the [Bach](/wiki/Bach "Bach")/[Stokowski](/wiki/Leopold_Stokowski "Leopold Stokowski") arrangement of the [Toccata and Fugue in D minor](/wiki/Toccata_and_Fugue_in_D_minor "Toccata and Fugue in D minor"), [Marcel Dupré](/wiki/Marcel_Dupr%C3%A9 "Marcel Dupré")'s _Cortege and Litany_ for Organ and Orchestra, and the world premiere of a _Fanfare_ by [Howard Shore](/wiki/Howard_Shore "Howard Shore"), composer for [_The Lord of the Rings_ films](/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_\(film_series\) "The Lord of the Rings (film series)"). Shore visited the store in May 2008 to meet with Peter Richard Conte and hear the Wanamaker Organ. The Philadelphia Orchestra Concert was co-sponsored by the Friends of the Wanamaker Organ and was a benefit for that organization.[\[11\]](#cite_note-11)

In 2019 the Wanamaker Organ facade, designed by [Daniel Hudson Burnham](/wiki/Daniel_Hudson_Burnham "Daniel Hudson Burnham"), was restored and re-gilded in 22-karat gold to a color scheme close in sympathy to its original appearance but which fits in with its new surroundings. Evergreene Architectural Arts did the work. Grant money from Macy's and several Philadelphia area charities funded this project, which was overseen by the Friends of the Wanamaker Organ.

In January 2025, Macy's announced the closing of 66 stores by March 2025[\[12\]](#cite_note-12) which included the Center City location where the organ is located.[\[13\]](#cite_note-13) The organ is protected as a [National Historic Landmark](/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark "National Historic Landmark") and cannot be taken down or moved.[\[14\]](#cite_note-14)[\[15\]](#cite_note-15) The new owner of the building, TF Cornerstone, has stated that it is “committed to the preservation of the organ and ensuring it remains a cherished part of the space.”[\[16\]](#cite_note-16) As of October 2025, the organ is performed in occasional concerts open to the public.[\[17\]](#cite_note-17)

### Organists

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Wanamaker_Organ&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Organists")\]

Although numerous famous organists have played special concerts on the organ, it has had only four chief organists in its history:

*   Dr. Irvin J. Morgan (1911–1917)
*   Mary E. Vogt (1917–1966)[\[8\]](#cite_note-bcpp-8)
*   [Dr. Keith Chapman](/wiki/Keith_Chapman_\(organist\) "Keith Chapman (organist)") (1966–1989)
*   Peter Richard Conte (1989–present)

For about a decade beginning in 1919, Dr. [Charles M. Courboin](/wiki/Charles_M._Courboin "Charles M. Courboin") was the organist for a series of special evening concerts, including several collaborations with the [Philadelphia Orchestra](/wiki/Philadelphia_Orchestra "Philadelphia Orchestra"). Courboin also headed the Wanamaker Organ Shop in the late 1920s.

## Architectural layout

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Wanamaker_Organ&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Architectural layout")\]

The pipes are laid out across the space occupied by five floors of the building, with the sections situated as follows:

*   2nd floor south – Main Pedal 32 ft, Lower Swell, Great, Percussions
*   3rd floor south – Main Pedal, Chorus, Upper Swell, Choir/Enclosed Great, Solo, Vox Humana Chorus
*   4th floor south – String
*   4th floor west – Orchestral (adjacent to String)
*   7th floor south – Major Chimes, Ethereal, Chinese Gong
*   7th floor north – Echo

The 32 ft Wood Open, 32 ft Diaphone, and 32 ft Metal Diapason pipes run the length of a little more than 2 stories, beginning on the second floor.[\[5\]](#cite_note-biswanger-5)

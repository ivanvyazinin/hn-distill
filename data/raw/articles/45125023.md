Our brains dic­tate our every move.

They’re the ones who spur us to study hard, so we can make some­thing of our­selves, in order to bet­ter our com­mu­ni­ties.

They name our babies, choose our clothes, decide what we’re hun­gry for.

They make and break laws, orga­nize protests, frit­ter away hours on social media, and give us the green light to binge watch a bunch of dumb shows when we could be read­ing [_War and Peace_](http://www.gutenberg.org/files/2600/2600-h/2600-h.htm).

They also plant the seeds for [_Fitz­car­ral­do_](https://www.rogerebert.com/reviews/great-movie-fitzcarraldo-1982)\-like cre­ative endeav­ors that take over our lives and gen­er­ate lit­tle to no income.

We may describe such endeav­ors as a labor of love, into which we’ve poured our entire heart and soul, but think for a sec­ond.

Who’s _real­ly_ respon­si­ble here?

The heart, that mus­cu­lar fist-sized Valen­tine, con­tent to just pump-pump-pump its way through life, lub-dub, lub-dub, from cra­dle to grave?

Or the brain, a crafty Iago of an organ, pos­ses­sor of bil­lions of neu­rons, com­plex, con­tra­dic­to­ry, [a mys­tery we’re far from unrav­el­ing?](https://science.howstuffworks.com/life/inside-the-mind/human-brain/5-brain-mysteries.htm)

Psy­chi­a­trist [Dr. Karen Nor­berg](https://www.nber.org/people/karen_norberg)’s brain has steered her to study such heavy duty sub­jects as [the day­care effect](https://www.nber.org/papers/w6769.pdf), [the rise in youth sui­cide](https://www.nber.org/chapters/c10690), and the [risk of pre­scrib­ing selec­tive sero­tonin reup­take inhibitors as a treat­ment for depres­sion](https://www.nber.org/papers/w12906.pdf).

![](https://www.openculture.com/wp-content/plugins/native-lazyload/assets/images/placeholder.svg)

![](https://cdn8.openculture.com/2019/03/28202811/KarenNorberg_02.jpg)

On a lighter note, it also told her to devote nine months to knit­ting an anatom­i­cal­ly cor­rect repli­ca of the human brain.

(Twelve, if you count three months of research before cast­ing on.)

How did her brain con­vince her to embark on this mad­cap assign­ment?

Easy. It arranged for her to be in the mid­dle of a more pro­sa­ic knit­ting project, then goosed her into notic­ing how the ruf­fles of that project resem­bled the wrin­kles of the cere­bral cor­tex.

Coin­ci­dence?

Not like­ly. Espe­cial­ly when one of the cere­bral cor­tex’s most impor­tant duties is deci­sion mak­ing.

As she explained in an inter­view with [_The Tele­graph_](https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/newstopics/howaboutthat/4245919/Psychiatrist-knits-anatomically-correct-woolly-brain.html?awc=15609_1553703284_e92124e118b2ff32664ca1e196411881&WT.mc_id=tmgoff_poth-inbaff_tra_hotels&utm_source=227305&utm_medium=AWIN&utm_content=tra_hotels&utm_campaign=www.thisiscolossal.com), brain devel­op­ment is not unlike the growth of a knit­ted piece:

> _You can see very nat­u­ral­ly how the ‘rip­pling’ effect of the cere­bral cor­tex emerges from prop­er­ties that prob­a­bly have to do with nerve cell growth. In the case of knit­ting, the effect is cre­at­ed by_ [_increas­ing the num­ber of stitch­es_](https://www.instructables.com/id/Knitting-Lessons-Increasing-and-Decreasing-Stitch/) _in each row._

![](https://www.openculture.com/wp-content/plugins/native-lazyload/assets/images/placeholder.svg)

![](https://cdn8.openculture.com/2019/03/28202958/norberg_brain_1239528c.jpg)

Dr. Norberg—who, yes, has on occa­sion referred to her project as a labor of love—told [_Sci­en­tif­ic Amer­i­can_](https://blogs.scientificamerican.com/news-blog/abby-normal-nope-psychiatrist-knits-2009-01-16/) that such a mas­sive crafty under­tak­ing appealed to her sense of humor because “it seemed so ridicu­lous and would be an enor­mous­ly com­pli­cat­ed, absurd­ly ambi­tious thing to do.”

That’s the point at which many people’s brains would give them per­mis­sion to stop, but Dr. Nor­berg and her brain per­sist­ed, push­ing past the hypo­thet­i­cal, cre­at­ing col­or­ful indi­vid­ual struc­tures that were even­tu­al­ly sewn into two cud­dly hemi­spheres that can be joined with a zip­per.

(She also let slip that her brain—by which she means the knit­ted one, though the obser­va­tion cer­tain­ly holds true for the one in her head—is female, due to its robust [cor­pus cal­lo­sum](https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/318065.php), the “tough body” whose mil­lions of fibers pro­mote com­mu­ni­ca­tion and con­nec­tion.)

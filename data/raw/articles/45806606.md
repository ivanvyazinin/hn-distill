**Lee Felsenstein** (born April 27, 1945) is an American [computer engineer](/wiki/Computer_engineer "Computer engineer") who played a central role in the development of [personal computers](/wiki/Personal_computer "Personal computer"). He was one of the original members of the [Homebrew Computer Club](/wiki/Homebrew_Computer_Club "Homebrew Computer Club") and the designer of the [Osborne 1](/wiki/Osborne_1 "Osborne 1"), the first mass-produced [portable computer](/wiki/Portable_computer "Portable computer").

Before the Osborne, Felsenstein designed the [Intel 8080](/wiki/Intel_8080 "Intel 8080") based [Sol-20](/wiki/Sol-20 "Sol-20")[\[1\]](#cite_note-sol-1) computer from [Processor Technology](/wiki/Processor_Technology "Processor Technology"), the [PennyWhistle](/wiki/Pennywhistle_modem "Pennywhistle modem")[\[2\]](#cite_note-penny-2)[\[3\]](#cite_note-ADM-3A-3) [modem](/wiki/Modem "Modem"), and other early "[S-100 bus](/wiki/S-100_bus "S-100 bus")" era designs. His shared-memory alphanumeric video display design, the [Processor Technology](/wiki/Processor_Technology "Processor Technology") [VDM-1](/wiki/VDM-1 "VDM-1") video display module board, was widely copied and became the basis for the standard display architecture of personal computers.

Many of his designs were leaders in reducing costs of computer technologies for the purpose of making them available to large markets. His work featured a concern for the social impact of technology and was influenced by the philosophy of [Ivan Illich](/wiki/Ivan_Illich "Ivan Illich"). Felsenstein was the engineer for the [Community Memory](/wiki/Community_Memory "Community Memory") project, one of the earliest attempts to place networked computer terminals in public places to facilitate social interactions among individuals, in the era before the commercial Internet.

## Life

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Lee_Felsenstein&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Life")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Processor_Technology_SOL_20_Computer.jpg/250px-Processor_Technology_SOL_20_Computer.jpg)](/wiki/File:Processor_Technology_SOL_20_Computer.jpg)

Processor Technology [Sol-20](/wiki/Sol-20 "Sol-20") computer designed by Lee Felsenstein

Felsenstein graduated from [Central High School](/wiki/Central_High_School_\(Philadelphia\) "Central High School (Philadelphia)") in [Philadelphia](/wiki/Philadelphia "Philadelphia") as a member of class 219.[\[4\]](#cite_note-4) As a young man, Felsenstein was a [New Left](/wiki/New_Left "New Left") radical. From October through December 1964, he was a participant in the [Free Speech Movement](/wiki/Free_Speech_Movement "Free Speech Movement") and was one of 768 arrested in the climactic "Sproul Hall Sit-In" of December 2–3, 1964. He also wrote for the _[Berkeley Barb](/wiki/Berkeley_Barb "Berkeley Barb")_, one of the leading [underground newspapers](/wiki/Underground_newspaper "Underground newspaper").

He had entered [University of California, Berkeley](/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley "University of California, Berkeley") first in 1963, joined the Co-operative Work-Study Program in Engineering in 1964 and dropped out at the end of 1967, working as a Junior Engineer at the [Ampex](/wiki/Ampex "Ampex") Corporation from 1968 through 1971, when he re-enrolled at Berkeley. He received a [B.S.](/wiki/B.S. "B.S.") in [electrical engineering and computer science](/wiki/Electrical_engineering_and_computer_science "Electrical engineering and computer science") from the University of California, Berkeley in 1972.

From 1981–1983, Felsenstein was employed at the [Osborne Computer Corporation](/wiki/Osborne_Computer_Corporation "Osborne Computer Corporation"). At Osborne, he was the designer of the [Osborne 1](/wiki/Osborne_1 "Osborne 1"), the first mass-produced portable computer. He then returned to freelance consulting. In 1992, he joined [Interval Research Corporation](/wiki/Interval_Research_Corporation "Interval Research Corporation"), where he worked until 2000. From then until 2005, he worked for Pemstar Pacific Consultants, an electronics design and [contract manufacturing](/wiki/Contract_manufacturing "Contract manufacturing") firm, which was subsequently acquired by [Benchmark Electronics](/wiki/Benchmark_Electronics "Benchmark Electronics"). Throughout, he acted as an occasional free-lance consulting designer or worked at his own design firm.

Many of his designs were leaders in reducing the costs of computer technologies for the purpose of making them available to large markets. His work featured a concern for the social impact of technology. The Community Memory project, begun as a project of [Resource One, Inc.](/wiki/Project_One_\(San_Francisco\) "Project One (San Francisco)") in 1972 and later incorporated in 1977 by Felsenstein with Efrem Lipkin, Ken Colstad, [Jude Milhon](/wiki/Jude_Milhon "Jude Milhon"), and Mark Szpakowski, was one of the earliest attempts to place networked computer terminals in such places as Berkeley supermarkets to attract casual use by persons from all walks of life passing through and facilitate social interactions among non-technical individuals, in the era before the Internet.[\[5\]](#cite_note-5)

Felsenstein was influenced in his philosophy by the works of [Ivan Illich](/wiki/Ivan_Illich "Ivan Illich"), particularly _[Tools for Conviviality](/wiki/Tools_for_Conviviality "Tools for Conviviality")_ (Harper and Row, 1973). This book advocated a "convivial" approach to design which allowed users of technologies to learn about the technology by encouraging exploration, tinkering, and modification. Felsenstein had learned about electronics in much the same fashion, and summarized his conclusions in several aphorisms, to wit – "In order to survive in a public-access environment, a computer must grow a computer club around itself." Others were – "To change the rules, change the tools," and "If work is to become play, then tools must become toys."

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Lee_Felsenstein.04.jpg/250px-Lee_Felsenstein.04.jpg)](/wiki/File:Lee_Felsenstein.04.jpg)

Felsenstein at the [Vintage Computer Festival](/wiki/Vintage_Computer_Festival "Vintage Computer Festival"), 2005

Felsenstein was one of the original members of the [Homebrew Computer Club](/wiki/Homebrew_Computer_Club "Homebrew Computer Club"), which formed in 1975 in response to the appearance of the [Altair 8800](/wiki/Altair_8800 "Altair 8800") computer kit. With a handy [yardstick](/wiki/Yardstick "Yardstick"), Felsenstein "moderated" meetings at the [SLAC](/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory "SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory") Auditorium. He was less a chair than a keeper of chaos. In this heyday of the development of the first personal computers, Felsenstein designed the Intel 8080 based [Sol-20](/wiki/Sol-20 "Sol-20")[\[1\]](#cite_note-sol-1) computer from Processor Technology, the [Micro Expander](/wiki/Micro_Expander "Micro Expander") computer,[\[6\]](#cite_note-6)[\[7\]](#cite_note-7) the [Pennywhistle modem](/wiki/Pennywhistle_modem "Pennywhistle modem"),[\[2\]](#cite_note-penny-2) and other early "S-100 bus" era designs. These existed in a market space with early generation hobbyist microcomputers from [Altair](/wiki/Altair_8800 "Altair 8800"), [IMSAI](/wiki/IMSAI "IMSAI"), [Morrow Designs](/wiki/George_Morrow_\(computers\) "George Morrow (computers)"), [Cromemco](/wiki/Cromemco "Cromemco"), and other vendors. Felsenstein's shared-memory alphanumeric video display design, the Processor Technology VDM-1 video display module board, was widely copied and became the basis for the standard display architecture of personal computers.

Felsenstein was named a "Pioneer of the Electronic Frontier" in 1994 by the [Electronic Frontier Foundation](/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation "Electronic Frontier Foundation"), and in 2007, he was given the Editor's Choice Award for Creative Excellence by [EE Times](/wiki/EE_Times "EE Times") magazine. In 1998, Felsenstein founded the [Free Speech Movement Archives](http://www.fsm-a.org/) as an online repository of historical information relating to that event, its antecedents and successors.

In 2003, while working with the [Jhai Foundation](/wiki/Jhai_Foundation "Jhai Foundation") of [San Francisco](/wiki/San_Francisco "San Francisco"), he designed an open-source telecommunications and computer system for installation in remote villages in the developing world. This system was dubbed "the Pedal-Powered Internet" by _[The New York Times Magazine](/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine "The New York Times Magazine")_ due to its reliance on pedal power generation. Installation of the first system in Laos was unsuccessful, but the design has been tested on an Indian reservation in the US and continues in development in India. In 2003, Felsenstein was named a Laureate of [The Tech Museum of Innovation](/wiki/The_Tech_Museum_of_Innovation "The Tech Museum of Innovation") ([San Jose, California](/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California")) for this work.

Felsenstein is the Founding Sensei of the [Hacker Dojo](/wiki/Hacker_Dojo "Hacker Dojo") in Mountain View, California, and was featured on a Fox News segment in late 2009 covering the non-profit facility.[\[8\]](#cite_note-8)

Felsenstein's older brother is the evolutionary biologist [Joseph Felsenstein](/wiki/Joseph_Felsenstein "Joseph Felsenstein"), a [National Academy of Sciences](/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences "National Academy of Sciences") member whose [PHYLIP](/wiki/PHYLIP "PHYLIP") system was one of the earliest examples of [bioinformatics](/wiki/Bioinformatics "Bioinformatics").[\[9\]](#cite_note-9) Early versions of PHYLIP were developed on the Sol-20 and Osborne 1,[\[10\]](#cite_note-10) computers designed by Felsenstein.

On April 16, 2016, Felsenstein was made a Fellow of the [Computer History Museum](/wiki/Computer_History_Museum "Computer History Museum"), "for his influence on the technical and social environment of the early personal computing era."

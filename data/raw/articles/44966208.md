# Game Math: Precise Control over Numeric Springing

Posted on [April 8, 2015](https://allenchou.net/2015/04/game-math-precise-control-over-numeric-springing/ "15:43") by [Allen Chou](https://allenchou.net/author/cjcat2266/ "View all posts by Allen Chou")

This post is part of my [Game Math Series](http://allenchou.net/game-math-series/).

Source files are on [GitHub](https://github.com/TheAllenChou/numeric-springing)

Check out [this post](http://allenchou.net/2015/04/game-math-numeric-springing-examples/) if you want to see more visual examples of numeric springing.

Numeric springing is a very powerful tool for procedural animation. You specify the initial value, initial velocity, target value, and some spring-related parameters; the result is a smooth springing effect. You can apply this technique to all sorts of numeric properties, some common ones being position, rotation, and scale of an object.

![spring](http://allenchou.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/spring.gif)

### The Common But Designer-Unfriendly Way

Here is a common, yet designer-unfriendly, implementation of numeric springing. Let ![x_i](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c8700e0258243116de0d4f288e2e3b44_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![v_i](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-43aad44054410fd6d3b89453d95c1502_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") denote the numeric value and velocity, respectively, at frame ![i](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-695d9d59bd04859c6c99e7feb11daab6_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"); and let ![k](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), ![d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4e8716946f6a868f015e0d62f28bc540_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), ![h](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-14b463d0ecd5b350ced6cf1d6a12eef3_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), ![x_t](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-e95410b7a7a6a79cbcbdc56ca038a68e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), denote the spring stiffness, damping factor, time step between frames, and target value, respectively.

    ![ \begin{flalign*}   v_{i+1} &= d v_i + h k (x_t - x_i) \\   x_{i+1} &= x_i + h v_{i+1} \\ \end{flalign*} ](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2f80be10bff9f675b24b96e9bb6a0464_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

Let’s dissect this implementation.

The difference between the target value and the current value ![(x_t - x_i)](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-cc19d1315430001ad249618a844b0ae8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") contributes to the velocity change after multiplied by the spring stiffness ![k](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"). The larger the difference and spring stiffness, the larger the change in velocity.

The new velocity ![v_{i+1}](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-451082126d2af4098e56e53ba19c3d79_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") inherits the value from the old velocity ![v_i](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-43aad44054410fd6d3b89453d95c1502_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") scaled by the damping factor ![d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4e8716946f6a868f015e0d62f28bc540_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), which should be a value between zero and one. If the damping factor is equal to one, then no scaling happens, and the new velocity completely inherits the value from the old velocity. If the damping factor is a fraction of one, then the magnitude of oscillation will decrease gradually.

That seems all reasonable and good, but just **what exactly** are the values for ![k](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4e8716946f6a868f015e0d62f28bc540_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") do we need to achieve a specific springing effect, say an oscillation at 5Hz frequency and the oscillation magnitude decreases by 90% every one second? Many people just decide to bite the bullet and spend a large amount of time hand-tweaking the values for ![k](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-3422b6bb5c160593658b7c39425d9880_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4e8716946f6a868f015e0d62f28bc540_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), until the result is acceptably close to the desired effect.

### We Can Be Exact

Now let’s take a look at the differential equation for a damped spring system centered around ![x = x_t](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-979c5f6aa0897c1e2f1d8bc31d10bc57_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com").

    ![\[ \frac{\mathrm{d}^2 x}{\mathrm{d} t^2}} + 2 \zeta \omega \frac{\mathrm{d} x}{\mathrm{d} t}} + \omega^2 (x - x_t) = 0 \]](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-718f51c540bea215dcd95035ab866442_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

I won’t go into details as to how this equation is derived. All you really need to know is that ![\omega](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-707fcec15e450815730425a6607a1858_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") (omega) and ![\zeta](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-972238cb0edae352c280a54fc315146f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") (zeta) govern how the system behaves and what they mean.

![\omega](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-707fcec15e450815730425a6607a1858_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is the **angular frequency** of the oscillation. An angular frequency of ![2\pi](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-5bfa2124624f767670227d1aeab8d85c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") (radians per second) means the oscillation completes one full period over one second, i.e. 1Hz.

![\zeta](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-972238cb0edae352c280a54fc315146f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is the **damping ratio**. A damping ratio of zero means there is no damping at all, and the oscillation just continues indefinitely. A damping ratio between zero and one means the spring system is **underdamped**; oscillation happens, and the magnitude of oscillation decreases exponentially over time. A damping ratio of 1 signifies a **critically damped** system, where this is the point the system stops showing oscillation, but only converging to the target value exponentially. Any damping ratio above 1 means the system is **overdamped**, and the effect of springing becomes more draggy as the damping ratio increases.

Here’s a figure I borrowed form [Erin Catto](https://twitter.com/erin_catto)‘s [presentation](http://box2d.org/files/GDC2011/GDC2011_Catto_Erin_Soft_Constraints.pdf) on soft constraints, to show you the comparison of undamped, underdamped, critically damped, and overdamped systems.

![damping ratios](http://allenchou.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/damping-ratios.png)

Below are the equations for simulating a damped spring system using the [implicit Euler method](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backward_Euler_method).

    ![ \begin{flalign*}   x_{i+1} &= x_i + h v_{i+1} \\   v_{i+1} &= v_i - h (2 \zeta \omega v_{i+1}) - h \omega^2 (x_{i + 1} - x_t) \\ \end{flalign*} ](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-8913b7edf071fe82f5f616e3bf20b28e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

Solving ![x_{i+1}](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-201e3cc6bc59b4d228522876cf9feb4f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![v_{i+1}](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-451082126d2af4098e56e53ba19c3d79_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") in terms of ![x_i](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-c8700e0258243116de0d4f288e2e3b44_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![v_i](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-43aad44054410fd6d3b89453d95c1502_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") using [Cramer’s rule](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cramer%27s_rule), we get:

    ![ \begin{flalign*}   x_{i+1} &= \frac{\Delta_x}{\Delta} \\   v_{i+1} &= \frac{\Delta_v}{\Delta},  \\ \end{flalign*} ](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-991fb113dbeba2d8437b14771bf7dac2_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

where:

    ![ \begin{flalign*}   \Delta &= (1 + 2 h \zeta \omega) + h^2 \omega^2 \\   \Delta_x &= (1 + 2 h \zeta \omega) x_i + h v_i + h^2 \omega^2 x_t \\   \Delta_v &= v_i + h \omega^2 (x_t - x_i) \\ \end{flalign*} ](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4633785db54134b408a1d2242391a936_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

Below is a sample implementation in C++. The variables `x` and `v` are initialized once and then passed into the function by reference every frame, where the function keeps updating their values every time it’s called.

/\*
  x     - value             (input/output)
  v     - velocity          (input/output)
  xt    - target value      (input)
  zeta  - damping ratio     (input)
  omega - angular frequency (input)
  h     - time step         (input)
\*/
void Spring
(
  float &x, float &v, float xt, 
  float zeta, float omega, float h
)
{
  const float f = 1.0f + 2.0f \* h \* zeta \* omega;
  const float oo = omega \* omega;
  const float hoo = h \* oo;
  const float hhoo = h \* hoo;
  const float detInv = 1.0f / (f + hhoo);
  const float detX = f \* x + h \* v + hhoo \* xt;
  const float detV = v + hoo \* (xt - x);
  x = detX \* detInv;
  v = detV \* detInv;
}

### Designer-Friendly Parameters

Now we have our formula and implementation all worked out, what parameters should we expose to the designers from our spring system so that they can easily tweak the system?

Inspired by the aforementioned presentation by Erin Catto, I propose exposing the **oscillation frequency** ![f](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9c09a708375fde2676da319bcdfe8b24_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") in Hz, and the **fraction of oscillation magnitude reduced** ![p_d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ad9f0d65bc1036de8b8f5d755f5bef6a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") over a **specific duration** ![t_d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2e5a1860ccae2354a0f48224f9964de1_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") due to damping.

Mapping ![f](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-9c09a708375fde2676da319bcdfe8b24_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") to ![\omega](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-707fcec15e450815730425a6607a1858_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is easy, you just multiply it by ![2 \pi](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-0d8fdba56b2482224cbd44d3cc176190_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"):

    ![\[ \omega = 2 \pi f \]](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-109c8a04a391e93cb704b748548102f8_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

Mapping ![p_d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ad9f0d65bc1036de8b8f5d755f5bef6a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![t_d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2e5a1860ccae2354a0f48224f9964de1_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") to ![\zeta](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-972238cb0edae352c280a54fc315146f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") is a little bit more tricky.

First you need to understand that the oscillation magnitude decreases exponentially with this curve:

    ![\[ y(t) = e^{-\zeta \omega t} \]](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-05d2c7c2c4aeb9d8f87a5ebef5513f1b_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

After plugging in ![p_d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-ad9f0d65bc1036de8b8f5d755f5bef6a_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and ![t_d](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-2e5a1860ccae2354a0f48224f9964de1_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"), we get:

    ![\[ y(t_d) = e^{-\zeta \omega t_d} = p_d \]](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-1c00cce65cd4a886bee2b415e7e36a80_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

So we can solve for ![\zeta](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-972238cb0edae352c280a54fc315146f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com"):

    ![\[ \zeta = \frac{\ln (p_d)}{-\omega t_d} \]](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-37e5d1175bdf64b450c5d8d64a82ceb3_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

For example, if we want the oscillation to decrease by 90% (![p_d = 0.1](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-d2e9de33e5cec5f82224694a8a80cb68_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")) every 0.5 second (![t_d = 0.5](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a7a15e4d3cc31799eacdb8625308919c_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")) with oscillation frequency 2Hz (![\omega = 4 \pi](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-a45ca84a336b6695a3f94af6d84df2bc_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")), then we get:

    ![\[ \zeta = \frac{\ln (0.1)}{-4 \pi \cdot 0.5} = 0.367 \]](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-4b85a9139c0b17f57fd3c0ea613bdfef_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com")

### Conclusion

Numeric springing is a very powerful tool for procedural animation, and now you know how to precisely control it. Given the desired oscillation frequency and percentage of oscillation magnitude reduction over a specific duration, you can now compute the exact angular frequency ![\omega](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-707fcec15e450815730425a6607a1858_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") and damping ratio ![\zeta](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-972238cb0edae352c280a54fc315146f_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") necessary to create the desired springing effect.

By the way, let’s look at the animated example shown above again:

![spring](http://allenchou.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/spring.gif)

This animation was created with ![\omega = 8 \pi](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-bee833bcdd78eb8e80005020f42c7cae_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") (4 full oscillation periods per second) and ![\zeta = 0.23](https://allenchou.net/wp-content/ql-cache/quicklatex.com-344ec069466ebffd5962c2710c39562e_l3.png "Rendered by QuickLaTeX.com") (oscillation magnitude decreases by 99.7% every second).

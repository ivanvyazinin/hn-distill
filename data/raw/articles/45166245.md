From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Four data sets with the same descriptive statistics, yet very different distributions

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Anscombe%27s_quartet_3.svg/500px-Anscombe%27s_quartet_3.svg.png)](/wiki/File:Anscombe%27s_quartet_3.svg)

The four [datasets](/wiki/Dataset "Dataset") composing Anscombe's quartet. All four sets have identical statistical parameters, but the graphs show them to be considerably different

**Anscombe's quartet** comprises four [datasets](/wiki/Dataset "Dataset") that have nearly identical simple [descriptive statistics](/wiki/Descriptive_statistics "Descriptive statistics"), yet have very different [distributions](/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution") and appear very different when [graphed](/wiki/Plot_\(graphics\) "Plot (graphics)"). Each dataset consists of eleven [(_x_, _y_) points](/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"). They were constructed in 1973 by the [statistician](/wiki/Statistician "Statistician") [Francis Anscombe](/wiki/Francis_Anscombe "Francis Anscombe") to demonstrate both the importance of graphing data when analyzing it, and the effect of [outliers](/wiki/Outlier "Outlier") and other [influential observations](/wiki/Influential_observations "Influential observations") on statistical properties. He described the article as being intended to counter the impression among statisticians that "numerical calculations are exact, but graphs are rough".[\[1\]](#cite_note-Anscombe-1)

*   The first [scatter plot](/wiki/Scatter_plot "Scatter plot") (top left) appears to be a simple [linear relationship](/wiki/Linear_relationship "Linear relationship"), corresponding to two correlated [variables](/wiki/Variable_\(mathematics\) "Variable (mathematics)"), where _y_ could be modelled as [gaussian](/wiki/Normal_distribution "Normal distribution") with mean linearly dependent on _x_.
*   For the second graph (top right), while a relationship between the two variables is obvious, it is not linear, and the [Pearson correlation coefficient](/wiki/Pearson_correlation_coefficient "Pearson correlation coefficient") is not relevant. A more general regression and the corresponding [coefficient of determination](/wiki/Coefficient_of_determination "Coefficient of determination") would be more appropriate.
*   In the third graph (bottom left), the modelled relationship is linear, but should have a different [regression line](/wiki/Regression_line "Regression line") (a [robust regression](/wiki/Robust_regression "Robust regression") would have been called for). The calculated regression is offset by the one [outlier](/wiki/Outlier "Outlier"), which exerts enough influence to lower the correlation coefficient from 1 to 0.816.
*   Finally, the fourth graph (bottom right) shows an example when one [high-leverage point](/wiki/High-leverage_point "High-leverage point") is enough to produce a high correlation coefficient, even though the other data points do not indicate any relationship between the variables.

The quartet is still often used to illustrate the importance of looking at a set of data graphically before starting to analyze according to a particular type of relationship, and the inadequacy of basic statistic properties for describing realistic datasets.[\[2\]](#cite_note-2)[\[3\]](#cite_note-3)[\[4\]](#cite_note-4)[\[5\]](#cite_note-5)[\[6\]](#cite_note-6)

It is not known how Anscombe created his datasets.[\[7\]](#cite_note-ChatterjeeFirat-7) Since its publication, several methods to generate similar datasets with identical statistics and dissimilar graphics have been developed.[\[7\]](#cite_note-ChatterjeeFirat-7)[\[8\]](#cite_note-8) One of these, the _[Datasaurus dozen](/wiki/Datasaurus_dozen "Datasaurus dozen")_, consists of points tracing out the outline of a dinosaur, plus twelve other datasets that have the same summary statistics.[\[9\]](#cite_note-9)[\[10\]](#cite_note-10)[\[11\]](#cite_note-11)

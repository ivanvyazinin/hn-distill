# Some thoughts on LLMs and Software Development

Martin Fowler: 28 Aug 2025

IвҖҷm about to head away from looking after this site for a few weeks (part vacation, part work stuff). As I contemplate some weeks away from the daily routine, I feel an urge to share some scattered thoughts about the state of LLMs and AI.

В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„

IвҖҷve seen a few early surveys on the effect AI is having on software development, is it really speeding folks up, does it improve or wreck code quality? One of the big problems with these surveys is that they arenвҖҷt taking into account _how_ people are using the LLMs. From what I can tell the vast majority of LLM usage is fancy auto-complete, often using co-pilot. But those I know who get the most value from LLMs reckon that auto-complete isnвҖҷt very useful, preferring approaches that allow the LLM to directly read and edit source code files to carry out tasks. My concern is that surveys that ignore the different work-flows of using LLMs will produce data thatвҖҷs going to send people down the wrong paths.

(Another complication is the varying capabilities of different models.)

В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„

IвҖҷm often asked, вҖңwhat is the future of programming?вҖқ Should people consider entering software development now? Will LLMs eliminate the need for junior engineers? Should senior engineers get out of the profession before itвҖҷs too late? My answer to all these questions is вҖңI havenвҖҷt the foggiestвҖқ. Furthermore I think anyone who says they know what this future will be is talking from an inappropriate orifice. We are still figuring out how to use LLMs, and it will be some time before we have a decent idea of how to use them well, especially if they gain significant improvements.

What I suggest, is that people experiment with them. At the least, read about what others are doing, but pay attention to the details of their workflows. Preferably experiment yourself, and do share your experiences.

В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқҮВ В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„

IвҖҷm also asked: вҖңis AI a bubbleвҖқ? To which my answer is вҖңOF COURSE ITвҖҷS A BUBBLEвҖқ. All major technological advances have come with economic bubbles, from canals and railroads to the internet. We know with near 100% certainty that this bubble will pop, causing lots of investments to fizzle to nothing. However what we donвҖҷt know is _when_ it will pop, and thus how big the bubble will have grown, generating some real value in the process, before that happens. It could pop next month, or not for a couple of years.

We also know that when the bubble pops, many firms will go bust, but not all. When the dot-com bubble burst, it killed pets.com, it killed WebvanвҖҰ but it did not kill Amazon.

В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„

I [retired from public speaking](https://martinfowler.com/articles/202106-reducing-speaking.html) a couple of years ago. But while I donвҖҷt miss the stress of giving talks, I do miss hanging out with my friends in the industry. So IвҖҷm looking forward to catching up with many of them at [GOTO Copenhagen](https://gotocph.com/2025). IвҖҷve been involved with the GOTO conference series since the 1990s (when it was called JAOO), and continue to be impressed with how they put together a fascinating program.

В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вңўВ В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„

My former colleague Rebecca Parsons, has been saying for a long time that hallucinations arenвҖҷt a bug of LLMs, they are a feature. Indeed they are _the_ feature. All an LLM does is produce hallucinations, itвҖҷs just that we find some of them useful.

One of the consequences of this is that we should always consider asking the LLM the same question more than once, perhaps with some variation in the wording. Then we can compare answers, indeed perhaps ask the LLM to compare answers for us. The difference in the answers can be as useful as the answers themselves.

Certainly if we ever ask a hallucination engine for a numeric answer, we should ask it at least three times, so we get some sense of the variation. Furthermore we shouldnвҖҷt ask an LLM to calculate an answer than we can calculate deterministically (yes, IвҖҷve seen this). It is OK to ask an LLM to generate code to calculate an answer (but still do it more than once).

В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„

Other forms of engineering have to take into account the variability of the world. A structural engineer builds in tolerance for all the factors she canвҖҷt measure. (I remember being told early in my career that the unique characteristic of digital electronics was that there was no concept of tolerances.) Process engineers consider that humans are executing tasks, and will sometimes be forgetful or careless. Software Engineering is unusual in that it works with deterministic machines. Maybe LLMs mark the point where we join our engineering peers in a world on non-determinism.

В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„

IвҖҷve often heard, with decent reason, an LLM compared to a junior colleague. But I find LLMs are quite happy to say вҖңall tests greenвҖқ, yet when I run them, there are failures. If that was a junior engineerвҖҷs behavior, how long would it be before H.R. was involved?

В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В В вқ„

LLMs create a huge increase in the attack surface of software systems. Simon Willison described the [The Lethal Trifecta](https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jun/16/the-lethal-trifecta/) for AI agents: an agent that combines access to your private data, exposure to untrusted content, and a way to externally communicate (вҖңexfiltrationвҖқ). That вҖңuntrusted contentвҖқ can come in all sorts of ways, ask it to read a web page, and an attacker can easily put instructions on the website in 1pt white-on-white font to trick the gullible LLM to obtain that private data.

This is particularly serious when it comes to agents acting in a browser. Read an attackerвҖҷs web page, and it could trick the agent to go to your bank account in another tab and вҖңbuy you a presentвҖқ by transferring your balance to the kind attacker. [WillisonвҖҷs view](https://simonwillison.net/2025/Aug/25/agentic-browser-security/) is that вҖңthe _entire concept_ of an agentic browser extension is fatally flawed and cannot be built safelyвҖқ.

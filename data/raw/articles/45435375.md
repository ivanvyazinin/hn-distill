# The Beer Can

Connecting old and new.

[![brr](/media/snowman.png)](/)

[brr](/) March 18, 2023

This post is a celebration of one of my favorite bits of South Pole infrastructure.

The Beer Can, or “vertical tower” in official USAP parlance, is how we move between our comfortable living zone above, and our critical infrastructure zone below.

   [![Outside 01](/media/beer-can/outside-01-small.jpg)_The beer can, photographed from outside._](/media/beer-can/outside-01.jpg)

   [![Outside 03](/media/beer-can/outside-03-small.jpg)_Another view of the beer can, photographed from outside._](/media/beer-can/outside-03.jpg)

But before we get to the Beer Can itself, let’s talk about these different zones.

Above-ground is our modern elevated station. Dedicated in 2008, it’s a huge, contiguous building that contains all the facilities and amenities for indoor living.

   [![Elevated Station Exterior 01](/media/beer-can/elevated-station-exterior-01-small.jpg)_View of the elevated station, showing the beer can on one side._](/media/beer-can/elevated-station-exterior-01.jpg)

The elevated station contains our berthing, food service, administrative offices, gym, weight room, store, sauna, IT, communications, greenhouse, laundry, craft room, music room, and lounges, all under one roof. Depending on your job, you can go days or weeks without having to set foot outside the comfort of the elevated station.

   [![Elevated Station Interior 01](/media/beer-can/elevated-station-interior-01-small.jpg)_The galley, inside the elevated station. Modern luxury, at the bottom of the world!_](/media/beer-can/elevated-station-interior-01.jpg)

Keeping all of this running, however, requires a huge industrial village, buried deep below the snow.

Power generation, water filtration, heavy equipment maintenance, carpentry, warehousing, fuel storage, and any number of other industrial processes take places in the “Arches”, a series of semicircle storage/work facilities. These were constructed decades ago, and they have been buried over time.

I originally included this photo on my [South Pole Topography](/posts/south-pole-topography) post, but it’s helpful context for this one as well.

   [![Arches 01](/media/beer-can/arches-01-small.jpg)_The arches, showing the snowcover, the elevation difference, and the beer can that connects the arches to the elevated station._](/media/beer-can/arches-01.jpg)

The architecture down in the arches is… different. More industrial. More utilitarian. This is the infrastructural heart of South Pole Station, where we do the work that keeps the elevated station running.

   [![Warehouse 01](/media/beer-can/warehouse-01-small.jpg)_Our primary indoor cold storage facility, the Logistics Arch, or "LO"._](/media/beer-can/warehouse-01.jpg)

   [![Ice Tunnel 01](/media/beer-can/ice-tunnel-01-small.jpg)_An ice tunnel deep underground, which contains buried utility lines._](/media/beer-can/ice-tunnel-01.jpg)

   [![Power Plant 01](/media/beer-can/power-plant-01-small.jpg)_A genset in our power plant, hard at work keeping the lights on and the station warm._](/media/beer-can/power-plant-01.jpg)

There’s a stark difference between these two zones! And this brings us, finally, to the Beer Can: the transition element which bridges the gap between these two zones.

The Beer Can is built alongside the elevated station. It’s a large, corrugated metal cylinder, which descends about 50 feet, to reconcile the elevation difference between the elevated station and the arches.

  [![Inside Top 01](/media/beer-can/inside-top-01-small.jpg)](/media/beer-can/inside-top-01.jpg)

As discussed in [South Pole Topography](/posts/south-pole-topography), everything we build here eventually gets buried. The gap between ground level then, and ground level now, is several dozen feet. Logistically annoying, but an inevitable reality of South Pole architecture.

To descend down to the arches from the elevated station, you first pass through the freezer doors from the heated elevated station.

   [![Beer Can Station Entrance 01](/media/beer-can/beer-can-station-entrance-01-small.jpg)_Elevated station doors, as seen from just inside the beer can._](/media/beer-can/beer-can-station-entrance-01.jpg)

The beer can, and most of the arches, are not heated. Hence – the freezer doors.

   [![Beer Can Temperature 01](/media/beer-can/beer-can-temperature-01-small.jpg)_It is very, very cold in here. Usually right around ambient outside temperature near the top._](/media/beer-can/beer-can-temperature-01.jpg)

The beer can contains 90 stairs, plus a freight elevator. Looking vertically down, you can see the drop down to the floor, 50ft below.

   [![Looking Down 01](/media/beer-can/looking-down-01-small.jpg)_Looking down from the top of the beer can, 50ft to the bottom._](/media/beer-can/looking-down-01.jpg)

Descending the stairs, you encounter what was “ground” level when the beer can was originally built.

Here’s a door (yes, I love [doors](/posts/doors-of-mcmurdo)) to the outside, partway down the beer can.

   [![Door 01](/media/beer-can/door-01-small.jpg)_Door to the outside from the beer can. Originally at ground level, now far below ground level, and kept clear through a herculean shoveling effort._](/media/beer-can/door-01.jpg)

   [![Outside 02](/media/beer-can/outside-02-small.jpg)_Outside the beer can door, showing the extent of the snow elevation difference from what was once ground level._](/media/beer-can/outside-02.jpg)

Reaching the bottom, you’re treated to a view of the infrastructure which carries services (electrical, communications, heat, water, sewage, fuel) to and from the elevated station:

   [![Base 01](/media/beer-can/base-01-small.jpg)_The base of the beer can, showing utility lines leading up to the elevated station._](/media/beer-can/base-01.jpg)

Beyond that is a corridor, connecting horizontally from the base of the beer can to the arches:

   [![Corridor 01](/media/beer-can/corridor-01-small.jpg)_Corridor down at the base of the beer can, looking toward the arches._](/media/beer-can/corridor-01.jpg)

From here on, you’re in the industrial / utility zone. The first arch is the power plant and water plant. Then the logistics (warehousing) arch. Then finally, the heavy equipment shop and carpentry shop.

The beer can is also home to South Pole Station’s one and only elevator! It’s a freight elevator, for getting supplies to and from the warehouse space down in the arches, and for getting trash and recyclables down to storage.

   [![Lift Usage 01](/media/beer-can/lift-usage-01-small.jpg)_Our freight elevator, loaded up with recyclables and trash, heading down to the arches._](/media/beer-can/lift-usage-01.jpg)

And the elevator in operation:

 

As you may have guessed, walking up and down 90 stairs is a **chore**.

   [![Looking Up 01](/media/beer-can/looking-up-01-small.jpg)_It's... a long way up._](/media/beer-can/looking-up-01.jpg)

_Especially_ since the station sits nearly two miles above sea level! You definitely feel the effects of the altitude.

   [![Stairs 01](/media/beer-can/stairs-01-small.jpg)_Lots of stairs._](/media/beer-can/stairs-01.jpg)

It’s quite the workout! So much so that we’ve turned it into a fun competition. “Beer cans” are a worthy goal, a stat to collect and record. As in – “I’m going to do a few beer cans before lunch”.

 

And that’s it! A celebration of the South Pole Beer Can. Yes, it’s basically just a glorified stairwell.

But it’s the physical and conceptual separator between our living zone and our infrastructure zone, and I think that’s neat. Clearly you did too, because you’ve read to the end.

Until next time!

# Bluetooth Channel Sounding: The Next Leap in Bluetooth Innovation

While demand for proximity detection continues to grow across IoT, automotive, and logistics applications, current solutions remain hindered by insecurity and imprecision. Bluetooth Channel Sounding represents the next leap, delivering higher accuracy, enhanced security, and scalable deployment. This article explores the underlying signal-processing techniques, highlights key implementation considerations, and evaluates performance benefits across use cases such as secure distance measurement and indoor positioning. A step-by-step design example is included to guide developers through integrating Channel Sounding into Bluetooth-enabled devices.

## What is Channel Sounding?

Bluetooth Low Energy (LE) has been incorporated into billions of electronic devices, including mobile phones, laptops, gaming controllers, and in-vehicle entertainment systems worldwide. In September 2024, the Bluetooth Special Interest Group (SIG) announced a breakthrough feature called Bluetooth Channel Sounding, designed to increase accuracy and security in distance measurement, part of the Bluetooth Core Specification 6.0 release.

Many applications, including asset and object tracking, geofencing, smart door locks, remote keyless entry, and warehouse management, require short-range wireless distance measurement (ranging). Previously known as High-Accuracy Distance Measurement, Channel Sounding relies on two proven ranging methods to measure the distance between two Bluetooth LE–connected devices to achieve a theoretical distance of 150 meters (492.13 feet) with an accuracy of ±20 cm (7.87 inches). Achievable range and accuracy depend on signal quality (signal-to-noise ratio, or SNR), which is determined by transmit power, environmental conditions, and the signal-processing algorithm used to estimate distance from phase-based ranging (PBR) measurement or round-trip time (RTT) measurement. The number of antennas used helps to increase the probability of good SNR. Channel Sounding uses PBR and RTT to achieve its accuracy and security. While both of these capabilities are required for specification compliance, applications may choose to use either method or both for distance estimation based on system requirements and specific use case needs.

### PBR

Channel Sounding is used to calculate the distance between two paired Bluetooth devices. In a two-device configuration, one unit serves as the “initiator,” while the other operates as the “reflector.” Using the PBR method, the initiator transmits a sine-wave signal to the reflector, which then returns the signal. This exchange is performed across multiple frequencies. The final distance between the devices is determined by analyzing the phase differences between the transmitted and received signals (see Figure 1).

### RTT

Using RTT to estimate the distance between two Bluetooth devices, designated as the initiator and reflector, is conceptually straightforward. The time of flight (ToF) for signal packets traveling from the initiator to the reflector (ToFI-R) and returning to the initiator (ToFR-I) provides the basis for distance calculation. Given that electronic signals propagate at the speed of light (c), a known constant, the distance between the devices can be derived using the following formula:

_c_ × (_ToFI-R_ + _ToFR-I_) ÷ 2

Remember that the ToF is an over-the-air timing method. In practice, the setup time for Device A and Device B, which is needed before transmission over the air, should be considered to ensure accurate results.

Bluetooth technology uses 40 channels in the 2.4-GHz band, each spaced 2 MHz apart. In contrast, Channel Sounding supports up to 72 channels with 1-MHz spacing, providing a significantly wider operational bandwidth. This expanded channel set plays a critical role in improving measurement accuracy and system robustness, particularly in environments characterized by reflective surfaces and multipath propagation. By utilizing a larger pool of narrowly spaced channels, Channel Sounding can better capture channel characteristics, reduce the impact of interference, and improve the reliability of distance estimation and positioning. This makes it especially valuable for Bluetooth deployments in real-world scenarios where obstacles and reflections are common.

## Channel Sounding security

Two common cyberattack techniques are spoofing and man-in-the-middle (MITM) attacks. In spoofing, malicious actors falsify their digital identities to impersonate trusted devices, deceiving users into granting access or divulging sensitive information. MITM attacks involve an adversary covertly positioning themselves between two communicating devices, intercepting and potentially modifying data exchanges. Once embedded within the system, attackers can manipulate communications or exfiltrate data to serve their malicious objectives.

Bluetooth Channel Sounding significantly enhances security when PBR and RTT are employed in tandem. Because these two ranging methods rely on fundamentally distinct physical principles, it becomes exceedingly difficult for attackers to compromise both simultaneously while maintaining coherent communication. This layered approach complicates coordinated spoofing and MITM attempts.

## Channel Sounding applications

“Channel Sounding supports many types of applications, including PEPS \[Passive Entry Passive Start\], automapping, and indoor ranging,” said Aashish Chaddha, product marketing manager at Silicon Labs. “For example, in multi-access-point \[AP\] networks, transmit power must adjust to each AP’s proximity to its neighbors. Channel Sounding allows APs to determine their relative positions by having a central coordinator assign one AP as the initiator to range with others. Repeating this process for all APs creates a geometric map of their layout, optimizing power control and spatial awareness.”

Bluetooth Channel Sounding provides a high-security ranging solution for proximity applications. Its reliable distance estimation and resistance to spoofing make it perfect for use cases where traditional signal strength methods are inadequate. Here are two illustrations.

### Secure car entry

In a conventional relay attack, one attacker positions themselves near the vehicle owner, intercepting authentication signals emitted by the owner’s smartphone or key fob. An accomplice then relays these signals to the vehicle over a separate communication channel, enabling unauthorized access without requiring possession of the original device.

Channel Sounding provides a robust defense against this threat. By incorporating advanced signal verification that leverages both PBR and RTT measurement methods, Channel Sounding introduces safeguards that are exceedingly difficult to compromise simultaneously. This layered validation ensures that unauthorized unlock attempts are unsuccessful, preserving the security and reliability of the vehicle access system.

### Warehouse application: precise indoor location

In warehouses, hospitals, and similar facilities, keeping track of the exact location of tools and equipment is essential for maintaining operational efficiency. Bluetooth Channel Sounding enables precise indoor localization, significantly reducing time wasted on manual searches and streamlining workflow. In critical situations, such as earthquakes or mass-casualty traffic incidents, hospital emergency rooms are often overwhelmed, and life-saving devices may be relocated across departments. In such cases, knowing the precise location of vital equipment becomes a matter of urgency, enabling faster response and more effective care delivery.

## Design example

Devices within large commercial or industrial buildings typically require multiple network APs to maintain consistent internet connectivity. Setting up such a system can be a time-intensive process, as it involves several critical steps: conducting a site survey to generate a heatmap of signal strength, optimizing the placement and configuration of APs, and continuously fine-tuning performance to mitigate signal degradation and interference. This performance is not static; it can be disrupted by environmental changes, such as the rearrangement of furniture, or by the introduction of new sources of interference, such as recently installed microwave equipment.

Here is an example of designing a four-AP setup process that uses Channel Sounding’s indoor ranging capabilities to continuously optimize performance. As shown in Figure 5, four network APs are set up in a medical facility: AP0 in an administrative office, AP1 for staff, AP2 in the doctor’s office, and AP3 connecting the MRI machine.

[![A medical facility is equipped with four network access points.](https://www.embedded.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2025/10/Fig.-5.png "A medical facility is equipped with four network access points")](https://www.embedded.com/fig-5-11/)

Figure 5: A medical facility is equipped with four network APs: AP0 located in the administrative office, AP1 designated for staff use, AP2 positioned in the doctor’s office, and AP3 connected to the MRI machine. (Source: Silicon Labs)

One of the design considerations in managing APs is the distance between them and the power needed for communication. If the APs are placed close together, reducing transmit power can help minimize interference and enhance overall network stability. Channel Sounding, with its ability to accurately determine the distance between devices, enables intelligent system optimization. By assessing device separation and deployment density decisions, dynamic adjustment to transmit power can be made.

A central coordinator selects one AP, such as AP0, to act as the initiator, while the remaining APs function as reflectors. The initiator then performs ranging operations with each of the other APs. This process is repeated for every AP in the system, ultimately generating a geometric map (Figure 5) that reveals the relative positions of all APs within the network, enabling the Bluetooth application to optimize the power needed for communication.

[![The generated geometric map shows the relative positions of all APs within the network.](https://www.embedded.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2025/10/Fig.-6.png "The generated geometric map shows the relative positions of all APs within the network")](https://www.embedded.com/fig-6-11/)

Figure 6: The generated geometric map shows the relative positions of all APs within the network. Channel Sounding then determines the appropriate power level for communication. (Source: Silicon Labs)

In this example, each AP in the system integrates a Channel Sounding system-on-chip (SoC), such as the Silicon Labs BG24 or BG24L, which can operate as either an initiator or a reflector. During the initial design phase, it is advantageous to evaluate the device using a development kit equipped with a dual-antenna Channel Sounding module, as shown in Figure 7. These pre-validated dev kits help eliminate many of the trial-and-error steps typically encountered during development. Once the design is finalized, the SoC can be embedded into a more compact form factor suitable for AP hardware. To further streamline development and accelerate deployment, Silicon Labs also offers software support for the dev kit.

[![Duel-antenna Channel Sounding board (front).](https://www.embedded.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2025/10/Fig.-7.png?w=1024 "Duel-antenna Channel Sounding board (front)")](https://www.embedded.com/fig-7-11/)

Figure 7: An example of development kits showing a Channel Sounding SoC, dual antennas, and on-board debugger (Source: Silicon Labs)

“Developers have different design needs when building with Bluetooth Channel Sounding: Some applications require more computing and memory to perform complex ranging calculations, while others need simple, responsive nodes that can participate in the process without doing the heavy lifting,” said Gansu Natarajan, Bluetooth product manager at Silicon Labs. “The pin-compatible BG24 and BG24L give developers flexibility. BG24 is ideal for acting as the initiator in Channel Sounding, with support for up to 256-kB SRAM and 1,536-kB flash, well-suited for more memory-intensive algorithms. On the other hand, BG24L is a great fit for reflector roles, such as in asset tags or proximity-based access devices, where the node only needs to respond reliably and efficiently without running the ranging computations itself.”

Bluetooth Channel Sounding has led to significant breakthroughs in Bluetooth LE technology. Many applications, such as secure tracking, precise indoor location within warehouses, smart door locks, and geofencing, benefit from this innovation through higher accuracy and security by adding Channel Sounding on top of RSSI and AoA/AoD.

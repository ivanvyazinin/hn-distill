# Armed police swarm student after AI mistakes bag of Doritos for a weapon

by[Michael Gwilliam](/author/mikegw/)

Published: Oct 23, 2025, 15:18

Save for later

Save for later

![doritos bag](https://www.dexerto.com/cdn-image/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/23/doritos-ai-gun-detection-student-swarmed.jpg?width=1200&quality=60&format=auto)Doritos

Concerns over [AI](https://www.dexerto.com/tag/ai/) surveillance in schools are intensifying after armed officers swarmed a 16-year-old student outside Kenwood High School in Baltimore when an AI gun detection system falsely flagged a Doritos bag as a firearm.

Taki Allen was hanging out with friends after football practice on October 20 when multiple police cars suddenly pulled up.

“It was like eight cop cars that came pulling up for us,” Allen told [WBAL-TV 11 News](https://www.wbaltv.com/article/student-handcuffed-ai-system-mistook-bag-chips-weapon/69114601). “They started walking toward me with guns, talking about ‘Get on the ground,’ and I was like, ‘What?’”

Article continues after ad

“They made me get on my knees, put my hands behind my back, and cuff me. Then they searched me and found nothing,” he said.

Allen was handcuffed at gunpoint. Police later showed him the AI-captured image that triggered the alert. The crumpled Doritos bag in his pocket had been mistaken for a gun.

“It was mainly like, am I gonna die? Are they going to kill me? “They showed me the picture, said that looks like a gun, I said, ‘no, it’s chips.’”

Article continues after ad

![Taki Allen student](https://www.dexerto.com/cdn-image/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/23/taki-allen-student-chips-AI-1024x576.jpg?width=1200&quality=75&format=auto)Fox/screenshot

Taki Allen doesn’t want to return to school after the incident.

## Student afraid to return to school after AI sends police after him

The AI system behind the incident is part of [Omnilert’s gun detection technology](https://www.omnilert.com/solution-brochures/omnilert-gun-detection-system), introduced in Baltimore County Public Schools last year. It scans existing surveillance footage and alerts police in real time when it detects what it believes to be a weapon.

Omnilert later admitted the incident was a “false positive” but claimed the system “functioned as intended,” saying its purpose is to “prioritize safety and awareness through rapid human verification.”

Baltimore County Public Schools echoed the company’s statement in a letter to parents, offering counseling services to students impacted by the incident.

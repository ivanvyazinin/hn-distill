# What’s Behind the Mysterious Ancient Wall in the Gobi Desert?

New research indicates that the Gobi Wall, which snakes through Inner Mongolia, was not a purely defensive structure.

![Two archaeologists excavate ancient wooden structure in arid Gobi Desert using brushes and excavation tools.](https://news.artnet.com/app/news-upload/2025/08/Excavation-at-G05-at-area.-Credit_-M.-Ullman-1024x768.jpg "Two archaeologists excavate ancient wooden structure in arid Gobi Desert using brushes and excavation tools.")

Excavation of the G05 section of the Gobi Wall. Photo: M. Ullman.

by  [Tim Brinkhof](https://news.artnet.com/about/tim-brinkhof-39148) September 24, 2025  [![Share](/app/themes/artnet-news/assets/img/share.svg)](https://www.addtoany.com/share) ShareShare This Article

When it comes to giant, centuries-old walls that may or may not be visible from outer space, the Great Wall of China tends to get all the love, and for [good reason](https://artsandculture.google.com/story/10-things-to-know-about-the-great-wall-of-china-simatai-great-wall-tourist-area/8wXxFCy7pu2HWA?hl=en). It’s the longest man-made structure in the history of the world, took over 2,000 years to build, and is the subject of countless legends and folktales. And yet, it’s not the only wall of its kind, not even in China.

Located northwest of the Great Wall and stretching through China into Mongolia and Russia is the Medieval Wall System: a 2,500 mile-long network of fortifications erected between the 10th and 13th century C.E., when the region was populated by a wide variety of cultures that sought to control each other’s movement.

The orange rocks of Bayan Zag in the Gobi desert, Mongolia. Photo: Wolfgang Kaehler / LightRocket via Getty Images.

While most of the Medieval Wall System has been extensively studied, one small yet significant segment—the so-called Gobi Wall—remains shrouded in mystery. For many years, what little was known about this 199 mile-long structure, which straddles the Gobi [Desert](https://news.artnet.com/art-world/see-ancient-relics-from-dunhuang-grottoes-before-its-too-late-185324) in Mongolia’s Ömnögovi province, came from Cold War studies conducted by the Soviet Union. Recently, this glaring lack of information inspired a team of researchers to study the Gobi Wall using modern technologies and methodologies. The results of their [research](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-445X/14/5/1087), published in April 2025, reveal a storied past.

#### **Studying the Gobi Wall**

While modern documentation of the Gobi Wall dates back to the 18th century, formal investigations did not begin until the 1950s, when excavations by Mongolian archaeologists landed the structure on Soviet-era maps. Since then, the wall has also been studied by Japanese and Russian archaeologists, who attributed its construction to the Tangut or Xi Xia dynasty, which ruled northwestern China from 1038 to 1227 C.E.

Through remote sensing, field surveys, and excavations, and laboratory analysis, the 2025 study managed to obtain a better understanding of the Gobi Wall’s age, construction process, maintenance, and purpose.

![Archaeologists examine ancient stone wall stretching across arid Gobi Desert landscape from elevated viewpoint.](https://news.artnet.com/app/news-upload/2025/08/gobi-wall-study-1024x627.jpg)

Aerial drone view of the preserved Gobi Wall section traversing the hillside. Photo: D Golan et al., _Land_.

Starting with age, the researchers confirmed that the wall was built under Xi Xia rule, with radiocarbon dating suggesting that much of the building took place during the reign of the first Xi Xia emperor, Yuanhao, whose time in power (1038 to 1048 C.E.) was marked by campaigns to conquer and secure new territories.

Charcoal deposits indicate the Gobi Wall was manned throughout the Xi Xia period, up until the dynasty’s final years. Also pointing to consistent occupation is the fact that the wall appears to have been constructed near wells and tree growths, which would have provided soldiers with the resources needed to remain at their posts.

![Aerial view of Gobi Desert fortification G03 with labeled distances, wall line, and nearby geographic features.](https://news.artnet.com/app/news-upload/2025/08/Drone-documentation-of-garrison-G03-Tal-Rogovski-arrangement-Dan-Golan-1024x593.jpg)

Drone documentation of garrison G03. Photo: Tal Rogovski, arrangement by Dan Golan.

Then there’s the purpose the Gobi Wall would have served. Previously, it was assumed that the wall, much like the Medieval System in general, was conceived as a purely defensive structure: a means to keep out the unwanted. Having taken a closer look, however, the researchers argue the Gobi Wall actually played a complex, proactive role in imperial strategy.

Although the Gobi Wall undoubtedly served a defensive purpose—large sections were built near sand dunes that would have acted as additional barriers—the structure also appears to have been a commercial and administrative hub, dividing people on one side while bringing them together on the other.

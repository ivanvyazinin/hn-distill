The **Leatherman** (c. 1839–1889) was a [vagabond](/wiki/Vagrancy "Vagrancy") famous for his handmade leather suit of clothes who traveled through the [northeastern United States](/wiki/Northeastern_United_States "Northeastern United States") on a regular circuit between the [Connecticut River](/wiki/Connecticut_River "Connecticut River") and the [Hudson River](/wiki/Hudson_River "Hudson River") from roughly 1857 to 1889. Of unknown origin, he was thought to be [French-Canadian](/wiki/French_Canadians "French Canadians") because of his fluency in the [French language](/wiki/French_language "French language"), his "broken English", and the French-language [prayer book](/wiki/Prayer_book "Prayer book") found on his person after his death. His identity remains unknown and controversial. He walked a repeating 365-mile (587 km) route year after year, which took him through certain towns in western [Connecticut](/wiki/Connecticut "Connecticut") and eastern [New York](/wiki/New_York_\(state\) "New York (state)"), returning to each town roughly every 34 days.[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1)

## Life

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Leatherman_\(vagabond\)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Life")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Leathermancave01.jpg/250px-Leathermancave01.jpg)](/wiki/File:Leathermancave01.jpg)

Entrance to the Leatherman Cave in [Watertown, Connecticut](/wiki/Watertown,_Connecticut "Watertown, Connecticut")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/07/This_is_the_Tories_Den.jpg/250px-This_is_the_Tories_Den.jpg)](/wiki/File:This_is_the_Tories_Den.jpg)

The Tory's den

Living in [rock shelters](/wiki/Rock_shelter "Rock shelter") and "leatherman caves", as they are now locally known,[\[2\]](#cite_note-letterbox-2) the Leatherman stopped at towns along his 365-mile (587 km) loop about every five weeks for food and supplies.[\[3\]](#cite_note-hudson-3) He was dubbed the "Leatherman" as his [adornment](/wiki/Adornment "Adornment") of hat, scarf, clothes, and shoes were handmade from [leather](/wiki/Leather "Leather").

An early article in the _[Burlington Free Press](/wiki/The_Burlington_Free_Press "The Burlington Free Press")_ dated April 7, 1870, refers to him as the "Leather-Clad Man". It also states that he spoke rarely and when addressed would simply speak in [monosyllables](/wiki/Monosyllable "Monosyllable"). According to contemporary rumors, he hailed from [Picardy, France](/wiki/Picardy "Picardy").[\[4\]](#cite_note-4)

Fluent in [French](/wiki/French_language "French language"), he communicated mostly with grunts and gestures, rarely using his broken English. When asked about his background, he would abruptly end the conversation.[\[5\]](#cite_note-redding-5)[\[6\]](#cite_note-bear-6) Upon his death, a French prayer book was found among his possessions.[\[6\]](#cite_note-bear-6) He declined meat on Fridays, giving rise to speculation that he was [Roman Catholic](/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church").[\[7\]](#cite_note-tkb-7)

It is unknown how he earned money. One store kept a record of an order: "one loaf of bread, a can of sardines, one-pound of fancy crackers, a pie, two quarts of coffee, one [gill](/wiki/Gill_\(volume\) "Gill (volume)") of brandy and a bottle of beer".[\[8\]](#cite_note-tarrytowns-8)

The Leatherman was well known in Connecticut. He was reliable in his rounds, and people would have food ready for him, which he often ate on their doorsteps.[\[6\]](#cite_note-bear-6)[\[9\]](#cite_note-skyweb-9) Ten towns along the Leatherman's route passed ordinances exempting him from the Connecticut state "[tramp law](/wiki/Vagrancy_law "Vagrancy law")" passed in 1879.[\[2\]](#cite_note-letterbox-2)

## Health and death

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Leatherman_\(vagabond\)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Health and death")\]

The Leatherman survived [blizzards](/wiki/Blizzard "Blizzard") and other foul weather by heating his rock shelters with fire. While his face was frostbitten at times during the winter, by the time of his death he had not lost any fingers, unlike other tramps of the time and area.[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1)

The Connecticut [Humane Society](/wiki/Humane_Society "Humane Society") had him arrested and hospitalized in 1888, which resulted in a diagnosis of "sane except for an emotional affliction", after which he was released, as he had money and desired freedom. He ultimately died from [cancer of the mouth](/wiki/Cancer_of_the_mouth "Cancer of the mouth").[\[8\]](#cite_note-tarrytowns-8) His body was found on March 24, 1889, in his Saw Mill Woods cave on the farm of George Dell in the town of [Mount Pleasant, New York](/wiki/Mount_Pleasant,_New_York "Mount Pleasant, New York"),[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1) near [Ossining](/wiki/Ossining_\(town\),_New_York "Ossining (town), New York").[\[5\]](#cite_note-redding-5)

## Identity controversy

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Leatherman_\(vagabond\)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Identity controversy")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Leathermancave02.jpg/250px-Leathermancave02.jpg)](/wiki/File:Leathermancave02.jpg)

Inside the Leatherman Cave in [Watertown, Connecticut](/wiki/Watertown,_Connecticut "Watertown, Connecticut")

The Leatherman's former tombstone read: "Final resting place of Jules Bourglay of Lyons, France, 'The Leather Man'..." He is identified with that name in many accounts.[\[2\]](#cite_note-letterbox-2)[\[10\]](#cite_note-watertown-10) However, according to researchers including Dan W. DeLuca,[\[11\]](#cite_note-:1-11) as well as his New York death certificate, his identity remains unknown.[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1) This name first appeared in a story published in the _Waterbury Daily American_ on August 16, 1884, but was later retracted on March 25, 26, and 27, 1889, and also in _[The Meriden Daily Journal](/wiki/The_Meriden_Daily_Journal "The Meriden Daily Journal")_ on March 29, 1889.[\[3\]](#cite_note-hudson-3)[\[9\]](#cite_note-skyweb-9) DeLuca was able to get a new headstone installed when the Leatherman's grave was moved away from Route 9 to another location within the cemetery on May 25, 2011. The new brass plaque simply reads "The Leatherman".[\[11\]](#cite_note-:1-11)

## Grave

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Leatherman_\(vagabond\)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Grave")\]

The Leatherman's grave is in the [Sparta Cemetery](/wiki/Sparta_Cemetery "Sparta Cemetery"), on [Route 9](/wiki/U.S._Route_9_in_New_York "U.S. Route 9 in New York") in [Ossining, New York](/wiki/Ossining_\(town\),_New_York "Ossining (town), New York"). The following inscription was carved on his original tombstone:[\[12\]](#cite_note-12)[\[13\]](#cite_note-13)

FINAL RESTING PLACE OF  
Jules Bourglay  
OF LYONS, FRANCE  
"THE LEATHER MAN"  
who regularly walked a 365-mile route  
through Westchester and Connecticut from  
the Connecticut River to the Hudson  
living in caves in the years  
1858–1889

### Reburial

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Leatherman_\(vagabond\)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Reburial")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/SpartaCemetery4.tif/lossy-page1-250px-SpartaCemetery4.tif.jpg)](/wiki/File:SpartaCemetery4.tif)

The 2011 grave site with new headstone that reads "The Leatherman".

The Leatherman's original grave in Sparta Cemetery was within 16 feet (5 m) of [Route 9](/wiki/U.S._Route_9_in_New_York "U.S. Route 9 in New York"). On May 25, 2011, the grave's contents were exhumed and reburied at a new site in the cemetery. No visible human remains were recovered during the exhumation. Only coffin nails and soil from the original burial plot were reburied in the new grave. One of the reasons for the exhumation was the hope there were remains that would help determine Leatherman's origins.[\[14\]](#cite_note-lm-14)[\[15\]](#cite_note-15)

Nicholas Bellantoni, a [University of Connecticut](/wiki/University_of_Connecticut "University of Connecticut") archaeologist and supervisor of the exhumation, cited time, the effect of traffic over the shallow original gravesite, and possible removal of graveside material by a road-grading project for destroying all hard and soft tissue in the grave.

## Popular media

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Leatherman_\(vagabond\)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Popular media")\]

*   The Leatherman inspired a song by the American rock band [Pearl Jam](/wiki/Pearl_Jam "Pearl Jam"), "Leatherman". It was released as a [B-side](/wiki/B-side "B-side") of the [single](/wiki/Single_\(music\) "Single (music)") "[Given to Fly](/wiki/Yield_\(album\)#Singles "Yield (album)")" from the 1998 album _[Yield](/wiki/Yield_\(album\) "Yield (album)")_.[\[3\]](#cite_note-hudson-3)
*   The song "[Mennyt mies](/wiki/Mennyt_mies "Mennyt mies")" by Finnish singer-songwriter [J. Karjalainen](/wiki/J._Karjalainen "J. Karjalainen") makes a reference to "Uuden-Englannin nahkamies" ("New England Leatherman").[\[16\]](#cite_note-16)
*   Leatherman was the subject of a 1984 video documentary which was shown on Connecticut Public Television.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]
*   In 1965, on his program _Perception_, [Dick Bertel](/wiki/Dick_Bertel "Dick Bertel") interviewed Mark Haber (1899–1994) about the Leatherman on [Channel 3, WTIC-TV (WFSB since 1974)](/wiki/WFSB "WFSB") in [Hartford, Connecticut](/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut "Hartford, Connecticut").[\[17\]](#cite_note-17)
*   The Leatherman's story (and his caves) are detailed as a vignette in the 2018 video game _[Where the Water Tastes Like Wine](/wiki/Where_the_Water_Tastes_Like_Wine "Where the Water Tastes Like Wine")_, where the player encounters a mysterious character later revealed to be the Leatherman himself.[\[18\]](#cite_note-18)\[_[unreliable source?](/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources "Wikipedia:Reliable sources")_\]
*   In a 2025 article in _[The New York Times Magazine](/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine "The New York Times Magazine")_, [Sam Anderson](/wiki/Sam_Anderson_\(writer\) "Sam Anderson (writer)") describes a trip along the "Old Leatherman's" route.[\[19\]](#cite_note-19)

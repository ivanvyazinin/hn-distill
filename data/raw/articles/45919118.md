# How I Learned the Pythagorean Theorem

[](/)

The younger child and I were talking about maths on the school run this morning, and today’s topic was geometry. I was pleased to discover that he’s already got a reasonable comprehension of the Pythagorean Theorem[1](#footnote-27581-1 "You know the one: the square of the triangle’s hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides. You could conceivably know it by some other mnemonic, but the essence is that if you know the lengths of two sides of any right-angled triangle, you can derive the length of the third with some moderately-simple arithmetic."): I was telling him that I was about his age when I first came across it, but in my case I first had a practical, rather than theoretical, impetus to learn it.

It was the 1980s, and I was teaching myself _Dr. Logo_, [Digital Research](https://www.digitalresearch.biz/)‘s implementation of the [Logo programming language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logo_\(programming_language\)) (possibly from [this book](https://archive.org/details/using-dr-logo-on-the-amstrad-martin-simsacme/page/n51/mode/2up)). One day, I was writing a program to draw an indoor scene, including a window through which a mountain would be visible. My aim was to produce something like this:

[![Turtle-graphics illustration of a yellow window made of four rectangular quarters, with a triangular mountain peak visible through it with its peak at the centre of the window.](https://bcdn.danq.me/_q23u/2025/11/trrtl-mountain3-640x560.png)](https://trrtl.com/#UEsDBAoAAAAAAOZVbVv1A5nT9gIAAPYCAAABAAAAX1siIyBjbGVhbiBzbGF0ZVxucmVzZXQoKVxuXG4jIHdpbmRvdyBmcmFtZVxuY29sb3IgJ3llbGxvdydcbmZvcndhcmQgMzAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuZm9yd2FyZCAyMDBcbnJpZ2h0IDkwXG5mb3J3YXJkIDMwMFxucmlnaHQgOTBcbmZvcndhcmQgMjAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuXG4jIGFjcm9zcyB0aGUgd2luZG93XG5wZW4gdXBcbmZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxucGVuIGRvd25cbmZvcndhcmQgMjAwXG5sZWZ0IDkwXG5cbiMgZG93biB0aGUgd2luZG93XG5wZW4gdXBcbmZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG5sZWZ0IDkwXG5mb3J3YXJkIDEwMFxubGVmdCA5MFxucGVuIGRvd25cbmZvcndhcmQgMzAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuXG4jICMgbW91bnRhaW4gYXR0ZW1wdCAxIGJ5IERhbiwgYWdlZCA4XG4jIGhvbWUoKVxuIyBjb2xvciAnd2hpdGUnXG4jIHJpZ2h0IDQ1XG4jIGZvcndhcmQgMTAwXG4jIHJpZ2h0IDkwXG4jIGZvcndhcmQgMTAwXG5cbiMgIyBtb3VudGFpbiBhdHRlbXB0IDIgYnkgRGFuLCBhZ2VkIDhcbiMgaG9tZSgpXG4jIGNvbG9yICdsaWdodGJsdWUnXG4jIHJpZ2h0IDQ1XG4jIGZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG4jIHJpZ2h0IDkwXG4jIGZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG5cbiMgbW91bnRhaW4gYXR0ZW1wdCAzIGJ5IERhbiwgYWdlZCA4LCB1c2luZyBQeXRoYWdvcmFzJyB0aGVvcmVtXG5ob21lKClcbmNvbG9yICdsaWdodGdyZWVuJ1xucmlnaHQgNDVcbmZvcndhcmQgMTQxICMgMTAwXjIgKyAxMDBeMiA9IDE0MV4yXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuZm9yd2FyZCAxNDFcbiJdUEsBAhQACgAAAAAA5lVtW/UDmdP2AgAA9gIAAAEAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAF9QSwUGAAAAAAEAAQAvAAAAFQMAAAAA)

All of these graphics were made using [my own 2019 implementation](/2019/02/15/trrtl/) of Logo, [TRRTL.COM](https://trrtl.com/): click on any graphic to continue drawing!

My window was 300 “steps”[2](#footnote-27581-2 "Just one way that Logo is/was a cute programming language was its use of “steps” – as in, turtle-steps – to measure distances. You might approximate them as pixels, but a “step” has meaning even for lines that don’t map linearly to pixels because they’re at wonky angles, for example.") tall by 200 steps wide and bisected in both directions when I came to make my first attempt at the mountain.

And so, naively, starting from the lower-left, I thought I’d need some code like this:

RIGHT 45
FORWARD 100
RIGHT 90
FORWARD 100  

But what I ended up with was this:

[![An attempt to draw the mountain results in it being too short: its peak doesn't reach half-way, and it ends before the far side of the window.](https://bcdn.danq.me/_q23u/2025/11/trrtl-mountain1-640x560.png)](https://trrtl.com/#UEsDBAoAAAAAAG1YbVvYOA3OxAEAAMQBAAABAAAAX1siIyBjbGVhbiBzbGF0ZVxucmVzZXQoKVxuXG4jIHdpbmRvdyBmcmFtZVxuY29sb3IgJ3llbGxvdydcbmZvcndhcmQgMzAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuZm9yd2FyZCAyMDBcbnJpZ2h0IDkwXG5mb3J3YXJkIDMwMFxucmlnaHQgOTBcbmZvcndhcmQgMjAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuXG4jIGFjcm9zcyB0aGUgd2luZG93XG5wZW4gdXBcbmZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxucGVuIGRvd25cbmZvcndhcmQgMjAwXG5sZWZ0IDkwXG5cbiMgZG93biB0aGUgd2luZG93XG5wZW4gdXBcbmZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG5sZWZ0IDkwXG5mb3J3YXJkIDEwMFxubGVmdCA5MFxucGVuIGRvd25cbmZvcndhcmQgMzAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuXG4jIG1vdW50YWluIGF0dGVtcHQgMSBieSBEYW4sIGFnZWQgOFxuaG9tZSgpXG5jb2xvciAnd2hpdGUnXG5yaWdodCA0NVxuZm9yd2FyZCAxMDBcbnJpZ2h0IDkwXG5mb3J3YXJkIDEwMCJdUEsBAhQACgAAAAAAbVhtW9g4Dc7EAQAAxAEAAAEAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAF9QSwUGAAAAAAEAAQAvAAAA4wEAAAAA)

Hypotenuse? More like need-another-try-potenuse.

I instantly realised my mistake: _of course_ the sides of the mountain would need to be longer so that the peak would reach the mid-point of the window and the far side would hit its far corner. But _how much_ longer ought it to be.

I intuited that the number I’d be looking for must be greater than 100 but less than 250: these were, logically, the _bounds_ I was working within. 100 would be correct if my line were horizontal (a “flat” mountain?), and 250 was long enough to go the “long way” to the centrepoint of the window (100 along, and 150 up). So I took a guess at 150 and… it was pretty close… but still wrong:

[![This time the mountain "overshoots" slightly: its peak is too far over and it stretches beyond the window frame.](https://bcdn.danq.me/_q23u/2025/11/trrtl-mountain2-640x560.png)](https://trrtl.com/#UEsDBAoAAAAAAIBYbVsIrHBOygEAAMoBAAABAAAAX1siIyBjbGVhbiBzbGF0ZVxucmVzZXQoKVxuXG4jIHdpbmRvdyBmcmFtZVxuY29sb3IgJ3llbGxvdydcbmZvcndhcmQgMzAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuZm9yd2FyZCAyMDBcbnJpZ2h0IDkwXG5mb3J3YXJkIDMwMFxucmlnaHQgOTBcbmZvcndhcmQgMjAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuXG4jIGFjcm9zcyB0aGUgd2luZG93XG5wZW4gdXBcbmZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxucGVuIGRvd25cbmZvcndhcmQgMjAwXG5sZWZ0IDkwXG5cbiMgZG93biB0aGUgd2luZG93XG5wZW4gdXBcbmZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG5sZWZ0IDkwXG5mb3J3YXJkIDEwMFxubGVmdCA5MFxucGVuIGRvd25cbmZvcndhcmQgMzAwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuXG4jIG1vdW50YWluIGF0dGVtcHQgMiBieSBEYW4sIGFnZWQgOFxuaG9tZSgpXG5jb2xvciAnbGlnaHRibHVlJ1xucmlnaHQgNDVcbmZvcndhcmQgMTUwXG5yaWdodCA5MFxuZm9yd2FyZCAxNTBcbiJdUEsBAhQACgAAAAAAgFhtWwiscE7KAQAAygEAAAEAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAF9QSwUGAAAAAAEAAQAvAAAA6QEAAAAA)

I remember being confused and frustrated that the result was so close but still wrong. The reason, of course, is that the relationship between the lengths of the sides of a triangle don’t scale in a 1:1 way, but this was the first time I found myself having to think about _why_.

So I found my mother and asked her what I was doing wrong. I’m sure it must have delighted her to dust-off some rarely-accessed knowledge from her own school years and teach me about Pythagoras’!

The correct answer, of course, is given by:

100 2 \+ 100 2

I so rarely get to use [MathML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MathML) that I had to look up the syntax.

The answer, therefore, is… **141.421** (to three decimal places). So I rounded to 141 and my diagram worked![3](#footnote-27581-3 "I’d later become unstuck by rounding, while trying to make a more-complex diagram with a zig-zag pattern running along a ribbon: a small rounding error became compounded over a long time and lead to me being a couple of pixels off where I intended. But that’s another story.")

What made this maths lesson from my mother so memorable was that _it fed a tangible goal_. I had something I wanted to _achieve_, and I learned the maths that I needed to get there. And now it’s impermeably etched onto my brain.

I learned the quadratic equation formula and how to perform algebraic integration by rote, and I guarantee that it’s less well-established in my long-term memory than, say, the sine and cosine rules or how to solve a simultaneous equation because _I’ve more-often needed to do **those** things outside of the classroom!_

So I guess the lesson is that I should be trying to keep an eye out for practical applications of maths that I can share with my kids. Real problems that are interesting to solve, to help build the memorable grounding that latter supports the more-challenging and intangible abstract maths that they may wish to pursue later.

Both kids are sharp young mathematicians, and the younger one seems especially to enjoy it, so feeding that passion feels well-worthwhile. Perhaps I should show them [TRRTL.COM](https://trrtl.com/) so they can try their hand at Logo!

But that’s another story.

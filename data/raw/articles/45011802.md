# Unlocking high software engineering pace: Partner with Product to pay down technical debt

Technical debt is inevitable. I learned this the hard way.

At one company, I joined to lead Engineering just as the team started building a new product for a new market. The team had worked for five years on a different product that they described as riddled with technical debt. It was a support nightmare. They were thrilled to start fresh. It was a chance to get it right!

I shouldn’t have been surprised when we chose technologies and made architectural and implementation decisions that we later came to regret. We were a good team, but we weren’t clairvoyant. We couldn’t see all of the ways the product would need to serve customers a year or two down the road.

In short, you can’t avoid at least some technical debt.

[![](https://dev.jimgrey.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/deferred-maintenance.jpg?w=1024)](https://dev.jimgrey.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/deferred-maintenance.jpg)

Deferred maintenance – don’t let this happen to you!

I’ve developed an approach that treats remediating technical debt as a partnership challenge more than a technical one. Here’s how it works.

## Make technical debt visible

I like to start by making technical debt visible. Let the whole team come together to identify and categorize problem areas. We’re looking for security issues, performance bottlenecks, and code that’s just hard to work with.

This needs periodic updating, by the way — no more than quarterly. Your technical debt landscape changes as you build new features, as old problems get worse, and as your business grows and changes.

But here’s the key: we track this stuff with business impact in mind. We measure things like how often we’re having production incidents, how long features are taking to deliver, and where our defect rates are highest.

This gives us data we can actually use in conversations with Product leadership.

## Build solid relationships with Product leaders

Which brings me to the most important part — building solid relationships with Product leaders. This can and should happen at all levels of your organization.

The idea is to create real give and take with Product, helping them understand how the state of the codebase actually affects their goals. When you show them that certain areas of the code make it take twice as long to build features, or that production issues are hurting customer retention, suddenly technical debt becomes their problem too, not just Engineering’s.

This isn’t about being manipulative. It’s about helping Product leaders understand the connection between code quality and business outcomes. Once they see it, they can’t unsee it.

## Look for strategic debt paydown opportunities

Here’s where it gets practical. When Product wants to enhance an area of code that’s problematic, that’s your opportunity.

I ask the team to roughly calculate the value of fixing the technical debt as part of the larger effort. Can you figure out that spending two weeks cleaning up this code will save the team six weeks on the next three features? Or that spending two weeks pulling those gnarly legacy services out into their own microservices allows you to both modernize the product while also creating value in the form of public endpoints? These are easy conversations.

You’re not asking for permission to “clean things up.” You’re saying that taking this small hit now pays off for a long time.

## Connect technical debt to predictability

Product leaders love Engineering teams that deliver predictably: within projected timeframes and at expected levels of quality. Very often, production incidents and high-defect areas point directly to unresolved technical debt. These create unplanned work that makes any team unpredictable.

When you can show Product how fixing underlying technical issues will help us build and maintain a high pace of forward motion, technical debt remediation becomes a business enabler, not just something Engineering wants to do.

## Focus on what’s actually in the way

This isn’t about having a perfect codebase. Here’s an admittedly imperfect metaphor: a codebase is like a big woods with hiking trails. Trees fall down in woods all the time, but you only go remove one when it blocks one of the trails.

Focus on technical debt that’s actively in the way–of performance, of quality, of your ability to deliver features. The rest, document and track, but don’t prioritize it until it’s about to be in the way.

This pragmatic approach ensures we’re investing our time where it’ll have the biggest impact.

## The payoff

Through this partnership with Product, you can build a broader culture where everyone agrees that keeping the codebase healthy is key to going faster and enabling better customer expansion and retention.

Product leaders start factoring technical health into their planning, and engineers feel empowered to propose improvements. In the end you deliver features faster, your systems are more reliable, and you can maintain a high pace of innovation and value creation.

But most importantly, you stop having those frustrating conversations where Engineering is asking for time to “fix technical debt” and Product is wondering why they can’t just build the features customers want.

Instead, you’re having strategic conversations about how to invest in the technical foundation that enables you to serve customers better, faster, and more reliably. That’s a conversation everyone can get behind.

## The bottom line

Technical debt will always be with us. The question is whether it’s going to be a constant source of friction between Engineering and Product, or whether it’s going to be a shared business challenge that you tackle together strategically.

I’ve seen both approaches. The partnership approach works better, every time.

# This Calif. construction worker unofficially broke a fabled world record

## Alo Slebir went from Santa Cruz's junior lifeguard program to breaking the biggest barrier in surfing

Alessandro “Alo” Slebir, pictured last winter, started surfing Mavericks at the age of 14 and quickly became one of California’s leading big wave surfers.

Photo by Frank Quirarte Photography/mavsurfer.surf

By [Lester Black](/author/lester-black/), Cannabis editorSep 11, 2025

It was a muggy July day in Santa Cruz and life could not have been more mundane for Alessandro “Alo” Slebir. The 24-year-old was pounding wooden concrete forms into a missing section of sidewalk. Cars raced by on a suburban highway as a neighbor walked over, looked at Slebir and asked, “Should I move my car?”

![](https://cdn-channels-pixel.ex.co/events/0012000001fxZm9AAE?integrationType=DEFAULT&template=design%2Farticle%2Fplatypus_two_column.tpl)

“Hey, no worries, you’re fine! You’re not in the way,” Slebir said, in his characteristically friendly demeanor before returning to his hammer.

You wouldn’t know it from the work boots or dirt smudged on his hands (nor would the neighbor, clearly), but just seven months earlier, Slebir was the man responsible [for unofficially breaking surfing’s most mythical goal](https://www.sfgate.com/sports/article/santa-cruz-surfer-rode-potential-world-record-wave-20008890.php): riding a world-record-shattering 108-foot-tall wave.

Alo Slebir said he will do anything that allows him to surf every swell that hits Mavericks, whether that means surf sponsors are paying his way or he needs to work a day job.

Lester Black/SFGATE

Such is the split-screen life of Slebir. In the summer, he works construction, but in the winter, he’s a big wave machine riding impossibly massive waves at Mavericks taller than most buildings. 

Despite being a surf legend in the making, Slebir spends more time in work boots than wet suits these days. Big wave surfing doesn’t pay enough to support his passion, so the 24-year-old is still working construction. Although that could change.

Slebir was recently [featured in an ESPN short](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RdGr48kv4X8) centered on him surfing a 100-foot wave, a height that still has yet to be officially confirmed. The newscasters compared it to a female runner clearing a 4-minute mile. The wave is currently being studied by experts at the Big Wave Challenge. Bill Sharp, the award show’s organizer, said in a news release that if any world records are attained, “that status will be revealed” at this year’s Big Wave Challenge Awards in Newport Beach on Sept. 13.

Alo Slebir appears as just as small dot on the face of a massive Mavericks wave on Dec. 23, 2024. Mavericks Rescue estimated this wave was 108 feet tall. Photo by Frank Quirarte Photography/mavsurfer.surf

That award show could catapult this Santa Cruz phenom into being the next worldwide surfing star — and, more importantly to the 24-year-old, let him finally drop the hammer for good.

## ‘It scared the living s—t out of me’

Slebir’s path to riding down a face of a purported 100-foot wave begins in perhaps the most obvious place possible: the Santa Cruz pier.

Advertisement

Article continues below this ad

Surfing is in the DNA of this coastal community. Santa Cruz is likely the first place anyone surfed in California, [when three Hawaiian princes](https://www.californiasun.co/the-hawaiian-princes-who-brought-surfing-to-california/) used locally milled redwood boards to surf at the mouth of the town’s river in 1885. The seaside city seems unintentionally designed for the sport, with a winding and rocky coastline that attracts swells from every direction, ensuring that there is a surfable wave breaking somewhere in Santa Cruz almost every day of the year.

The three Hawaiian princes, David Kawananakoa, Edward Keliiahonui and Jonah Kuhio Kalanianaole, pictured at St. Matthew’s Hall, San Mateo, Calif., in 1885. They introduced surfing to California.

Digital Archives of Hawai'i

Slebir’s parents, his mom from Italy and his father from California, decided to raise him in Santa Cruz, where naturally he’s been on a board longer than he can remember. His first waves were near the pier, where he wore a life jacket while his dad carried him on a longboard. His parents told him he hated it, and he remembers having a distinct fear of the ocean as a young kid: “I vaguely remember being terrified of what at the time were probably 3- or 4-foot waves.” But growing up in Santa Cruz seems to have a way of pounding surfing into you. 

Slebir credits the town’s junior lifeguard program for making him comfortable in the ocean, and then the Mission Hill Middle School’s surf team for kicking off his love of the sport. It didn’t hurt that he had a friend who lived near the world-class Steamer Lane surf spot; he would stash his gear at his friend’s house and then ride his bike there after school so he could surf every day.

Advertisement

Article continues below this ad

By the time he was 14, he was showing enough promise that Pat Shaughnessy, a local firefighter and [Mavericks surfer](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pCH2UD57f4Q), decided Slebir was ready to try Mavericks, located 50 miles north of Santa Cruz. Slebir paddled out and followed Shaughnessy into the surf lineup; he remembers seeing Garrett McNamara, the former world record holder and legendary big wave surfer behind HBO’s “100 Foot Wave” series. Slebir proceeded to get pounded by multiple waves without getting a single ride.

“It scared the living s—t out of me,” he remembers. And yet he was immediately hooked.

Alo Slebir wipes out on a massive Mavericks wave. Photo by Frank Quirarte Photography/mavsurfer.surf

Slebir spent the next 10 years surfing Mavericks whenever he could. He went to UC Santa Cruz so he wouldn’t be far away from the wave, graduating in 2023 with a degree in legal studies. He became obsessed with his diet, training everyday in jujitsu and weightlifting. And two years ago, he linked up with Luca Padua, a fellow young Mavericks surfer from Half Moon Bay. They formed a team, deciding they would exclusively work together to use Jet Skis to pull each other into waves at Mavericks so they could take on whatever came their way.

The pair got their biggest test on a cold Northern California day in late December.

## ‘He surfed it perfectly’

Mavericks is a freak of nature. Located some 25 miles south of San Francisco, swirling underwater rock formations beneath a rocky outcropping called Pillar Point act as a “[magnifying glass](https://www.surfline.com/surf-news/mechanics-of-mavericks-california-big-wave-xl-wave-break/46358)” for swells from Alaska, turning them into massive waves. These huge waves then break over a rocky reef that, when the timing is right, form perfectly rideable wave faces.

FILE: A surfer launches off the lip of a wave at Mavericks on Monday, Dec. 17, 2018, near Princeton-by-the-Sea, Calif.

MediaNews Group via Getty Images

The surf spot near Half Moon Bay is one of “only a handful of places on the planet where waves in the 50- to 80-foot range will break in a way that surfers can ride,” said Steve Hawk, a former editor of Surfer magazine. “Often when the waves get gigantic, they lose their shape, they just become big closeouts where it breaks all at once and there’s no shoulder to ride.”

Mavericks was first pioneered by Jeff Clark, a Half Moon Bay local who grew up enthralled with the waves he saw off Pillar Point. He surfed them alone for over a decade in the late 1970s and 1980s, before sharing them with his friends. Eventually, other surfers found out, and Hawk helped blow the spot up by publishing a photo of a Mavericks wave on the cover of Surfer magazine in 1992. 

Mavericks quickly gained world fame and notoriety after pro surfer Mark Foo died after he fell while surfing a wave at the spot in December of 1994. Professionals flew from Hawaii to San Francisco to ride its winter waves, and a dedicated following of locals grew around the spot, spurred on by local [videographers like Powerlines Productions](https://www.youtube.com/user/powerlinestv), which documented every major swell. Surfers now spend their winters watching swell reports and waiting for winter storms in Alaska to send powerful swells toward Half Moon Bay. 

FILE: Mavericks legend Jeff Clark participates in the opening ceremony of the 2018/2019 World Surf League Big Wave Tour Mavericks Challenge at Mavericks Beach in Half Moon Bay, Calif., on Friday, Oct. 26, 2018.

MediaNews Group via Getty Images

On Dec. 21, 2024, one such “[monster](https://gcaptain.com/monster-pacific-storm-unleashes-massive-65-foot-waves/)” Pacific storm formed south of the Aleutian Islands, and two days later, the waves from this storm started pounding the Northern California coast, [killing one person](https://www.sfgate.com/weather/article/california-beaches-largest-swell-2024-19998427.php) and [snapping the Santa Cruz pier in half](https://www.sfgate.com/bayarea/article/part-santa-cruz-pier-collapsed-amid-massive-ocean-19998595.php). Swells are measured not only by their height, but also by the time between waves, called swell period. Any increase in swell period exponentially increases the strength of a wave, and this storm was producing extraordinary swell periods over 25 seconds long.

Mavericks thrives in long period waves, and the surf spot came alive on Dec. 23 with some of the biggest waves ever seen at the spot. Longtime Mavericks surfer Peter Mel called it “[the biggest mavs ever!](https://www.sfgate.com/sports/article/massive-calif-swell-delivers-day-days-19999962.php)”

Padua and Slebir were in the middle of these swirling seas, taking turns as one surfer drove a Jet Ski while the other surfer held on to a rope behind it, getting whipped into wave after wave. At about 3:15 p.m., Padua was driving when the biggest wall of water Slebir had seen all day rolled in, nearly blocking the horizon as far as he could see. He remembers Padua saying a simple, “Yup,” and gunning the Jet Ski’s engine. 

“I don’t think it hit me until I realized how fast I was going. Every wave that day was really, really big; they all started blending together. But I remember that wave being the fastest I’ve gone all day. That’s kind of when I knew, all right, that’s definitely a big wave,” Slebir said.

Alo Slebir and his surfing partner Luca Padua. Slebir credits his partner with putting him in the perfect position on Dec. 23 to catch the wave that is now a contender for a world record. Photo by Frank Quirarte Photography/mavsurfer.surf

Slebir pointed his board down the wall of water toward its breaking point, racing down and then compressing into a turn at the bottom of the wave’s face that sent him back into the heart of the wave. For a moment, he stood almost casually tall as the wave curled and rampaged behind him. Three seconds later, the wave’s lip leaped forward, nearly catching him, but he rode out to the wave’s shoulder, grinning in disbelief as looked at Padua.

The size of the wave and the way Slebir rode it immediately gained recognition.

“In my mind, he surfed it perfectly. He stayed just ahead of it, he didn’t run away from it,” Hawk said.

Photos of the wave blew up on social media. Lenny, the big wave surfer, commented “100 foot wave,” and a few days later, [Mavericks Rescue estimated that the wave was 108 feet tall](https://www.sfgate.com/sports/article/santa-cruz-surfer-rode-potential-world-record-wave-20008890.php), which, if true, would smash the current world record of 86 feet.

## ‘Just barely scraping by’

Back in Santa Cruz, six hours after Slebir finished repairing that missing sidewalk, the surfer-turned-construction worker was on his third job of the day. This time, he was helping build an elaborate backyard patio for one of the multimillion-dollar homes near West Cliff Drive, shoveling gravel into a truck — his bulging arms making quick work of each shovelful.

Advertisement

Article continues below this ad

Slebir’s life changed in some small ways after the wave. His Instagram follower count — a metric that’s become almost the most important number in a surfer’s ability to make money — increased from 2,000 to over 24,000. He already was getting some money from his sponsors, which include a supplement company and a local seltzer maker, but he is still “not even close” to getting enough money from surfing to pay his bills.

Alo Slebir said nothing will stop him from surfing Mavericks, even if that means he has to work construction. Lester Black/SFGATE

He currently works in construction for a longtime friend, who also happens to be a former Mavericks surfer, which allows him flexibility in the winter to chase every swell that hits Mavericks. But he’s worried about what happens when he gets kicked off his parent’s insurance when he turns 26, and is considering getting his MBA or becoming a firefighter. 

“I don’t want to be 30 years old and still thinking about what I want to do for a living. That’ll come up faster than I believe. I’ll keep trying to do the Mavericks thing, but insurance is also a thing,” Slebir said.

Advertisement

Article continues below this ad

The entire professional surfing industry is currently struggling to support the leading riders. [Steamer Lane’s Cold Water Classic](https://www.surfer.com/news/pro-surfers-making-less-money-than-ever) competition paid out $15,000 in 2008; now, it pays only $2,500 to the winner. And [sponsorship money is universally down](https://www.surfer.com/culture/your-ad-here), thanks in large part to the collapse of the surfing clothing brands that once paid for these athletes to fly around the world surfing. 

Hawk said during the surf industry’s heyday, a surfer like Slebir may have been able to make a living off surfing, but it wouldn’t be a massive amount and many big wave surfers are forced to work outside jobs. In 2023, Luke Shepardson [famously won the Eddie Aikau Big Wave Invitational](https://www.gq.com/story/luke-shepardson-profile), the most prestigious big wave competition, during his break from being the lifeguard at the very same beach.

FILE: On-duty lifeguard Luke Shepardson is hoisted up on the shoulders of Hawaii surfers Landon McNamara, Billy Kemper and Mark Healey after he won the Eddie Aikau Big Wave Invitational on Jan. 22, 2023, in Waimea Bay, Haleiwa, Hawaii. Donald Miralle/Getty Images

“There’s a long history of people in the surf world, really accomplished, devoted, committed big wave surfers just barely scraping by, doing other things besides surfing to pay their rent,” Hawk said. “He’s not alone in that, which in some ways makes it a more impressive and pure pursuit, because it’s for the love of it and not for the riches.”

Slebir’s fate could change this year if his Dec. 23 wave is certified as a world record. Measuring wave heights is incredibly difficult and controversial — the technology is fairly imprecise — and the initial 108-foot estimate was only described as an early estimate. The public will know more after the Sept. 13 Big Wave Challenge show, though even if the show’s organizers agree with the 108-foot measurement, final confirmation on any world record could take even longer. The last world record took 18 months before it was certified by Guinness World Records. Kylie Galloway, a spokesperson for Guinness, said they usually work with outside authorities for these types of records.

## ‘I’m not angry about it’

For many surfers, the entire idea of measuring a wave height is misunderstanding the point. Most surfers don’t use heights like runners use stopwatches to measure their success. Matt Warshaw, a former professional and expert on surfing, said the height measuring Sharp does is like “snake oil” both because it’s imprecise and because some waves that have a smaller height, like Tahiti’s deadly reef break Teahupoo, should actually be more respected.

“I think everything having to do with numbers and wave height is sort of stupid. Especially because a wave like Teahupoo doesn’t have to be big to be really terrifying,” Warshaw said.

Alo Slebir compares Mavericks to watching a massive waterfall crash in the ocean, and said it’s so alluring that he would be at the surf spot even if he couldn’t surf.

Photo by Frank Quirarte Photography/mavsurfer.surf

Warshaw said that Slebir rode his wave perfectly, and deserves all the attention he can get, but also said he doesn’t think the world should be hung up on Slebir getting paid money.

Advertisement

Article continues below this ad

“Surfing is more interesting and more true to itself when the guys that are doing incredible things are lifeguards and construction workers,” Warshaw said.

Slebir himself is in a similar camp. He doesn’t seem to have a drop of resentment toward other surfers getting paid while he has to pour cement. 

“I’m not angry about it. I just got to do what I’ve got to do to go ride big waves. We’re not in it for the money. We’re in it for our own selfish pursuit,” Slebir said.

Advertisement

Article continues below this ad

Slebir doesn’t even seem hung up on getting a world record. He said he’s just happy he got the wave, and gave his local big wave spot some more of the global attention it deserves.

“I think Mavericks was overlooked in a sense. It’s cool to show that California does get that big. That’s what’s most special to me. We’re going to be doing this regardless — don’t sleep on her,” Slebir said.

_**Bertie the Brain**_ is one of the first games developed in the [early history of video games](/wiki/Early_history_of_video_games "Early history of video games"). It was built in [Toronto](/wiki/Toronto "Toronto") by [Josef Kates](/wiki/Josef_Kates "Josef Kates") for the 1950 [Canadian National Exhibition](/wiki/Canadian_National_Exhibition "Canadian National Exhibition"). The four meter (13 foot) tall computer allowed exhibition attendees to play a game of [tic-tac-toe](/wiki/Tic-tac-toe "Tic-tac-toe") against an [artificial intelligence](/wiki/Artificial_intelligence_\(video_games\) "Artificial intelligence (video games)"). The player entered a move on a keypad in the form of a three-by-three grid, and the game played out on a grid of lights overhead. The machine had an adjustable [difficulty level](/wiki/Difficulty_level "Difficulty level"). After two weeks on display by [Rogers Majestic](/wiki/Rogers_Vacuum_Tube_Company "Rogers Vacuum Tube Company"), the machine was disassembled at the end of the exhibition and largely forgotten as a curiosity.

Kates built the game to showcase his [additron tube](/wiki/Additron_tube "Additron tube"), a miniature version of the [vacuum tube](/wiki/Vacuum_tube "Vacuum tube"), though the [transistor](/wiki/Transistor "Transistor") overtook it in computer development shortly thereafter. Patent issues prevented the additron tube from being used in computers besides _Bertie_ before it was no longer useful. _Bertie the Brain_ is a candidate for the first video game, as it was potentially the first computer game to have any sort of visual display of the game. It appeared only three years after the 1947 invention of the [cathode-ray tube amusement device](/wiki/Cathode-ray_tube_amusement_device "Cathode-ray tube amusement device"), the earliest known [interactive](/wiki/Interactivity "Interactivity") [electronic game](/wiki/Electronic_game "Electronic game") to use an electronic display. _Bertie_'s use of light bulbs rather than a screen with real-time [visual graphics](/wiki/Video_game_graphics "Video game graphics"), however, much less moving graphics, does not meet some definitions of a video game.

## History

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Bertie_the_Brain&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")\]

_Bertie the Brain_ was a [video game](/wiki/Video_game "Video game") version of [tic-tac-toe](/wiki/Tic-tac-toe "Tic-tac-toe"), built by [Dr. Josef Kates](/wiki/Josef_Kates "Josef Kates") for the 1950 [Canadian National Exhibition](/wiki/Canadian_National_Exhibition "Canadian National Exhibition").[\[1\]](#cite_note-CNE-1) Kates had previously worked at [Rogers Majestic](/wiki/Rogers_Vacuum_Tube_Company "Rogers Vacuum Tube Company") designing and building radar tubes during [World War II](/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), then after the war pursued graduate studies in the computing center at the [University of Toronto](/wiki/University_of_Toronto "University of Toronto") while continuing to work at Rogers Majestic.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Spacing-2) While there, he helped build the University of Toronto Electronic Computer ([UTEC](/wiki/UTEC "UTEC")), one of the first working computers in the world. He also designed his own miniature version of the [vacuum tube](/wiki/Vacuum_tube "Vacuum tube"), called the [additron tube](/wiki/Additron_tube "Additron tube"), which he registered with the [Radio Electronics Television Manufacturers' Association](/wiki/Radio_Electronics_Television_Manufacturers%27_Association "Radio Electronics Television Manufacturers' Association") on 20 March 1951 as type 6047.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Spacing-2)[\[3\]](#cite_note-Tube1-3)[\[4\]](#cite_note-Tube2-4)[\[5\]](#cite_note-Tube3-5)

After filing for a patent for the additron tube, Rogers Majestic pushed Kates to create a device to showcase the tube to potential buyers. Kates designed a specialized computer incorporating the technology and built it with the assistance of engineers from Rogers Majestic.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Spacing-2)[\[6\]](#cite_note-TCW3031-6) The large, four meter (13 foot) tall metal computer could only play tic-tac-toe and was installed in the Engineering Building at the Canadian National Exhibition from 25 August–9 September 1950.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Spacing-2)[\[7\]](#cite_note-CNEdates-7)

The additron-based computer, labeled as "Bertie the Brain" and subtitled "The Electronic Wonder by Rogers Majestic", was a success at the two-week exhibition, with attendees lining up to play it. Kates stayed by the machine when possible, adjusting the difficulty up or down for adults and children. Comedian [Danny Kaye](/wiki/Danny_Kaye "Danny Kaye") was photographed defeating the machine (after several attempts) for _[Life](/wiki/Life_\(magazine\) "Life (magazine)")_ magazine.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Spacing-2)

## Gameplay

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Bertie_the_Brain&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Gameplay")\]

_Bertie the Brain_ was a game of tic-tac-toe in which the player would select the position for their next move from a grid of nine lit buttons on a raised panel. The moves would appear on a grid of nine large squares set vertically on the machine as well as on the buttons, with either an X- or O-shaped light turning on in the corresponding space. The computer would make its move shortly after. A pair of signs to the right of the playfield, alternately lit up with "Electronic Brain" and an X or "Human Brain" and an O, marked which player's turn it was, and would light up along with "Win" when a player had won. _Bertie_ could be set to several difficulty levels.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Spacing-2) The computer responded almost instantly to the player's moves and at the highest difficulty level was designed to be unbeatable.[\[6\]](#cite_note-TCW3031-6)

## Legacy

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Bertie_the_Brain&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Legacy")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Additron_Tube_schematic.png/250px-Additron_Tube_schematic.png)](/wiki/File:Additron_Tube_schematic.png)

Circuitry schematic from the US patent for the [additron tube](/wiki/Additron_tube "Additron tube")

After the exhibition, _Bertie_ was dismantled and "largely forgotten" as a novelty. Kates has said that he was working on so many projects at the same time that he had no energy to spare for preserving it, despite its significance.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Spacing-2) Despite being the first implemented computer game—preceded only by theorized chess programs—and featured in an unpublished _Life_ magazine article, the game was largely forgotten, even by video game history books.[\[6\]](#cite_note-TCW3031-6) _Bertie'_s primary purpose, to promote the additron tube, went unfulfilled, as it was the only completed application of the technology. By the time Rogers Majestic pushed Kates to develop a working model for the Exhibition, he had been working on the tubes for a year, developing several revisions, and the University of Toronto team felt that the development was too slow to attempt to integrate them into the UTEC.[\[8\]](#cite_note-TCR-8)

Although other firms expressed interest to Kates and Rogers Majestic in using the tubes, issues with acquiring patents prevented him, Rogers Majestic, or the University of Toronto from patenting the tubes anywhere outside Canada until 1955, and the patent application was not accepted in the United States until March 1957, six years after filing.[\[8\]](#cite_note-TCR-8)[\[9\]](#cite_note-AdditronUSPatent-9) By then, research and use of vacuum tubes was heavily waning in the face of the rise of the superior [transistor](/wiki/Transistor "Transistor"), preventing any re-visitation of _Bertie_ or similar machines. Kates went on to a distinguished career in Canadian engineering, but did not return to working on vacuum tubes or computer games.[\[8\]](#cite_note-TCR-8)

_Bertie_ was created only three years after the 1947 invention of the [cathode-ray tube amusement device](/wiki/Cathode-ray_tube_amusement_device "Cathode-ray tube amusement device"), the earliest known [interactive](/wiki/Interactivity "Interactivity") [electronic game](/wiki/Electronic_game "Electronic game"), and while non-visual games had been developed for research computers such as [Alan Turing](/wiki/Alan_Turing "Alan Turing") and [Dietrich Prinz](/wiki/Dietrich_Prinz "Dietrich Prinz")'s _[Turochamp](/wiki/Turochamp "Turochamp")_ chess program for the [Ferranti Mark 1](/wiki/Ferranti_Mark_1 "Ferranti Mark 1") at the [University of Manchester](/wiki/University_of_Manchester "University of Manchester"), _Bertie_ was the first computer-based game to feature a visual display of any sort.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Spacing-2)[\[10\]](#cite_note-TVGD-10)[\[11\]](#cite_note-Replay-11) _Bertie_ is considered under some definitions in contention for the title of the first video game. While definitions vary, the prior cathode-ray tube amusement device was a purely analog electrical game, and while _Bertie_ did not feature an electronic screen it did run on a computer.[\[12\]](#cite_note-EVG-12) Another special-purpose computer-based game, _[Nimrod](/wiki/Nimrod_\(computer\) "Nimrod (computer)")_, was built in 1951, while the software-based tic-tac-toe game _[OXO](/wiki/OXO_\(video_game\) "OXO (video game)")_ and a [draughts](/wiki/Draughts "Draughts") program by [Christopher Strachey](/wiki/Christopher_Strachey "Christopher Strachey") were in 1952 the first [computer games](/wiki/PC_game "PC game") to display [visuals](/wiki/Video_game_graphics "Video game graphics") on an electronic screen rather than light bulbs.[\[6\]](#cite_note-TCW3031-6)[\[10\]](#cite_note-TVGD-10)[\[13\]](#cite_note-TCU-13)

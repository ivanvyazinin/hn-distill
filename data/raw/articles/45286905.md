# John Grisham Still Wonders: Will Texas Kill Robert Roberson?

Robert Roberson, who was convicted of killing his daughter using now-debunked "junk science" will face execution in less than a month. We talked to author and attorney John Grisham, who is writing the book about the extraordinary case.

By [Bethany Erickson](https://www.dmagazine.com/writers/bethany-erickson/) | September 17, 2025|3:11 pm

Ilana Panich-Linsman for the Innocence Project

[](https://facebook.com/sharer/sharer.php?u=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.dmagazine.com%2Ffrontburner%2F2025%2F09%2Fauthor-john-grisham-still-wonders-will-texas-kill-robert-roberson%2F)[](https://twitter.com/intent/tweet/?text=John Grisham Still Wonders: Will Texas Kill Robert Roberson? @DMagazine&url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.dmagazine.com%2Ffrontburner%2F2025%2F09%2Fauthor-john-grisham-still-wonders-will-texas-kill-robert-roberson%2F)[](https://www.linkedin.com/shareArticle/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.dmagazine.com%2Ffrontburner%2F2025%2F09%2Fauthor-john-grisham-still-wonders-will-texas-kill-robert-roberson%2F)[](https://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.dmagazine.com%2Ffrontburner%2F2025%2F09%2Fauthor-john-grisham-still-wonders-will-texas-kill-robert-roberson%2F&description=John Grisham Still Wonders: Will Texas Kill Robert Roberson?)[](/cdn-cgi/l/email-protection#516e2224333b3432256c1b3e393f711623382239303c710225383d3d71063e3f353423226b7106383d3d710534293022711a383d3d71033e3334232571033e333423223e3f6e77303c216a333e35286c39252521227462107463177463172626267f353c3036302b383f347f323e3c74631737233e3f253324233f3423746317636163647463176168746317302425393e237c3b3e393f7c3623382239303c7c2225383d3d7c263e3f353423227c26383d3d7c25342930227c3a383d3d7c233e333423257c233e333423223e3f746317)

Every Wednesday at 9 a.m., advocates and lawyers for Robert Roberson check the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals website and cross their fingers, hoping that they’ll see a decision on the death row inmate’s case. Time is running out—Roberson has 29 days until he is due to be executed for a crime that an [increasing number of people](https://innocenceproject.org/news/facing-a-miscarriage-of-justice-here-lawmakers-experts-and-prominent-voices-speak-out-in-support-of-robert-roberson/) believe he did not commit.

Barring a successful appeal, it’s likely that Roberson, who was convicted of capital murder after being accused of shaking his toddler daughter, Nikki Curtis, to death in 2002, will die on October 16, despite the persistent questions about his guilt. If Roberson loses his appeals, he will become the first person in the U.S. executed because of an allegation of “shaken baby syndrome.”

A year ago, author John Grisham wrote about Roberson for _D Magazine_, asking, “[Will Texas Kill This Innocent Man?](https://www.dmagazine.com/publications/d-magazine/2024/september/will-texas-kill-this-innocent-man/)” He is watching the time tick closer to the answer to that question, and like many, is concerned. Now working on a book about the case, he and I spoke at length on Monday about what we’ve learned in the last year.

“It looks like the clock may run out without any kind of action from the court,” Grisham, who is also an attorney, said. “There’s a great debate among the lawyers: Will the court act before the execution date? And there’s nothing to force them to do that. So the CCA (Texas Court of Criminal Appeals) could sit on the case until Robert is executed, which is kind of hard to believe, but it could happen.”

For many who have reviewed his voluminous case files, it’s a clear case of a conviction based on “junk science,” an old, debunked understanding of what doctors may see in a child who has been shaken severely. Roberson’s lawyers have filed several appeals over the years, only to be denied. They have submitted [five additional filings](https://drive.google.com/file/d/11bjsbvQ_PnV4wfKzCC56_1wy6ThyApRp/view) since February. So far, the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals has not made a move on any of them.

It’s not just that the science behind that diagnosis is considered dubious at best now. Roberson, his attorneys argue, didn’t commit a crime because there was no crime. “Texans should be outraged that the court has scheduled an execution date for a demonstrably innocent man,” [Gretchen Sween](https://www.dmagazine.com/law/2025/08/the-ongoing-effort-to-save-robert-roberson/), one of Roberson’s attorneys, said when his execution date was set. “Everyone who has taken the time to look at the evidence of Robert Roberson’s innocence—including the lead detective, one of the jurors, a range of highly qualified experts, and a bipartisan group of Texas lawmakers—has reached the same conclusion: Nikki’s death was a terrible tragedy. Robert did not kill her. There was no crime.”

Nikki, they argue, had been frequently sick and had spells where she would stop breathing. In the days leading up to her death, Roberson and his mother had visited an emergency room in Palestine, Texas, and then her pediatrician for diarrhea, vomiting, and coughing. Both times, doctors prescribed Phenergan, an anti-nausea drug that now has an FDA black-box warning about prescribing it to young children. Roberson has insisted that his sick daughter fell from bed in the middle of the night, and when he returned hours later, she was not breathing. He then rushed her to the emergency room. Nikki’s illness and the medication, Roberson’s lawyers say, have never really been explored by the courts as contributing factors to her death. Roberson became a prime suspect when nurses and doctors at the ER and later Children’s Dallas, where Nikki was transported, felt he wasn’t reacting like a parent whose child was in distress. It was only later that he was officially diagnosed with [autism](https://www.dmagazine.com/micropost/dallas-morning-news-poor-word-choice-highlights-an-issue-in-roberson-case/).

Since his conviction, Roberson has sat on death row for more than 20 years. He was originally set to be executed in October 2024, but that date came and went after a Texas House committee [subpoenaed Roberson](https://www.dmagazine.com/frontburner/2024/10/how-the-effort-to-save-robert-roberson-took-an-unprecedented-turn/) to testify at a meeting occurring a day after his execution date. During [that legislative hearing](https://www.dmagazine.com/frontburner/2024/10/how-the-effort-to-save-robert-roberson-took-an-unprecedented-turn/), much of the testimony that day discussed how sick Nikki was, and the medication used to treat her symptoms. It also centered around Roberson’s autism diagnosis and how that impacted his reaction to his daughter’s medical distress when doctors and detectives spoke to him. Both the detective who arrested him, Brian Wharton, and one of the jurors who convicted him, [Terre Compton](https://www.dmagazine.com/micropost/former-roberson-juror-testifies-before-house-committee/), testified that they no longer felt Roberson was guilty.

Grisham says that many don’t know just how close Roberson came to being executed last year. “He came within 90 minutes,” he said. “I talked to him a couple of weeks ago when I was at Livingston. He spent the entire afternoon at Huntsville on the death wing, waiting, and waiting, and waiting for some news, and I think even had his final meal.”

But everyone knew that the reprieve that came from the extraordinary legislative wrangling last fall would not continue. Earlier this year, Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton demanded that Anderson County, where Roberson was tried originally, set an execution date. In July, that date [was set](https://www.dmagazine.com/micropost/robert-roberson-will-be-executed-in-october/) for October 16. 

Which brings us to the waning days before Roberson’s execution date. Grisham remains hopeful that, at the very least, the appeals court rules for a new trial, and says “the perfect ending” would be if the court finds Roberson innocent. However, he has also seen numerous death row cases and knows the odds. “It’s hard to believe, but this is this is not unusual in death penalty litigation to have some last-minute claims that include new evidence and not to be able to get that evidence before the court because the courts say, ‘OK, look, we’ve already ruled on this case five, six, seven, eight times and we’re tired of it.  And the jury spoke 20-something years ago, the appellate courts have repeatedly affirmed what the jury did, and so we are not going to intervene.

“I don’t know if there’s a Hail Mary this year,” he continued. “I don’t know if there’s a miracle this year. We don’t know. We had hoped that with the new composition of the CCA, there may be some more open minds for new evidence, and that still may be the case.” 

Before we hung up, I asked Grisham if he was hopeful that Roberson’s case, which has attracted the attention of so many, including him, would change the conversation around the death penalty. “I’m a little skeptical,” I said. “There are fewer death verdicts every year, fewer executions every year. The death penalty is dying, a slow death in America, not because of courageous judges or courageous congressmen, courageous lawmakers, but because of courageous jurors,” said Grisham, who wrote the 2010 novel _The Confession_, which imagines what would happen if proof came that an innocent man had been executed. “Jurors are saying, ‘Show us the whole picture. Let us know where this person came from, even though he’s a convicted murderer. So they’re allowed to show more sympathy, and they’re they’re using not death, but life without parole, and that’s appropriate in many of these cases.”

Today came, and once again, Roberson’s case was not on the appellate court’s list of opinions and hearings. However, it was not yet denied, which means that hope—albeit ever-shrinking—is still something Roberson and his team can hold on to.

Twenty-nine days and counting.

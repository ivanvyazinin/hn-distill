# Categorical Foundations for CuTe Layouts

In GPU programming, performance depends critically on how data is stored and accessed in memory. While the data we care about is typically multi-dimensional, the GPU’s memory is fundamentally one-dimensional. This means that when we want to load, store, or otherwise manipulate data, we need to map its multi-dimensional logical coordinates to one-dimensional physical coordinates. This mapping, known as a **layout**, is essential for reading from and writing to memory correctly and efficiently. Moreover, with respect to the GPU’s SIMT execution model, layouts are used to describe and manipulate partitionings of threads over data. This is important to ensure optimized memory access patterns and correct invocation of specialized hardware instructions, such as those used to target tensor cores.

CUTLASS pioneered a novel approach to layouts that both features shape and stride tuples of arbitrary nesting and depth, and a “layout algebra” formed out of certain fundamental operations, such as composition, complementation, logical division and logical product. These **CuTe layouts** are incredibly expressive, allowing one to describe partitioning patterns for all generations of tensor core instructions, for example. They are also fascinating from a mathematical perspective, since they feature an unusual and subtle notion of function composition that demands a theoretical explanation.

In a new paper, we develop a robust mathematical theory underlying this approach, connecting CuTe layouts and their algebra to the theory of **categories** and **operads** and developing a new graphical calculus of layout diagrams for computing their operations. A pdf copy of our paper is linked [here](https://research.colfax-intl.com/download/categories-of-layouts/), and the companion git repository may be found [here](https://github.com/ColfaxResearch/layout-categories).

![](/wp-content/uploads/2023/10/PDF_32.png) [Categories of Layouts](https://research.colfax-intl.com/download/categories-of-layouts/?tmstv=1758579684)

In this blog post, we give a concise overview of the main ideas and results of our paper, referring the reader to the paper for a comprehensive treatment, many worked examples, and proofs. To state our results, we assume the reader knows the basic idea of a [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_\(mathematics\)) and a [functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functor); cf. Appendix A of our paper for a quick introduction. We also assume that the reader is familiar with the basics of CuTe layouts, which we henceforth simply refer to as layouts.

## Tractable layouts

While defining and computing layout operations for arbitrary layouts is difficult, we can develop an intuitive framework for working with layouts by restricting to **tractable layouts**. These include almost all layouts one encounters in practice, such as

*   **row-major** and **column-major** layouts, which are ubiquitous,
*   **compact** layouts, which store data in consecutive memory addresses,
*   **projections**, which broadcast multiple copies of data, and
*   **dilations**, which enable padded loads and stores.

For now, let’s focus on the **flat** case, where the shape and stride of our layouts are tuples ![ (x_1, \ldots, x_n) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%28x_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+x_n%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), rather than more general nested tuples. Throughout this article, we always suppose that shape tuples are comprised of positive integers and stride tuples are comprised of non-negative integers.

First, we define an ordering ![ \preceq ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Cpreceq+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) on integer pairs ![ s : d ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+s+%3A+d+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) by

![ s : d \preceq s' : d' \quad \text{ if and only if } \quad d < d' \: \text{ or } \: d = d' \text{ and } s \leq s'.](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+s+%3A+d+%5Cpreceq+s%27+%3A+d%27+%5Cquad+%5Ctext%7B+if+and+only+if+%7D+%5Cquad+d+%3C+d%27+%5C%3A+%5Ctext%7B+or+%7D+%5C%3A+d+%3D+d%27+%5Ctext%7B+and+%7D+s+%5Cleq+s%27.&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

**Definition:** We say that a flat layout

![ L = (s_1, \ldots, s_m) : (d_1, \ldots, d_m) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L+%3D+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29+%3A+%28d_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+d_m%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

is **_tractable_** if for all pairs of integers ![ 1 \leq i, j  \leq m ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+1+%5Cleq+i%2C+j++%5Cleq+m+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), the following condition holds:

![ \text{if  } s_i : d_ i \preceq s_j : d_j \text{  and  } d_i, d_j \neq 0, \text{  then  } s_i d_i \text{  divides  } d_j. ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Ctext%7Bif++%7D+s_i+%3A+d_+i+%5Cpreceq+s_j+%3A+d_j+%5Ctext%7B++and++%7D+d_i%2C+d_j+%5Cneq+0%2C+%5Ctext%7B++then++%7D+s_i+d_i+%5Ctext%7B++divides++%7D+d_j.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

If _L_ is tractable, then _L_ can be encoded by a **diagram.** For example,

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/tractable-layout.png?resize=920%2C540&ssl=1)

The shape of the encoded layout is the tuple on the left, and the stride of the encoded layout is determined by the arrows and the tuple on the right by taking prefix products:

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/layout_diagram.png?resize=960%2C461&ssl=1)

These diagrams are visual depictions of **morphisms** in a category **Tuple**:

**Definition:** Let **Tuple** denote the category in which

1.  an object is a tuple ![(s_1, \ldots, s_m) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) of positive integers, and
2.  a morphism ![ f : (s_1, \ldots, s_m) \to (t_1, \ldots, t_n) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+f+%3A+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29+%5Cto+%28t_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+t_n%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is specified by a map of finite pointed sets

![ \alpha: \{ \ast, 1, \ldots, m \} \to \{ \ast, 1, \ldots, n\} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Calpha%3A+%5C%7B+%5Cast%2C+1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+m+%5C%7D+%5Cto+%5C%7B+%5Cast%2C+1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+n%5C%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

subject to the conditions

1.  ![\alpha(*) = *](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%28%2A%29+%3D+%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002),
2.  if ![\alpha(i) \neq *](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%28i%29+%5Cneq+%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) and ![\alpha(i) = \alpha(i')](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+%5Calpha%28i%27%29&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), then ![i = i'](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=i+%3D+i%27&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002),
3.  if ![\alpha(i) = j \neq *](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+j+%5Cneq+%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), then ![s_i = t_j](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=s_i+%3D+t_j&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002).

We say such a morphism _f_ lies over _α_, and refer to _f_ as a _**tuple morphism**_.

Each of the previously depicted diagrams was obtained from a tuple morphism _f_ by depicting the domain ![ (s_1, \ldots, s_m) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) and codomain ![(t_1, \ldots, t_n)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28t_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+t_n%29&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) of f vertically, and drawing an arrow from ![s_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=s_i&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) to ![t_j](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=t_j&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) if ![ \alpha(i) = j ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+j+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002). We can now give a precise definition of the layout encoded by a tuple morphism.

**Definition:** If _f_ is a tuple morphism, then the **_layout encoded by_** _f_ is the layout

![ L_f = (s_1, \ldots, s_m) : (d_1, \ldots, d_m) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_f+%3D+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29+%3A+%28d_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+d_m%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

whose shape is the domain of _f_, and whose stride is given by

![ d_i = \begin{cases} t_1 \cdots t_{j-1} & \text{if } \alpha(i) = j \\ 0 & \text{if } \alpha(i) = *. \end{cases} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+d_i+%3D+%5Cbegin%7Bcases%7D+t_1+%5Ccdots+t_%7Bj-1%7D+%26+%5Ctext%7Bif+%7D+%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+j+%5C%5C+0+%26+%5Ctext%7Bif+%7D+%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+%2A.+%5Cend%7Bcases%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

An important subtlety is that there are many different tuple morphisms which encode the same layout. For example, each of the tuple morphisms

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/layout-diagrams3.png?resize=960%2C249&ssl=1)

encodes the layout _L_ = (4, 5) : (1, 64). The morphism _f_ is clearly the simplest: it does not contain superfluous entries (like the “7” in _g_), and the entries not hit by _f_ are consolidated (unlike the “2” and “8” in _h_). When a morphism _f_ satisfies these properties, we say _f_ has **standard form**.

If we further assume that our layouts _L_ and morphisms _f_ are **non-degenerate,** which corresponds to the conditions

![s_i = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad d_i = 0, ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=s_i+%3D+1+%5Cquad+%5CRightarrow+%5Cquad+d_i+%3D+0%2C+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

![s_i = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \alpha(*) = *, ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=s_i+%3D+1+%5Cquad+%5CRightarrow+%5Cquad+%5Calpha%28%2A%29+%3D+%2A%2C+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

on the layout side and morphism side, respectively, then we can prove the following correspondence theorem.

**Theorem:** There is a one-to-one correspondence between **non-degenerate tractable flat layouts** and **non-degenerate tuple morphisms of standard form**.

If _L_ is a non-degenerate tractable layout, we write ![f_L](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f_L&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) for the tuple morphism corresponding to _L_, and refer to ![f_L](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f_L&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) as the **standard representation** of _L_.

## Layout functions and the realization functor

The most important invariant of a layout L is its **layout function** ![\Phi_L](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5CPhi_L&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002). When L is tractable, its layout function arises naturally from the category **Tuple** through a **realization functor**

![ | \cdot | : \textbf{Tuple} \to \textbf{FinSet} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%7C+%5Ccdot+%7C+%3A+%5Ctextbf%7BTuple%7D+%5Cto+%5Ctextbf%7BFinSet%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

Let’s recall the definition of layout functions. In order to do so, we must first recall the definition of **colexicographic isomorphisms** and their inverses. It will be convenient to use the notation \[0, N) = {0, 1, …, N – 1}.

**Definition**: If ![ S = (s_1, \ldots, s_m) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+S+%3D+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is a tuple of positive integers of size M, then the **_colexicographic isomorphism_** is the function

![ \mathrm{colex}_S: [0, s_1) \times \cdots \times [0, s_m) \to [0, M) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Cmathrm%7Bcolex%7D_S%3A+%5B0%2C+s_1%29+%5Ctimes+%5Ccdots+%5Ctimes+%5B0%2C+s_m%29+%5Cto+%5B0%2C+M%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

given by

![ \mathrm{colex}_S(x_1, \ldots, x_m) = \sum_{i=1}^m x_i \cdot s_1 \cdots s_{i-1}. ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Cmathrm%7Bcolex%7D_S%28x_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+x_m%29+%3D+%5Csum_%7Bi%3D1%7D%5Em+x_i+%5Ccdot+s_1+%5Ccdots+s_%7Bi-1%7D.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

The **_inverse colexicographic isomorphism_** is the function

![ \mathrm{colex}_S^{-1}: [0, M) \to [0, s_1) \times \cdots \times [0, s_m) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Cmathrm%7Bcolex%7D_S%5E%7B-1%7D%3A+%5B0%2C+M%29+%5Cto+%5B0%2C+s_1%29+%5Ctimes+%5Ccdots+%5Ctimes+%5B0%2C+s_m%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

given by

![ \mathrm{colex}_S^{-1}(x) = (x_1, \ldots, x_m) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Cmathrm%7Bcolex%7D_S%5E%7B-1%7D%28x%29+%3D+%28x_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+x_m%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

where

![ x_i = \lfloor x / (s_1 \cdots s_{i-1} ) \rfloor \: \mod s_1 \cdots s_i. ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+x_i+%3D+%5Clfloor+x+%2F+%28s_1+%5Ccdots+s_%7Bi-1%7D+%29+%5Crfloor+%5C%3A+%5Cmod+s_1+%5Ccdots+s_i.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

When L is tractable, we can recover its layout function by means of a **realization functor** from **Tuple** to the category of finite sets.

**Theorem:** There is a functor

![ | \cdot | : \textbf{Tuple} \to \textbf{FinSet}, ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%7C+%5Ccdot+%7C+%3A+%5Ctextbf%7BTuple%7D+%5Cto+%5Ctextbf%7BFinSet%7D%2C+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

which we call _realization_, satisfying the following properties:

1.  If S is a tuple of size M, then |S| = \[0, M).
2.  If S and T are tuples of size M and N, respectively, and ![f: S \to T](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f%3A+S+%5Cto+T&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is a tuple morphism, then its realization ![ |f|: [0, M) \to [0, N) \subset \mathbb{Z} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%7Cf%7C%3A+%5B0%2C+M%29+%5Cto+%5B0%2C+N%29+%5Csubset+%5Cmathbb%7BZ%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is the layout function of ![ L_f ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_f+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002).

In particular, this result provides an easy proof that the composition of tuple morphisms is compatible with composition of layouts, which we will discuss below.

## Layout Operations

Many important layout operations, such as coalesce, complement, and composition, have analogues in the category **Tuple**. Let’s take a closer look at each of these operations.

### Coalesce

If ![L = (s_1, \ldots, s_m) : (d_1, \ldots, d_m)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=L+%3D+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29+%3A+%28d_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+d_m%29&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is a layout, then we can coalesce _L_ by iteratively replacing any instance of

![ (\ldots, s_i, s_{i+1}, \ldots) : (\ldots, d_i, s_i d_i, \ldots) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%28%5Cldots%2C+s_i%2C+s_%7Bi%2B1%7D%2C+%5Cldots%29+%3A+%28%5Cldots%2C+d_i%2C+s_i+d_i%2C+%5Cldots%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

with

![ (\ldots, s_i s_{i+1}, \ldots) : (\ldots, d_i, \ldots) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%28%5Cldots%2C+s_i+s_%7Bi%2B1%7D%2C+%5Cldots%29+%3A+%28%5Cldots%2C+d_i%2C+%5Cldots%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

We denote the resulting layout by _coal_(_L_). For example, if

![ L = (2, 2, 5, 5, 5) : (1, 2, 8, 40, 200) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L+%3D+%282%2C+2%2C+5%2C+5%2C+5%29+%3A+%281%2C+2%2C+8%2C+40%2C+200%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

then

![ \mathit{coal}(L) = (4, 125) : (1, 8). ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Cmathit%7Bcoal%7D%28L%29+%3D+%284%2C+125%29+%3A+%281%2C+8%29.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

This construction has a direct analogue in the category **Tuple**. If _f_ is a tuple morphism, then we can coalesce _f_ by collapsing parallel arrows, and multiplying the corresponding entries. For example:

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/coalesce.png?resize=884%2C404&ssl=1)

We prove that the coalesce operation in **Tuple** is compatible with layout coalesce.

**Theorem:** If _f_ is a tuple morphism, then the layout encoded by _coal_(_f_) is

![ L_{\mathit{coal}(f)} = \mathit{coal}(L_f). ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_%7B%5Cmathit%7Bcoal%7D%28f%29%7D+%3D+%5Cmathit%7Bcoal%7D%28L_f%29.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

### Complement

If _L_ is a layout and _N_ is a positive integer, then _comp_(_L_, _N_) is a sorted, coalesced layout whose concatenation with _L_ is compact. This means that the layout function of the concatenation is an isomorphism onto its image. There is a minimal integer _N_ with respect to which _L_ admits a complement, and in this case, we write _comp_(_L_) = _comp_(_L_, _N_). For example, if

![ L = (2,2,2):(1,10,60) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L+%3D+%282%2C2%2C2%29%3A%281%2C10%2C60%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

then

![ comp(L) = (5, 3) : (2, 20). ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+comp%28L%29+%3D+%285%2C+3%29+%3A+%282%2C+20%29.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

Again, there is an analogue of complements in the category **Tuple**. We can compute the complement ![f^c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f%5Ec&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) of a tuple morphism _f_ by including the entries not hit by _f_. For example,

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/complement.png?resize=960%2C345&ssl=1)

We prove that complements in the category **Tuple** are compatible with layout complements.

**Theorem:** If _f_ is an injective tuple morphism of standard form, then

![ L_{f^c} = \mathit{comp}(L_f). ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_%7Bf%5Ec%7D+%3D+%5Cmathit%7Bcomp%7D%28L_f%29.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

### Composition

If A and B are layouts, then the **composition** ![ B \circ A ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+B+%5Ccirc+A+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is a layout such that for any ![ x \in [0, size(B \circ A)) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+x+%5Cin+%5B0%2C+size%28B+%5Ccirc+A%29%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), we have

![ \Phi_{B \circ A}(x) = \Phi_B ( \Phi_A(x)). ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5CPhi_%7BB+%5Ccirc+A%7D%28x%29+%3D+%5CPhi_B+%28+%5CPhi_A%28x%29%29.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

There are other properties which uniquely characterize the layout ![B \circ A ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=B+%5Ccirc+A+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), and we refer the reader to Definition 2.3.7.1 of the paper for full details. For example, if _A_ = (2, 2) : (5, 50) and _B_ = (5, 2, 5, 2) : (1, 25, 5, 50), then the composition of A and B is (2, 2) : (25, 50).

If _f_ and _g_ are tuple morphisms with _codomain_(_f_) = _domain_(_g_), then we can compose _f_ and _g_ to form the tuple morphism ![g \circ f](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=g+%5Ccirc+f&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002). For example, the tuple morphisms

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/composition1.png?resize=774%2C337&ssl=1)

are composable, and their composite is as depicted below.

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/composition2alt.png?resize=960%2C301&ssl=1)

We prove that composition in the category **Tuple** is compatible with layout composition.

**Theorem:** If _f_ and _g_ are composable tuple morphisms, then

![ L_{g \circ f} = L_g \circ L_f. ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_%7Bg+%5Ccirc+f%7D+%3D+L_g+%5Ccirc+L_f.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

This theorem provides a tool for computing the composition of flat, tractable layouts _A_ and _B_. Namely, we can take the standard representations ![ f = f_A ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+f+%3D+f_A+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) and ![g = f_B ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=g+%3D+f_B+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) of _A_ and _B_, and if these morphisms happen to be composable, we can obtain the composite of _A_ and _B_ via the formula

![ B \circ A = L_{g \circ f}. ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+B+%5Ccirc+A+%3D+L_%7Bg+%5Ccirc+f%7D.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

However, it is not often the case that the standard representations _f_ and _g_ of some arbitrarily chosen tractable layouts _A_ and _B_ are composable, even if the layouts themselves are. Instead, we may aim to compute the composition of _A_ and _B_ by

1.  starting with the standard representations _f_ and _g_ of _A_ and _B_,
2.  modifying _f_ and _g_ to obtain composable morphisms _f’_ and _g’_ which realize to the same layout functions as for _f_ and _g_, and
3.  composing _f’_ and _g’_ to obtain the morphism ![g' \circ f'](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=g%27+%5Ccirc+f%27&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), which encodes ![B \circ A](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=B+%5Ccirc+A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002).

In order to make this procedure both rigorous and general, we must broaden our scope to consider _nested_ or _hierarchical_ layouts.

## Nested layouts and nested tuple morphisms

Let’s fix some notation and terminology. A **_profile_** is a nested tuple each of whose entries is the symbol ∗. For example _P_ = (∗, (∗, ∗)) and _Q_ = ((∗, ∗), ∗, (∗, ∗)) are profiles. A **_nested tuple_** _S_ is uniquely determined by its **_flattening_** ![(s_1, \ldots, s_m)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), which is an ordinary tuple, and its profile _P_. It is convenient to write

![ S = (s_1, \ldots s_m)_P ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+S+%3D+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots+s_m%29_P+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

when working with nested tuples. For example, if _S_ = ((2, 2), (5, 5)), then we can write

![ S = (2, 2, 5, 5)_P ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+S+%3D+%282%2C+2%2C+5%2C+5%29_P+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

where _P_ = ((∗, ∗), (∗, ∗)). If _L_ = _S_ : _D_ is a layout, then _S_ and _D_ are required to have the same profile, so we may write a general layout as

![ L = (s_1, \ldots, s_m)_P : (d_1, \ldots, d_m)_P. ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L+%3D+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29_P+%3A+%28d_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+d_m%29_P.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

We refer to the layout

![ L^\flat = (s_1, \ldots, s_m) : (d_1, \ldots, d_m) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L%5E%5Cflat+%3D+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29+%3A+%28d_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+d_m%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

as the **_flattening_** of L. Much of our story about flat layouts may be easily ported to the nested case.

**Definition:** We say a layout _L_ is **_tractable_** if its flattening _L_♭ is tractable.

If L is a tractable, then L can be encoded by a **diagram.** For example,

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/nested_layouts.png?resize=960%2C464&ssl=1)

These diagrams represent morphisms in a category **Nest**.

**Definition:** Let **Nest** denote the category in which

1.  an object is a nested tuple ![(s_1, \ldots, s_m)_P ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29_P+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) of positive integers, and
2.  a morphism ![ f : (s_1, \ldots, s_m)_P \to (t_1, \ldots, t_n)_Q ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+f+%3A+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29_P+%5Cto+%28t_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+t_n%29_Q+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is specified by a map of finite pointed sets

![ \alpha: \{ \ast, 1, \ldots, m \} \to \{ \ast, 1, \ldots, n\} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Calpha%3A+%5C%7B+%5Cast%2C+1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+m+%5C%7D+%5Cto+%5C%7B+%5Cast%2C+1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+n%5C%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

satisfying the same conditions above as for a tuple morphism, namely:

1.  ![\alpha(*) = *](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%28%2A%29+%3D+%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002),
2.  if ![\alpha(i) \neq *](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%28i%29+%5Cneq+%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) and ![\alpha(i) = \alpha(i')](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+%5Calpha%28i%27%29&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), then ![i = i'](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=i+%3D+i%27&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002),
3.  if ![\alpha(i) = j \neq *](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+j+%5Cneq+%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002), then ![s_i = t_j](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=s_i+%3D+t_j&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002).

We say such a morphism _f_ lies over _α_, and refer to _f_ as a **_nested tuple morphism._**

**Definition:** If f is a nested tuple morphism, then the **_layout encoded by_** f is the layout

![ L_f = (s_1, \ldots, s_m)_P : (d_1, \ldots, d_m)_P ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_f+%3D+%28s_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+s_m%29_P+%3A+%28d_1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+d_m%29_P+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

whose shape is the domain of _f_, and whose stride _entries_ are given by

![ d_i = \begin{cases} t_1 \cdots t_{j-1} & \text{if } \alpha(i) = j \\ 0 & \text{if } \alpha(i) = *. \end{cases} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+d_i+%3D+%5Cbegin%7Bcases%7D+t_1+%5Ccdots+t_%7Bj-1%7D+%26+%5Ctext%7Bif+%7D+%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+j+%5C%5C+0+%26+%5Ctext%7Bif+%7D+%5Calpha%28i%29+%3D+%2A.+%5Cend%7Bcases%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

We can define **standard form** and **non-degeneracy** in the nested case, and we again have a correspondence theorem.

**Theorem:** There is a one-to-one correspondence between **non-degenerate tractable layouts** and **non-degenerate nested tuple morphisms of standard form**.

We can compare the categories **Nest** and **Tuple** through the flattening functor

![ (-)^\flat : \mathbf{Nest} \to \mathbf{Tuple}. ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%28-%29%5E%5Cflat+%3A+%5Cmathbf%7BNest%7D+%5Cto+%5Cmathbf%7BTuple%7D.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

In particular, we can postcompose with the realization functor from **Tuple** to **FinSet** to obtain a realization functor 

![ | \cdot | : \textbf{Nest} \to \textbf{FinSet} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%7C+%5Ccdot+%7C+%3A+%5Ctextbf%7BNest%7D+%5Cto+%5Ctextbf%7BFinSet%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

defined on **Nest**, such that it enjoys the same properties as before.

**Theorem:** The realization functor from **Nest** to **FinSet** satisfies the following properties:

1.  If _S_ is a nested tuple of size _M_, then |_S_| = \[0, _M_).
2.  If _S_ and _T_ are nested tuples of size _M_ and _N_, respectively, and ![f : S \to T ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f+%3A+S+%5Cto+T+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is a tuple morphism, then the realization ![|f| : [0, M) \to [0, N) \subset \mathbf{Z} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%7Cf%7C+%3A+%5B0%2C+M%29+%5Cto+%5B0%2C+N%29+%5Csubset+%5Cmathbf%7BZ%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) is the layout function of ![ L_f ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_f+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002).

In particular, this theorem leads to an easy proof of the following result:

**Theorem:** If _f_ and _g_ are composable nested tuple morphisms, then

![ L_{g \circ f} = L_g \circ L_f ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_%7Bg+%5Ccirc+f%7D+%3D+L_g+%5Ccirc+L_f+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

The category **Nest** supports analogues of many important layout operations such as coalesce, complement, logical division, and logical product. We summarize these operations and their compatibility with the corresponding layout operations below.

**Theorem:**

1.  We define a coalesce operation _coal(f)_ on nested tuple morphisms, which is compatible with layout coalesce, in that

![ L_{\mathit{coal}(f)} = \mathit{coal}(L_f). ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_%7B%5Cmathit%7Bcoal%7D%28f%29%7D+%3D+%5Cmathit%7Bcoal%7D%28L_f%29.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

2.  We define a complement operation ![f^c](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f%5Ec&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) on nested tuple morphisms, which is compatible with layout complements in that if _f_ is an injective nested tuple morphism of standard form, then

![ L_{f^c} = \mathit{comp}(L_f) ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_%7Bf%5Ec%7D+%3D+%5Cmathit%7Bcomp%7D%28L_f%29+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

3.  We define a notion of _divisibility_ of nested tuple morphisms, and a logical division operation ![f \oslash g](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f+%5Coslash+g&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) when _g_ divides _f_. This operation is compatible with logical division of layouts, in that

![ \mathit{coal}(L_{f \oslash g}) = \mathit{coal}(L_f \oslash L_g). ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Cmathit%7Bcoal%7D%28L_%7Bf+%5Coslash+g%7D%29+%3D+%5Cmathit%7Bcoal%7D%28L_f+%5Coslash+L_g%29.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

4.  We define a notion of _product admissibility_ for nested tuple morphisms, and a logical product operation ![f \otimes g](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f+%5Cotimes+g&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) when _f_ and _g_ are product admissible. This operation is compatible with logical products of layouts, in that

![ L_{f \otimes g} = L_f \otimes L_g. ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+L_%7Bf+%5Cotimes+g%7D+%3D+L_f+%5Cotimes+L_g.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

## The composition algorithm

Now that we have generalized our story to the nested case, we can explain our **composition algorithm** which computes the composition ![B \circ A](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=B+%5Ccirc+A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) of tractable layouts _A_ and _B_ using our categorical framework. There are several important constructions used in our algorithm which we have not already discussed, namely **mutual refinements**, **pullbacks**, and **pushforwards**. We will explain these concepts in the context of our example, and refer readers to sections 4.1.2 and 4.1.3 of the paper for full details.

Suppose we want to compute the composition of the layouts _A_ = (6, 6) : (1, 6) and _B_ = (12, 3, 6) : (1, 72, 12). Since _A_ and _B_ are tractable, we may represent them with the tuple morphisms

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/AB_to_fg.png?resize=934%2C264&ssl=1)

These morphisms are not composable, since the codomain (6, 6) of _f_ is not equal to the domain (12, 3, 6) of _g_. This means that we can not use the morphisms _f_ and _g_ to compute the composite ![B \circ A](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=B+%5Ccirc+A&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) directly. We can, however, proceed with our computation by finding a **mutual refinement** of (6, 6) and (12, 3, 6), as depicted below

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fg_refine1.png?resize=412%2C199&ssl=1)

Intuitively, such a mutual refinement is a specification of how we can break up the codomain of _f_ and the domain of _g_ in a compatible way. We can use our mutual refinement to convert _f_ and _g_ into composable morphisms _f’_ and _g’_. In the case of _f_, our mutual refinement indicates that we should factor the first 6 into (2, 3), and include an extra 6 in the codomain of _f_:

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/modify_f.png?resize=960%2C273&ssl=1)

Rigorously, the construction of _f’_ from _f_ is an instance of a **pullback**.

In the case of _g_, our mutual refinement indicates that we should factor 12 as (6, 2):

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/modify_g-1.png?resize=960%2C250&ssl=1)

Rigorously, the construction of g’ from g is an instance of a **pushforward**.

The nested tuple morphisms _f’_ and _g’_ are composable, so we may form the composite

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/compose_fgmod2.png?resize=960%2C209&ssl=1)

and computing the encoded layout yields

![ B \circ A = L_{g' \circ f'} = ((2, 3), 6) : ((6, 72), 1). ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+B+%5Ccirc+A+%3D+L_%7Bg%27+%5Ccirc+f%27%7D+%3D+%28%282%2C+3%29%2C+6%29+%3A+%28%286%2C+72%29%2C+1%29.+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002)

We have walked through an example of the composition algorithm in action. We refer the reader to section 4.1.3 of the paper for a full and precise description of this algorithm, and to section 4.1.4 for further examples. We would also like to emphasize that since **logical division** and **logical products** are defined in terms of composition, this algorithm may be used to compute these operations as well.

## Connections to the theory of operads

As we hinted at in the introduction, there are some interesting connections between the theory of layouts that we have developed and the theory of operads. Explaining them is not at all necessary to understand or use our results, but they are of independent mathematical interest, and at any rate served to guide our approach to the subject. In this final section, oriented towards mathematicians rather than the working programmer, we tersely discuss some of these connections.

We first describe how the category **Tuple** naturally occurs as a subcategory of the [categories of operators](https://ncatlab.org/nlab/show/category+of+operators) of an operad. We then introduce the operad of profiles and propose an alternative definition of a category of nested tuples that builds in refinements as “backwards” morphisms, which contextualizes many of the maneuvers around refinement done in the composition algorithm. Following common practice, we identify operads with their category of operators, e.g., the commutative operad is the category of finite pointed sets.

Consider the partially ordered set **ℤ**\>0 of positive integers under the relation of divisibility: _a_ ≤ _b_ if and only if _a_ divides _b_. Like with every poset, we have an associated category whose objects are the set’s elements and where _a_ **→** _b_ if and only if _a_ ≤ _b_, and by abuse of notation we also denote this category as **ℤ**\>0. Now, consider this category as a symmetric monoidal category under the operation of multiplication and apply the operadic nerve to produce the operad **ℤ**\>0⊗, which comes equipped with a structure functor to the category of finite pointed sets. (For a reference on the operadic nerve, see Construction 2.1.1.7 in [Higher Algebra](https://www.math.ias.edu/~lurie/papers/HA.pdf).) We have the wide subcategory E0⊗ of finite pointed sets on those maps injective away from the basepoint, which is the operad encoding a single unary operation. Then, the pullback of **ℤ**\>0 to E0⊗ identifies with the definition of **Tuple** excluding condition 2c), and imposing 2c) defines **Tuple** as a subcategory of this pullback.

From this perspective, how do we then incorporate profiles? Profiles themselves form a (single-colored, symmetric) operad, whose set of _n_\-ary operations consists of profiles of length _n_, and which we endow with trivial symmetric group action. Under the operadic nerve, denote this operad as P⊗. One can then consider profiles with labels in any symmetric monoidal category C⊗ by forming the pullback of operads; for **ℤ**\>0⊗, denote the resulting pullback as P**ℤ**\>0⊗. Then, considering depth 1 profiles, we also get that **Tuple** is a subcategory of the pullback of P**ℤ**\>0⊗ over E0⊗ (and indeed, of P**ℤ**\>0⊗ itself).

Moreover, since P**ℤ**\>0⊗ contains both tuple morphisms and refinements (because multiplying integers was the monoidal product), it is a suitable ambient category in which to make more sophisticated constructions. Specifically, as we saw with the diagrams appearing in the composition algorithm, it is natural to consider factorizations followed by tuple morphisms as themselves literally comprising morphisms in some category. There is a standard construction in category theory that can do this for us; namely, we can form a certain [category of spans](https://ncatlab.org/nlab/show/span) in P**ℤ**\>0⊗, where the class of forward morphisms are those in the wide subcategory **Tuple** and the class of backward morphisms **Ref** consists of the [cocartesian edges](https://ncatlab.org/nlab/show/Cartesian+morphism) over those maps ![ \alpha: \{ \ast, 1, \ldots, m \} \to \{ \ast, 1, \ldots, n\} ](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=+%5Calpha%3A+%5C%7B+%5Cast%2C+1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+m+%5C%7D+%5Cto+%5C%7B+%5Cast%2C+1%2C+%5Cldots%2C+n%5C%7D+&bg=ffffff&fg=000&s=0&c=20201002) of finite pointed sets such that

1.  _α_ is **active**: if _α_(i) = ∗, then i = ∗.
2.  _α_ is **surjective**.
3.  _α_ is **non-decreasing** when restricted to {1, …, m}.

Here, the point of taking cocartesian edges is to consider e.g. maps (a, b) **→** c where ab = c instead of the general case of ab dividing c (i.e., we get exactly the refinements).

Note that for the span construction to be well-defined, we need to check that one can form pullbacks of morphisms in **Tuple** along those in **Ref** in P**ℤ**\>0⊗. However, one can prove this.

Finally, we denote the resulting category of spans as **Span(Tuple, Ref)**. A typical morphism in this category looks like

![](https://i0.wp.com/research.colfax-intl.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/span_morphism.png?resize=522%2C253&ssl=1)

where, following standard notation for span diagrams, we have now drawn the left grouping of arrows to have the arrows pointing backwards.

By definition, **Span(Tuple, Ref)** contains **Tuple** and **Refop** as subcategories. We can then extend the realization functor from **Tuple** to **FinSet** over **Span(Tuple, Ref)** so that refinements are sent to _inverse_ colexicographic isomorphisms. Conceptually, this provides an alternative viewpoint on a category of nested tuples since, in contrast to **Nest**, the _objects_ of the category **Span(Tuple, Ref)** are flat tuples, but morphisms are nested; this is concordant with viewing a layout as defining a map valued on the depth 1 reduction of its shape.

## [Apple vs. Facebook is Kayfabe](/2025/08/apple-vs-fb-kayfabe/)

### How do we know? In-app Browsers.

August 25, 2025

_This is part of the series ["Browser Choice Must Matter"](/series/browser-choice-must-matter)_

![Photo by Claudia Raya](/2025/08/apple-vs-fb-kayfabe/claudia-raya-wrestling-unsplash.avif) 

_[Photo by Claudia Raya](https://unsplash.com/photos/a-group-of-people-in-a-wrestling-ring-1euWXa-SEKw)_

Apple vs. Facebook is, and always was, [kayfabe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kayfabe). In reality, Apple is Facebook's chauffeur; holding Zuck's coat while Facebook[1](#fn-not-calling-it-that-1) wantonly surveils iPhones owners.[2](#fn-kayfabe-for-now-2)

[![Facebook's gross profit over time.](/2025/08/apple-vs-fb-kayfabe/fb-profits-historical-chart.png)](https://m.macrotrends.net/stocks/charts/META/meta-platforms/gross-profit)

Facebook's gross profit over time.

While Facebook and Apple mugged for the cameras as ["App Tracking Transparency"](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/apptrackingtransparency) rolled out, Facebook's income tells a very different story. [Net income](https://www.macrotrends.net/stocks/charts/META/meta-platforms/net-income) dipped between late 2021 and early 2023 thanks to [accelerating capital expenditures](https://finbox.com/SWX:FB/explorer/capex/) in other lines of business, not reductions in revenue. Despite strenuous efforts to sell the move, it's hard to discern any impact from ATT whatsoever.

How can we be sure Apple's wise? Because Cupertino continues to allow Facebook's wide-scale abuse of [In-App Browsers:](https://open-web-advocacy.org/blog/in-app-browsers-the-worst-erosion-of-user-choice-you-havent-heard-of/)

[Open Web Advocacy - In-App Browser Primer](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-6mFC__dMWM)

Apple has long facilitated and enriched mass surveillance through native apps, both directly from in-app activity, but much more insidiously through the In-App Browsers that Facebook tacks onto every link you tap.

[I have written about them before](/2021/07/hobsons-browser/), and they still stink to high heavens.

In-App Browsers are, for Facebook's purposes, _ad-blocker blockers_. Cheat codes for the enterprising panopticon proprietor. [Much wringing of hands transpires](https://krausefx.com/blog/ios-privacy-instagram-and-facebook-can-track-anything-you-do-on-any-website-in-their-in-app-browser) every time one of these knockoff browsers is caught (or suspected) of injecting script into web pages. The fear is that this will enable a level of tracking by apps that is not otherwise possible.

As scary (and real) as the threat is, it is also a misdirection.

To effect total surveillance, Facebook and ByteDance and don't need to inject scripts into the runtime, they only need your browser not to block their "ad tags" that are _already_ embedded in every high-traffic page on the internet. Combined with [the ability to watch every URL you navigate to in a WebView](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/webkit/wknavigationdelegate), this is more than enough to correlate in-app activity with web browsing without leaving overt fingerprints.

As long as users remain in the Native App Upside Down, data collected from tracking endpoints remains immeasurably richer. It is, in effect, a loophole that only requires users be denied access to their browser of choice whenever it is convenient for native apps.

Real browsers matter because they are _user agents_; they [represent the interests of users,](https://www.w3.org/TR/design-principles/#priority-of-constituencies) rather than ad networks (Facebook, Google, ByteDance) and the OS vendors that are desperate to keep apps from decamping to portable, interoperable alternatives. Users configure their browsers with privacy and security settings that synchronize between devices, and expand those protections with extensions that might prevent unwanted snooping.

By contrast, IABs from Facebook and ByteDance (etc.) do not feature most privacy-preserving settings or extensions, are fiendishly hard to disable, and do not sync between devices in the same app. They don't even synchronise preferences _between multiple apps from the same company on the same device_.[3](#fn-the-biggest-tell-3)

Users that install browsers, configure them to preserve their privacy, then use Facebook app's after selecting _"Ask App Not to Track"_ are reliably sold up the river by Apple, who seem content to facilitate this end-run around the (nominal) rules.

Privacy settings? Gone. Extensions? Poof! And because Facebook has code on every top site, the tracking suddenly roars back to life, out of band of the channels that Apple wants you to believe are important (or at least that it has taken heat for in the past).

FB's tracking is so pervasive in modern web pages that it doesn't need to exfiltrate data from the IAB to track you. It just needs to keep you away from your real browser, where it might not be able to join up clicks/taps.

It isn't exactly clear if the IAB is the basis for [recent reports of secretive "deterministic matching"](https://on.ft.com/4lIHyJ6) efforts at Facebook, but it's safe to assume that Facebook's bullheaded determination to steal clicks and deny users their choice in browsers isn't simply an oversight.

## [Apple Knows](#apple-knows)

None of this is news to Apple. [They have read the posts](/2021/07/hobsons-browser/#minimal-interventions) and have [made timid interventions](https://webkit.org/blog/10882/app-bound-domains/) to avoid being blamed for the most obviously nasty versions of IAB tracking. But acting against the deep rot? No joy.

Not only has Apple not responded to [advocacy on behalf of users](https://open-web-advocacy.org/blog/in-app-browsers-the-worst-erosion-of-user-choice-you-havent-heard-of/) from groups like [OWA](https://open-web-advocacy.org/), it has failed to impose either common-sense, pro-privacy restrictions on IABs, or to support action by regulators. As we've seen above, it doesn't even require apps to avoid privacy-degrading IABs when ATT is enabled.

And Apple is absolutely aware of these concerns because they have been raised publicly and in regulatory circles for years. Yet it does not act.

Why?

Facebook's dark patterns are directly facilitated by Apple and Google. It is their SDKs and policies that make this not only possible, but pervasive. So why do they deliver users unto perdition?

Denying users true browser choice helps keep the big app vendors in the store. Those big vendors understand that native APIs offer increases in data they can collect and monetize, increasing their profits.

Keeping big fish in the store, in turn, helps Apple and Google corral others into API enclosure ghettos. If users know that to get their "main" apps, they must go to the store, then the store becomes the place to look for all software. Moreover, for competitors to have any hope of equivalent profits, they must play the same Store-enabled race to the privacy bottom.

And that race to the bottom helps the duopolists, no matter how much they want you to believe otherwise.

Apple and Google are trying to maintain a distribution model chokehold over mobile software. The duopoly they have designed allows them to tax developers outrageously for access to commodity APIs. Dependence on proprietary versions of APIs, in turn, makes it hard for software vendors to consider building for other platforms with their limited engineering budgets and focus.

This, in turn, reduces the size of the (potentially) portable software catalogue, which harms the prospects of new entrants to the market. For users, the power of the native app duopoly make it hard to escape these ecosystems for less predatory alternatives.

The entire point of the multi-layered exercise, in the end, is to subvert interoperability. And to do _that_, it's necessary to keep the anchor apps happy. Which is why Apple and Google let Facebook spy on you via through the web.

Apple isn't defending your privacy, it's retreating just far enough into the hedge that you won't notice the App Store setting up Facebook, Messenger, Instagram, and TikTok to take the blame for the APIs that Apple itself has recklessly provided. Compared with browser, iOS native apps have facilitated a universe of privacy invasion that was previously unthinkable. Apple _did that_, and _continues to do that_, and now it wants credit for forcing developers to "comply" (_wink wink_) with anti-tracking rules it can't be bothered to enforce.

The answer is blindingly obvious: forbid IABs and/or force native apps to use the system-provided browser-overlay systems — [`SFSafariViewController`](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/safariservices/sfsafariviewcontroller) and [Android Custom Tabs](https://developer.chrome.com/docs/android/custom-tabs) — where user's choice of browser and customisations will be respected.

This isn't hard. In fact, it's one of the simplest interventions possible. And yet neither Apple nor Google are willing to pick a real fight with Facebook and do right by users.

And until they do, you can be certain the privacy preening is all for show.[4](#fn-the-only-solution-worth-a-damn-4)

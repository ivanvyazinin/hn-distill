Microsoft Teams is about to deal a heavy blow to those who like to work from home for peace and quiet. In a new feature update rolling out December 2025, the platform will track a worker’s location using the office Wi-Fi, to see whether you’re actually there or not.

From a boss’ perspective, this would eliminate any of that confusion as to where your team actually is. But for those people who have found their own sanctuary of peaceful productivity by working from home, consider this a warning that Teams is about to tattle on you.

## How does it tattle?

![Microsoft Teams together mode](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/ygEjWVbG3KuQLQe46wg25h.gif)

(Image credit: Microsoft)

As per the [Microsoft 365 roadmap](https://www.microsoft.com/en-gb/microsoft-365/roadmap): "When users connect to their organization's Wi-Fi, Teams will automatically set their work location to reflect the building they are working in." And the location of that worker will apparently update automatically upon connecting.

Not much more detail is given, but I can try to fill in some gaps here.

There’s already an option in Teams to [set your work location](https://support.microsoft.com/en-gb/office/set-your-work-location-in-microsoft-teams-6c14a0f5-3cd6-427d-b1d2-aa0365aebf88), which is (for now) being used to help identify where you are in, say, a large office or a campus. It’s my suspicion that this could be automated based on Wi-Fi details like IP address, to change based on what your system is connected to.

Looking back at the roadmap, this is set to launch both on Windows and macOS, and the rollout starts at the end of this year. “This feature will be off by default,” reads the announcement — good news for those who have non-techie bosses. But “tenant admins will decide whether to enable it and require end-users to opt-in.” Ouch.

## What can we learn from the history of skipping the office?

 ![Microsoft Teams](https://cdn.mos.cms.futurecdn.net/wgrBKCPqoX5XpcyQNLFeib.jpg)

(Image credit: Shutterstock)

There was a big move to get people back to working in the office, and Amazon staffers who weren't happy with that could change their SSID (home Wi-Fi name) to match the company’s official office network.

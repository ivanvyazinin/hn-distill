From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Underground structure decorated with seashells

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Shell-grotto-margate.jpg/250px-Shell-grotto-margate.jpg)](/wiki/File:Shell-grotto-margate.jpg)

The dome inside the Shell Grotto

The **Shell Grotto**, sometimes called the **Shell Temple**, is an ornate underground [shell grotto](/wiki/Shell_grotto "Shell grotto") in the [seaside town](/wiki/Seaside_town "Seaside town") of [Margate](/wiki/Margate "Margate"), [Kent](/wiki/Kent "Kent"), England. The [grotto](/wiki/Grotto "Grotto"), which has a passageway and main room, was dug out from [chalk](/wiki/Chalk "Chalk"), a soft [limestone](/wiki/Limestone "Limestone") common in this region. Almost all the surface area of the walls and roof is covered in [mosaics](/wiki/Mosaic "Mosaic") created entirely of [seashells](/wiki/Seashell "Seashell"), totalling about 2,000 square feet (190 m2) of mosaic, with approximately 4.6 million shells. Its age, creators, and purpose are unknown, which has inspired a wide range of speculation, although several other shell grottos in England were made in the 18th century. This grotto was rediscovered in about 1835 and first opened to the public as a privately-owned [tourist attraction](/wiki/Tourist_attraction "Tourist attraction") in 1837.[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1) The grotto is a Grade I-[listed building](/wiki/Listed_building "Listed building")[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1) and remains open to the public. Attached to the grotto is a modern museum room, cafe, and [gift shop](/wiki/Gift_shop "Gift shop").[\[2\]](#cite_note-2)

## Structure and decoration

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Shell_Grotto,_Margate&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Structure and decoration")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/2008-08-24_MargateGrotto.jpg/250px-2008-08-24_MargateGrotto.jpg)](/wiki/File:2008-08-24_MargateGrotto.jpg)

Inside the Shell Grotto

The Shell Grotto consists of a winding subterranean passageway, about 8 feet (2.4 m) high and 70 feet (21 m) in length, terminating in a rectangular room, referred to as the "[altar](/wiki/Altar "Altar") chamber" and measuring approximately 15 by 20 ft (5 by 6 m).

The grotto is entirely underground. Steps at the upper end lead into a passage about 3 feet 6 inches (1.07 m) wide, roughly hewn out of the chalk, which winds down in serpentine fashion until it reaches an arch, the walls and roof of which here onward are covered in with shell mosaic. The arch leads to what is known as the "[rotunda](/wiki/Rotunda "Rotunda")", a central circular column, meeting at the farther side at the dome — a shaft rising to the surface, capped to allow some daylight into the structure. The sub-base of the dome is triangular, equilateral, and with an arch in the centre of each side. The two arches in the sides are those leading from the rotunda, whilst the arch in the base leads into the "serpentine passage". This passage, with its curving walls and over-arching vaults, is rich in mosaics of varied design.

At the end of the serpentine passage, a further arch leads into the rectangular chamber. Here the decoration takes on a more formal and geometric character, but still finely drawn and executed. The subjects are chiefly star and sun shapes. The focal point, the "altar", is the curved niche, which faces the gothic-style entrance arch.[\[3\]](#cite_note-3)

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Shell_Grotto%2C_Margate%2C_Kent_3_-_2011.09.17.jpg/250px-Shell_Grotto%2C_Margate%2C_Kent_3_-_2011.09.17.jpg)](/wiki/File:Shell_Grotto,_Margate,_Kent_3_-_2011.09.17.jpg)

A detail of the shells

The most frequently used shells throughout the mosaic — [mussels](/wiki/Mussel "Mussel"), [cockles](/wiki/Cockle_\(bivalve\) "Cockle (bivalve)"), [whelks](/wiki/Whelk "Whelk"), [limpets](/wiki/Limpet "Limpet"), [scallops](/wiki/Scallop "Scallop"), and [oysters](/wiki/Oyster "Oyster") — are largely local. They could have been found in sufficient numbers from four possible bays: Walpole Bay in Cliftonville; [Pegwell Bay](/wiki/Pegwell_Bay "Pegwell Bay") especially at Shellness Point, [Cliffsend](/wiki/Cliffsend "Cliffsend"), near Richborough; Sandwich Bay, Sandwich; and [Shellness](/wiki/Shellness "Shellness") on the [Isle of Sheppey](/wiki/Isle_of_Sheppey "Isle of Sheppey"). The majority of the mosaic is formed from the flat [winkle](/wiki/Common_periwinkle "Common periwinkle"), which is used to create the background infill between the designs. However, this shell is found only rarely locally, so could have been collected from shores west of [Southampton](/wiki/Southampton "Southampton"), where it is abundant.

## History

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Shell_Grotto,_Margate&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History")\]

### Origins

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Shell_Grotto,_Margate&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Origins")\]

The origin and purpose of the structure are unknown.[\[4\]](#cite_note-:1-4) A member of the [Kent Archaeological Society](/wiki/Kent_Archaeological_Society "Kent Archaeological Society") analyzed the grotto and concluded in 2006 that it was likely a mediaeval [denehole](/wiki/Denehole "Denehole"), a small [chalk mine](/wiki/Chalk_mining "Chalk mining"), reworked and decorated in the 17th or 18th century.[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1) For example, that era of decoration would be contemporary with the [shell grotto](/wiki/Shell_grotto "Shell grotto") built around 1720 in [Pope's villa](/wiki/Pope%27s_villa "Pope's villa") in [Twickenham](/wiki/Twickenham "Twickenham").[\[5\]](#cite_note-5) The decoration may have been created or added to in the early 19th century.[\[6\]](#cite_note-:4-6)

People have come up with a variety of speculations and hypotheses for the age and purpose of the grotto, such as: it was an 18th or 19th-century rich man's [folly](/wiki/Folly "Folly");[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1); it was connected with the [Knights Templar](/wiki/Knights_Templar "Knights Templar") or [Freemasonry](/wiki/Freemasonry "Freemasonry").[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1)[\[7\]](#cite_note-7) In the late 1940s, a writer, who was also interested in the [Baconian theory of Shakespeare authorship](/wiki/Baconian_theory_of_Shakespeare_authorship "Baconian theory of Shakespeare authorship"), published a book about his theory of Phoenician origins for the shell grotto.[\[8\]](#cite_note-8) In 1952, a member of the Canadian Geographical Society claimed similarities between the shell decorations and [Bronze Age](/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") art from the [Minoan civilization](/wiki/Minoan_civilization "Minoan civilization").[\[9\]](#cite_note-9)

### Discovery

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Shell_Grotto,_Margate&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Discovery")\]

There are conflicting accounts of the grotto's discovery, although most agree on a date of 1835. The earliest reference to the discovery appears in an 1838 article in a predecessor of the _[Kentish Mercury](/w/index.php?title=Kentish_Mercury&action=edit&redlink=1 "Kentish Mercury (page does not exist)")_:[\[10\]](#cite_note-KM-1838-05-09-10)

> Belle Vue cottage, a detached residence, has been lately been purchased by a gentleman, who, having occasion for some alterations, directed the workmen to excavate some few feet, during which operation the work was impeded a large stone, the gentleman being immediately called to the spot, directed a minute examination, which led to the discovery of an extensive grotto, completely studded with shells in curious devices, most elaborately worked up, extending an immense distance in serpentine walks, alcoves, and lanes, the whole forming one of the most curious and interesting sights that can possibly conceived, and must have been executed by torch light. We understand the proprietor intends shortly to open the whole for exhibition, at small charge for admission.[\[10\]](#cite_note-KM-1838-05-09-10)

It has remained in private ownership ever since.

### Tourist attraction and historic site

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Shell_Grotto,_Margate&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Tourist attraction and historic site")\]

In 1932, a new owner took over the grotto, and soon afterwards substituted electric lighting for the gas lighting that, over the decades, had blackened the once-colourful shells. Cleaning trials show that in the majority of the grotto, the shells have lost their colour under the dirt and are white\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\].

One of the walls was damaged by [bombing during World War II](/wiki/The_Blitz "The Blitz") and later reconstructed.[\[1\]](#cite_note-:0-1)[\[11\]](#cite_note-:5-11)

In 1973, the grotto was added to the [National Heritage List for England](/wiki/National_Heritage_List_for_England "National Heritage List for England") as a Grade I [listed building](/wiki/Listed_building "Listed building").[\[6\]](#cite_note-:4-6)

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Margate_%2C_entrance%2C_Shell_Grotto_-_geograph.org.uk_-_7124805.jpg/250px-Margate_%2C_entrance%2C_Shell_Grotto_-_geograph.org.uk_-_7124805.jpg)](/wiki/File:Margate_,_entrance,_Shell_Grotto_-_geograph.org.uk_-_7124805.jpg)

Museum room and souvenir shop

It was listed for sale in 1976.[\[12\]](#cite_note-12) In 1981, new owners purchased the grotto and adjacent houses.[\[13\]](#cite_note-:3-13) In 1992, they said they averaged about 17,000 visitors during the tourist season between Easter and October, down from 30,000 in the early 1980s.[\[13\]](#cite_note-:3-13) They also operated the nearby [Margate Caves](/wiki/Margate_Caves "Margate Caves") attraction.[\[13\]](#cite_note-:3-13) In 1999, the grotto was put up for sale again.[\[14\]](#cite_note-14)

The structure has suffered the effects of water penetration. By 1999, it needed significant reinforcement and drainage improvements.[\[15\]](#cite_note-15)

The Friends of the Shell Grotto was formed in 2008 and is a not-for-profit trust established to promote, conserve, and preserve the grotto as a unique historical monument.[\[16\]](#cite_note-:2-16)

It was removed from the [_Heritage at Risk Register_](/wiki/Heritage_at_Risk_Register "Heritage at Risk Register") in 2012 after a five-year conservation programme, carried out in partnership with [Historic England](/wiki/Historic_England "Historic England"). A scheme to sponsor replacement mosaic panels, the Roundel Project, was established in 2012.[\[16\]](#cite_note-:2-16) The craftspeople who worked to restore the shell decorations were nominated for an English Heritage award in 2012.[\[17\]](#cite_note-17) Additional restoration work was undertaken in 2025.[\[11\]](#cite_note-:5-11)

The owner has participated in initiatives to bring more tourism and economic activity to Margate.[\[18\]](#cite_note-18)

## Cultural depictions

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Shell_Grotto,_Margate&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Cultural depictions")\]

Novels that feature the grotto include _The Realm of Shells_ (2006), by Sonia Overall, and _Elijah's Mermaid_ (2012), by [Essie Fox](/wiki/Essie_Fox "Essie Fox").[\[19\]](#cite_note-19)

# Airbus A320 Series Poised To Overtake Boeing 737 Family As Most-Delivered Commercial Airliner

The Airbus A320 narrowbody series is on the verge of surpassing Boeing’s best-selling 737 family to become the most-delivered commercial airliner in the world, according to data from the aviation analytics company Cirium, first reported by [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-08-16/airbus-is-about-to-eclipse-a-record-that-boeing-held-for-decades).

Launched as a direct challenger to Boeing’s long-dominant 737, the A320 program has taken nearly four decades to reach this milestone. Now, the European aircraft manufacturer has finally caught up to its US rival.

## A Closer Look Into The Numbers

    ![American Airlines Airbus A320 N659AW departure from runway 7L at Phoenix Sky Harbor Intl. Airport.](https://static1.simpleflyingimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/american-airlines-airbus-a320-n659aw-departure-from-runway-7l-at-phoenix-sky-harbor-intl-airport.jpg?q=49&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=2)Photo: Robin Guess | Shutterstock

As of early August 2025, [Airbus](/tag/airbus/) had narrowed the gap to just 20 aircraft, with lifetime deliveries of 12,155 Airbus A320-family jets, according to Cirium data. At the current pace, that difference is expected to disappear as soon as September 2025.

[In a LinkedIn post](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7358795438960910359/), Max Kingsley-Jones, head of advisory at Cirium Ascend Consultancy, noted that [![](https://static0.simpleflyingimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/boeing-icon.png?fit=contain&h=22&w=22)Boeing](/tag/boeing/) had delivered 12,175 Boeing 737s since the first -100 entered service with Lufthansa in December 1967. Airbus, meanwhile, handed over its first A320-100 to Air France in March 1988 and has since delivered 12,155 narrowbody aircraft. Kinglsey-Jones added:

> “But did anyone back then expect it could become number one – and on such high production volumes? I certainly didn’t, and nor probably did Airbus Industrie…”

## A320 and 737 Dominate The World’s Commercial Passenger Fleet

    ![Ryanair Boeing 737 Landing In Frankfurt](https://static1.simpleflyingimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/jake-hardiman-ryanair-boeing-737-frankfurt-1.JPG?q=49&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=2)Photo: Jake Hardiman | Simple Flying

Together, the Airbus A320 and Boeing 737 families account for nearly half of all passenger commercial narrowbody jets flying today. By 1988, when Airbus launched its new A320 narrowbody jet, Boeing had already established a strong lead, having delivered around 1,500 of its popular jets. But [Airbus gained an edge by being the first to upgrade its Airbus A320 with new-generation engines](https://simpleflying.com/airbus-a320-most-successful-aircraft-family/), creating the fuel-efficient “neo” variant that quickly became a favored choice among airlines.

Facing growing pressure to compete, Boeing introduced the 737 MAX series, which attempted to fit larger, more powerful engines onto the aircraft’s older design. This led to serious problems. The Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS), which was introduced due to changes in the aircraft's aerodynamics caused by larger, more forward-mounted engines, malfunctioned, contributing to two fatal 737 MAX crashes that killed 346 people onboard.

As a result, the Boeing 737 MAX was grounded worldwide. [Airlines stopped flying the aircraft](https://simpleflying.com/design-flaw-kill-boeing-737-max/), and it remained grounded until late 2020. The United States Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) and other regulators required extensive software updates, pilot training, sensor upgrades, and wiring modifications before the aircraft could return to service. By late 2020, the Boeing 737 MAX was recertified, with new safety protocols in place. Countries around the globe gradually resumed commercial flights, including China, in early 2023.

## Setting The Airbus A320 Apart

    ![SAS Airbus A320 In Stockholm](https://static1.simpleflyingimages.com/wordpress/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/lukas-souza-08-30-23-arn-sas-airbus-a320-2.jpg?q=49&fit=crop&w=825&dpr=2)Photo: Lukas Souza | Simple Flying

To make the A320 stand out, Airbus took several steps when it launched the program. It adopted digital fly-by-wire controls, which were lighter than conventional hydraulic systems, and replaced the traditional central yoke with a side-stick for the pilot. This change freed up cockpit space, improved visibility of instruments, and offered a modern, ergonomic interface.

The A320’s design included a taller landing gear, raising the aircraft higher off the ground than rivals like the Boeing 737. This allowed for larger engines, easier maintenance access, and more operational flexibility at airports with varied runway conditions.

Additionally, airlines could select between two different engine types on the Airbus A320ceo narrowbody, either CFM International or International Aero Engines (IAE). This flexibility allowed airlines to optimize for fuel efficiency, maintenance preferences, or cost considerations, making the Airbus A320 narrowbody aircraft more appealing across different markets. Later, the Airbus A320neo offered a choice between the CFM International LEAP-1A and the Pratt & Whitney PW1100G engines.

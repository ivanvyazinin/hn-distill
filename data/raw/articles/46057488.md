# Voyager 1 Is About to Reach One Light-day from Earth

_By_ [Piyush Gupta](https://scienceclock.com/author/piyush-gupta/) Published November 25, 2025

5 Min Read

![Artist’s concept of the Voyager 1 spacecraft speeding through interstellar space.](https://scienceclock.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Voyager-860x484.jpg)

Artist’s concept of the Voyager 1 spacecraft speeding through interstellar space. (Image: NASA / JPL‑Caltech)

After nearly 50 years in space, NASA’s [Voyager 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_1) is about to hit a historic milestone. By [November 15, 2026](https://science.nasa.gov/mission/voyager/where-are-voyager-1-and-voyager-2-now/), it will be 16.1 billion miles (25.9 billion km) away, meaning a radio signal will take a full 24 hours—a full light-day—to reach it. For context, a [light-year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year) is the distance light travels in a year, about 5.88 trillion miles (9.46 trillion km), so one light-day is just a tiny fraction of that.

Launched in 1977 to explore Jupiter and Saturn, Voyager 1 entered [interstellar space](https://scienceclock.com/aliens-got-bored-and-stopped-searching-humans-says-scientist/) in [2012](https://science.nasa.gov/mission/voyager/frequently-asked-questions/#:~:text=Have%20any%20human,back%20to%20Earth.), becoming the most distant human-made object ever. Traveling at around [11 miles per second (17.7 km/s)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_1#:~:text=Traveling%20at%20about%2017%C2%A0km/s%20\(11%C2%A0mi/s\)), it adds roughly 3.5 [astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit) (the distance from Earth to the Sun) each year. Even after decades in the harsh [environment](https://scienceclock.com/moss-survives-9-months-in-space-vacuum/) of [space](https://scienceclock.com/category/space/), Voyager 1 keeps sending data thanks to its [radioisotope thermoelectric generators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator), which will last into the 2030s.

Communicating with Voyager 1 is slow. Commands now take about a day to arrive, with another day for confirmation. Compare that to the [Moon](https://scienceclock.com/saturns-moon-enceladus-signals-organic-molecules-key-to-lifes-chemistry/) (1.3 seconds), [Mars](https://scienceclock.com/microbe-that-could-turn-martian-dust-into-oxygen/) (up to 4 minutes), and Pluto (nearly 7 hours). The [probe’s distance](https://scienceclock.com/are-self-replicating-alien-probes-already-in-our-solar-system-new-study-explains/) makes every instruction a patient exercise in deep-space operations. To reach our closest star, [Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri), even at light speed, would take over four years—showing just how tiny a light-day is in cosmic terms.

![The ‘Pale Blue Dot’ image of Earth, captured by Voyager 1.](https://scienceclock.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Pale_Blue_Dot.png)

The ‘Pale Blue Dot’ image of Earth, captured by Voyager 1. (Image: NASA / Public Domain)

Voyager 1’s journey is more than a record for distance. From its planetary flybys to the iconic **‘**[Pale Blue Dot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pale_Blue_Dot)’ image, it reminds us of the vast scale of the solar system and the incredible endurance of a spacecraft designed to keep exploring, even without return.

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Scientific theory in vertebrate development

Axial twist

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/AxialTwistSchema.png/330px-AxialTwistSchema.png)](/wiki/File:AxialTwistSchema.png)

Schema of the proposed development of the axial twist. Developmental phases are (from top to bottom): (1) the embryo turns on its left side; (2) the anterior head grows in the same direction, but the rest of the body grows oppositely into a twist. So that ultimately (3) external bilateral symmetry is regained. Note that there is no evolutionary pressure for internal symmetry so the heart (and other organs) remain asymmetric.

Details

[System](/wiki/Organ_system "Organ system")

[vertebrate body plan](/wiki/Body_plan "Body plan")

[Anatomical terminology](/wiki/Anatomical_terminology "Anatomical terminology")

\[[edit on Wikidata](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q122613301 "d:Q122613301")\]

The **axial twist theory** ([a.k.a.](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/also_known_as "wiktionary:also known as") axial twist _hypothesis_) is a proposed [scientific theory](/wiki/Scientific_theory "Scientific theory") to explain a range of unusual aspects of the [body plan](/wiki/Body_plan "Body plan") of [vertebrates](/wiki/Vertebrate "Vertebrate") (including [humans](/wiki/Humans "Humans")).[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1) It states that the [rostral](/wiki/Rostral_\(anatomical_term\) "Rostral (anatomical term)") part of the head is "turned around" regarding the rest of the body.[\[2\]](#cite_note-Kinsbourne2013-2) This end-part consists of the face (eyes, nose, and mouth) as well as part of the brain ([cerebrum](/wiki/Cerebrum "Cerebrum") and [thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus")). According to the theory, the vertebrate body has a left-handed [chirality](/wiki/Chirality#Biology "Chirality").

The axial twist theory competes with a [number of other proposals](#Relation_to_other_theories_and_hypotheses) that focus on more limited, specific aspects, most of which explain [contralateral forebrain organization](/wiki/Contralateral_brain "Contralateral brain"), the phenomenon that the left side of the brain mainly controls the right side of the body and vice versa.[\[3\]](#cite_note-Viggiano2012-3) None of the proposed theories explaining this phenomenon, including axial twist theory, have gained general recognition.[\[4\]](#cite_note-Mora2019-4) The genetic basis underlying the proposed developmental twist is not yet understood.

The axial twist theory would explain various anatomical phenomena, and addresses how and when the proposed twist between the end of the head and the rest of the body [develops](/wiki/Developmental_biology "Developmental biology"). It also addresses the possible evolutionary history. One prediction of the theory was the [aurofacial asymmetry](/wiki/Aurofacial_asymmetry "Aurofacial asymmetry"), which was then found [empirically](/wiki/Empirically "Empirically"),[\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5) albeit by one of the authors of the original theory.

Phenomena the theory can explain include:

*   [Contralateral organization of the brain](/wiki/Contralateral_brain "Contralateral brain")
*   Left-sided orientation of the heart
*   Asymmetric position of the [gastrointestinal tract](/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract "Gastrointestinal tract"), the [liver](/wiki/Liver "Liver"), and the [pancreas](/wiki/Pancreas "Pancreas")
*   [Optic chiasm](/wiki/Optic_chiasm "Optic chiasm")
*   [Chiasm](/wiki/Chiasm_\(anatomy\) "Chiasm (anatomy)") of the [trochlear nerve](/wiki/Trochlear_nerve "Trochlear nerve")
*   Non-crossed [olfactory tract](/wiki/Olfactory_tract "Olfactory tract")
*   Aurofacial asymmetry
*   [Yakovlevian torque](/wiki/Yakovlevian_torque "Yakovlevian torque")
*   Asymmetry of the [thoracal](/wiki/Thorax "Thorax") [vertebra](/wiki/Vertebra "Vertebra")

According to the axial twist [developmental model](/wiki/Scientific_modelling "Scientific modelling"), the [anterior](/wiki/Anterior "Anterior") part of the head turns against the rest of the body, except for the inner [organs](/wiki/Organs "Organs"). Due to this twist, the [forebrain](/wiki/Forebrain "Forebrain") and face are turned around such that left and right, but also anterior and [posterior](/wiki/Posterior_\(anatomy\) "Posterior (anatomy)") are flipped in the [adult](/wiki/Adult "Adult") vertebrate.

## History

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")\]

In the end of the 19th century, the famous [neuroscientist](/wiki/Neuroscientist "Neuroscientist") and [Nobel Prize](/wiki/Nobel_Prize "Nobel Prize") winner [Santiago Ramón y Cajal](/wiki/Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal "Santiago Ramón y Cajal") proposed a theory to explain the contralateral organization of the forebrain that was rapidly and widely accepted.[\[6\]](#cite_note-Flechsig1899-6)[\[4\]](#cite_note-Mora2019-4) This theory, the [visual map theory](/wiki/Contralateral_brain#Visual_map_theory_by_Cajal "Contralateral brain"), proposes that the optic chiasm restores the retinal image on the visual cortex,[\[7\]](#cite_note-Cajal-7) Cajal's theory remained virtually undisputed for more than a century.[\[8\]](#cite_note-Llinas2003-8)[\[9\]](#cite_note-Vulliemoz2005-9) However, more ideas have been put forward (see section [#Relation to other theories and hypotheses](#Relation_to_other_theories_and_hypotheses)).

## Embryology

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Embryology")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Axial_twist_in_zebrafish_embryo.pdf/page1-380px-Axial_twist_in_zebrafish_embryo.pdf.jpg)](/wiki/File:Axial_twist_in_zebrafish_embryo.pdf)

Antero-dorsal view on the head and anterior trunk region of a zebrafish embryo (Danio rerio) on the egg surface. Source: figure 4 of reference [\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

Although the embryological development of the axial twist has not been studied explicitly, there are clear indications from the [zebrafish](/wiki/Zebrafish "Zebrafish") and the [chick](/wiki/Chicken "Chicken").[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1) The twist begins briefly after the [neurulation](/wiki/Neurulation "Neurulation") and commences in a [rostrocaudal](/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Cranial_and_caudal "Anatomical terms of location") (front-to-tail) direction.[\[10\]](#cite_note-Bellairs2005-10)

Philipp Keller's group traced each cell of developing zebrafish embryos until the first heartbeat.[\[11\]](#cite_note-Keller2008-11) Tracing the movements of the cells in the future [eye](/wiki/Eye "Eye") region and the hind part of the head, revealed opposite movement directions, in accordance with an axial twist. Whereas the left-eye eye-region cells tendentially moved outwards and downwards ([ventrally](/wiki/Ventrally "Ventrally")), those of the right eye region moved out- and upwards, as visualized by a [time-lapse video](/wiki/Time-lapse_photography "Time-lapse photography").[\[12\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012Supp-12) On the other hand, the surface cells of the hind side of the head moved to the left, consistent with an axial twist.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

The [chick development](/wiki/Chicken#Embryology "Chicken") has been studied well. The development is usually described according to the [Hamburger–Hamilton stages](/wiki/Hamburger%E2%80%93Hamilton_stages "Hamburger–Hamilton stages").[\[13\]](#cite_note-Hamburger1951-13) The twisting begins during stage 6 on the rostral side of the head region[\[14\]](#cite_note-LopezSanchez2001-14) and commences until stage 14 towards the heart region.[\[10\]](#cite_note-Bellairs2005-10) Whereas the anterior head region rotates with the right side moving in an upward direction and the left side downward, the heart region moves in the opposite direction. In the end, the chick is turned on its right side, whereas the heart, not taking part in the twisting, has landed on the left side of the body.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

### Genetic mechanisms

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Genetic mechanisms")\]

Main articles: [Nodal signaling pathway](/wiki/Nodal_signaling_pathway "Nodal signaling pathway") and [Left-right asymmetry](/wiki/Left-right_asymmetry "Left-right asymmetry")

The axial twist emerges through opposite asymmetric development. This can be observed as a wave moving across the embryo from anterior to posterior. It is now well established, that the [Nodal signaling cascade](/wiki/Nodal_signaling_pathway "Nodal signaling pathway") and the right-to-left flow produced by [ciliated cells](/wiki/Cilium "Cilium") in the [primitive streak](/wiki/Primitive_streak "Primitive streak") are central in setting up the asymmetric organization. Three aspects of this growth wave are:[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

*   the asymmetric growth (left-side-turn) in the anterior tip of the [primitive node](/wiki/Primitive_node "Primitive node") (a.k.a. [Spemann-Mangold organizer](/wiki/Spemann-Mangold_organizer "Spemann-Mangold organizer") in [amphibians](/wiki/Amphibians "Amphibians") or Hensen's node in birds),[\[14\]](#cite_note-LopezSanchez2001-14)[\[15\]](#cite_note-Pieper2020-15)
*   the left-right [situs](/wiki/Situs_solitus "Situs solitus") of the organs and
*   the right-side-turn of the body.

Nodal, [FGF8](/wiki/FGF8 "FGF8"), and [shh](/wiki/Sonic_hedgehog_protein "Sonic hedgehog protein") as well as the motor protein [Kif5c](/wiki/KIF5C "KIF5C") have been associated with the asymmetric growth of the anterior primitive node, although only Nodal seems to be expressed before the initiation of the asymmetry.[\[15\]](#cite_note-Pieper2020-15)[\[16\]](#cite_note-Tsikolia2012-16) The [Nodal](/wiki/Nodal_homolog "Nodal homolog") gene and a [zinc finger](/wiki/Zinc_finger "Zinc finger") gene ([cSnR](/w/index.php?title=CSnR&action=edit&redlink=1 "CSnR (page does not exist)")) control the asymmetric development of the heart.[\[17\]](#cite_note-Isaac1997-17) The right-side-turn of the body is tightly linked to the same genetic mechanisms.[\[17\]](#cite_note-Isaac1997-17)

### Developmental malformations

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Developmental malformations")\]

In [holoprosencephaly](/wiki/Holoprosencephaly "Holoprosencephaly"), the hemispheres of the cerebrum (or part of it) are not aligned on the left and right sides but only on the frontal and occipital sides of the skull, and the head usually remains very small. According to the axial twist theory, this represents an extreme case of [Yakovlevian torque](/wiki/Yakovlevian_torque "Yakovlevian torque"),[\[18\]](#cite_note-Toga-18) and may occur when the cerebrum does not turn during early embryology.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

Cephalopagus or janiceps twins are [conjoined twins](/wiki/Conjoined_twin "Conjoined twin") who are born with two faces, one on either side of the head. These twins have two brains and two spinal cords, but these are located on the left and the right side of the body.[\[19\]](#cite_note-Viggiano2002-19) According to the axial twist model, the two nervous systems could not turn due to the complex configuration of the body and therefore remained on either side.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

## Evolution

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Evolution")\]

The axial twist is thought to have evolved in a common ancestor of all vertebrates, but the mechanism remains speculative. However, twisting and asymmetric development are well known from other [deuterostomes](/wiki/Deuterostomes "Deuterostomes"), such as [Echinodermata](/wiki/Echinodermata "Echinodermata"), [Cephalochordata](/wiki/Cephalochordata "Cephalochordata"), and [Tunicata](/wiki/Tunicata "Tunicata"); as well as from [gastropods](/wiki/Gastropod "Gastropod").[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

De Lussanet _et al._ posit two possible evolutionary origins: one, that an ancestral vertebrate turned to its left side during the transition from a free-swimming larva to a bottom-feeding adult stage, like modern-day [flatfishes](/wiki/Flatfish "Flatfish"). The other hypothesis is that an ancestor of vertebrates was a bottom-feeder that turned to the left to move around, with this body position evolving to the orientation of the free-swimming last common ancestor. This second hypothesis would connect to the lifecycle of the closely related Cephalochordata, which have a mouth that is initially on the left side before moving to ventral position but lack an axial twist. Following the hypothesis of Dzik _et al._ (1995), de Lussanet suggests that if the second hypothesis is correct, then the first stage was the enigmatic [Ediacaran](/wiki/Ediacaran "Ediacaran") fossil _[Dickinsonia](/wiki/Dickinsonia "Dickinsonia")_, and the second stage is represented by the early [Cambrian](/wiki/Cambrian "Cambrian") _[Yunnanozoon](/wiki/Yunnanozoon "Yunnanozoon")_.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

Even the most distant clades of vertebrates – the [agnathan](/wiki/Agnathan "Agnathan") [lampreys](/wiki/Lampreys "Lampreys") and [hagfish](/wiki/Hagfish "Hagfish") – possess an optic chiasm and contralateral brain organization,[\[20\]](#cite_note-Nieuwenhuys1998-20) as well as a left-sided heart and asymmetric bowels.[\[21\]](#cite_note-Marinelli1956-21) Also, every vertebrate has a contralateral organization of the forebrain.[\[20\]](#cite_note-Nieuwenhuys1998-20) Fossil skull impressions of early vertebrates from the [Ordovician](/wiki/Ordovician "Ordovician") and later show the presence of an optic chiasm.[\[22\]](#cite_note-Janvier96-22)

## Morphology

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Morphology")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/FigOppositeAsymmetry.pdf/page1-130px-FigOppositeAsymmetry.pdf.jpg)](/wiki/File:FigOppositeAsymmetry.pdf)

Caricature showing the external asymmetries due to the axial twist

The axial twist takes place in the early embryo of a vertebrate. There is an [evolutionary pressure](/wiki/Evolutionary_pressure "Evolutionary pressure") for animals towards [bilateral symmetry](/wiki/Bilateral_symmetry "Bilateral symmetry"), due to [sexual selection](/wiki/Sexual_selection "Sexual selection") (better looks to potential mates) and functional selection (e.g., better locomotion). The evolutionary pressure decreases with better symmetry. Accordingly, the pressure decreases as a body part is less associated with the body surface (less sexual selection) and the locomotor system (less functional selection).[\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5) Consequently, the axial twist theory predicts that small, systematic asymmetries remain on the outside of the body and that these asymmetries are larger on the inside of the body.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)[\[23\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2015-23)[\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5)

### Brain torque and spinal asymmetry

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Brain torque and spinal asymmetry")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Yakovlev_and_thoracal_spine_asymmetries.pdf/page1-380px-Yakovlev_and_thoracal_spine_asymmetries.pdf.jpg)](/wiki/File:Yakovlev_and_thoracal_spine_asymmetries.pdf)

Opposite rotational asymmetries as viewed from below. Left: the Yakovlevian torque in the cerebrum (exaggerated). Redrawn from Toga & Thompson.[\[24\]](#cite_note-Toga2003-24) Right: the opposite, rightward asymmetry of the thoracal spine.[\[25\]](#cite_note-Kouwenhoven2006-25) Source: figure 4 of reference [\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5)

Main article: [Contralateral brain](/wiki/Contralateral_brain "Contralateral brain")

The forebrain ([cerebrum](/wiki/Cerebrum "Cerebrum") and [thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus")) predominantly represents the opposite side of the body (and the visual world). However, [motor control](/wiki/Motor_control "Motor control") usually requires information from both sides of the body, and so the [contralateral representation is by no means absolute](/wiki/Contralateral_brain#The_contralateral_organization_is_incomplete "Contralateral brain"). Rather, almost every region of the brain connects to both sides of the body.[\[26\]](#cite_note-Ebbesson1980-26)

The [Yakovlevian torque](/wiki/Yakovlevian_torque "Yakovlevian torque")[\[24\]](#cite_note-Toga2003-24) (a.k.a. "counterclockwise brain torque"[\[27\]](#cite_note-Mock-27) refers to an anatomical peculiarity of the normal brain. On average, the [frontal lobes](/wiki/Frontal_lobes "Frontal lobes") are asymmetric to the left (the right lobe appears slightly larger than the left), whereas the [occipital lobe](/wiki/Occipital_lobe "Occipital lobe") is asymmetric to the right; the [central sulcus](/wiki/Central_sulcus "Central sulcus") and [temporal lobe](/wiki/Temporal_lobe "Temporal lobe") of the right cortical hemisphere are further to the front than those on the left. Overall, these asymmetries are equivalent to a slight rotation of the cerebrum (i.e. torque). Such a rotation is exactly as predicted by the axial twist theory, given that the cerebrum is not a superficial structure.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

The torque is also known as "occipital bending"[\[28\]](#cite_note-Maller-28) if it is more strongly expressed on the occipital side than on the frontal side.

The spine is slightly asymmetric. In healthy subjects, the [thoracic vertebrae](/wiki/Thoracic_vertebrae "Thoracic vertebrae") (vertebra T6-T12) were on average asymmetric, such that the mid-line points to the right (2.5° in T6).[\[25\]](#cite_note-Kouwenhoven2006-25) Thus, the Yakovlevian torque and the spinal asymmetry are in opposite direction, just as predicted by the axial twist theory.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

### Central nervous system decussations

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Central nervous system decussations")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Gray690.png/250px-Gray690.png)](/wiki/File:Gray690.png)

Some afferent decussations.

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Gray680.png/160px-Gray680.png)](/wiki/File:Gray680.png)

Pyramidal decussations.

Main article: [Decussation](/wiki/Decussation "Decussation")

If the right forebrain represents predominantly the left body and the left forebrain the right body, there should be positions of major nerve crossings behind the forebrain.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

[Anatomically](/wiki/Neuroanatomy "Neuroanatomy"), the contralateral organization of the forebrain is manifested by major [decussations](/wiki/Decussation "Decussation") (based upon the [Latin](/wiki/Latin "Latin") notation for [ten](/wiki/10 "10"), 'deca,' as an [uppercase](/wiki/Uppercase "Uppercase") 'X') and [chiasmas](/wiki/Chiasm_\(anatomy\) "Chiasm (anatomy)") (after the [Greek](/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") [uppercase](/wiki/Uppercase "Uppercase") letter 'Χ,' [chi](/wiki/Chi_\(letter\) "Chi (letter)")). A decussation denotes a crossing of bundles of [axonal](/wiki/Axonal "Axonal") fibers inside the [central nervous system](/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system"). As a result of such decussations: The [efferent connections](/wiki/Efferent_nerve_fiber "Efferent nerve fiber") of the cerebrum to the [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia"), the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum"), and the [spinal cord](/wiki/Spinal_cord "Spinal cord") are crossed; and the [afferent](/wiki/Afferent_nerve_fiber "Afferent nerve fiber") connections from the spine, the cerebellum, and the [pons](/wiki/Pons "Pons") to the thalamus are crossed.[\[20\]](#cite_note-Nieuwenhuys1998-20) Thus, motor, [somatosensory](/wiki/Somatosensory "Somatosensory"), [auditory](/wiki/Auditory_system "Auditory system"), and visual primary regions in the forebrain predominantly represent the contralateral side of the body.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

Most [afferent](/wiki/Afferent_nerve_fiber "Afferent nerve fiber") and [efferent](/wiki/Efferent_nerve_fiber "Efferent nerve fiber") connections of the forebrain have bilateral components, especially outside the primary sensory and motor regions.[\[26\]](#cite_note-Ebbesson1980-26)

### Visual system

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Visual system")\]

Main articles: [Visual system](/wiki/Visual_system "Visual system") and [Optic chiasm](/wiki/Optic_chiasm "Optic chiasm")

See also: [Contralateral brain § The contralateral organization is incomplete](/wiki/Contralateral_brain#The_contralateral_organization_is_incomplete "Contralateral brain")

In the visual system, the eyes and its muscles lie in front of the twist, while the sensory and motor centers lie behind the twist. Thus, the developing nerves are predicted to seek their insertion on the opposite side of their origin.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

Four of the [cranial nerves](/wiki/Cranial_nerves "Cranial nerves") serve the eye directly: one sensory and three motor nerves. The [optic nerve](/wiki/Optic_nerve "Optic nerve") is sensory and crosses the midline in the [optic chiasm](/wiki/Optic_chiasm "Optic chiasm"). The [oculomotor nerve](/wiki/Oculomotor_nerve "Oculomotor nerve"), [trochlear nerve](/wiki/Trochlear_nerve "Trochlear nerve"), and [abducens nerve](/wiki/Abducens_nerve "Abducens nerve") are motor nerves that control one or more of the eye muscles. The oculomotor nerve crosses the midline before leaving the central nervous system. The trochlear nerve crosses the midline in a chiasma on the dorsal side and the abducens innervates an eye muscle on the same side.[\[29\]](#cite_note-Nieuwenhuys2008-29): Fig. 17.8 

In the light of the axial twist theory, this complicated pattern can be understood. The eyes, like the mouth and the nose originate from the anterior head region, i.e. in front of the twist. The only cranial nerve that originates from the forebrain is the [olfactory nerve](/wiki/Olfactory_nerve "Olfactory nerve") (see below). All other cranial nerves originate from regions of the central nervous system that lie behind the twist.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

The optic nerve inserts on the [optic tectum](/wiki/Optic_tectum "Optic tectum") of the [midbrain](/wiki/Midbrain "Midbrain"). In [tetrapods](/wiki/Tetrapods "Tetrapods") and [bony fish](/wiki/Bony_fish "Bony fish") it also branches off to the [LGN](/wiki/LGN "LGN") of the thalamus in the forebrain, but not in other vertebrates such as [sharks](/wiki/Sharks "Sharks") and [skates](/wiki/Skate_\(fish\) "Skate (fish)")). In sharks, the visual center in the cerebrum obtains its fibers from the optic tectum. On the way, these fibers cross the midline again so that each hemisphere of the cerebrum of sharks represents the eye on the same side.[\[30\]](#cite_note-Luiten1981a-30)[\[31\]](#cite_note-Luiten1981b-31) Thus, the optic tract largely follows the prediction of the axial twist theory. The branch towards the LGN exists only in tetrapods[\[29\]](#cite_note-Nieuwenhuys2008-29) and is therefore generally understood as acquired later in the evolution of vertebrates and thus makes an exception.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

The [abducens nucleus](/wiki/Abducens_nucleus "Abducens nucleus") is located in the [pons](/wiki/Pons "Pons"). The abducens nerve innervates the [lateral rectus muscle](/wiki/Lateral_rectus_muscle "Lateral rectus muscle") of the eye in most vertebrates, except lampreys and hagfishes.[\[21\]](#cite_note-Marinelli1956-21) It thus seems that the lateral rectus muscle evolved later and independently of the other eye muscles, and presents an exception to the axial twist model.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

### Olfactory system

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Olfactory system")\]

See also: [Olfactory system § Clinical significance](/wiki/Olfactory_system#Clinical_significance "Olfactory system")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Sobo_1909_623.png/250px-Sobo_1909_623.png)](/wiki/File:Sobo_1909_623.png)

The olfactory (smelling) tracts do not chiasmate

The [olfactory tracts](/wiki/Olfactory_tracts "Olfactory tracts") run parallel to the optic tract but do not form a chiasm. Accordingly, each [olfactory bulb](/wiki/Olfactory_bulb "Olfactory bulb") connects to the same-side centers of the frontal cerebrum. This is entirely consistent with the axial twist theory because the nose is part of the anterior head region which twists along with the forebrain.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] Since the primary olfactory centers are at home in the cerebrum (olfaction is the only sense that originates in the cerebrum), each olfactory lobe is predicted to be represented by the cerebrum on the same side, which is indeed the case: whereas the olfactory tracts neighbour the optic tracts directly they do not chiasmate but insert on the ipsilateral side of the cerebrum (See figure).\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

### Aurofacial asymmetry

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Aurofacial asymmetry")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Face-schema_aurofacial_asymmetry.png/250px-Face-schema_aurofacial_asymmetry.png)](/wiki/File:Face-schema_aurofacial_asymmetry.png)

Exaggerated schema of the aurofacial asymmetry as predicted by the axial twist theory. During embryology and development, the face elements (red) are predicted to move toward the center from the left, with respect to the mid-plane between the ears. Source: figure 1c of reference[\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5)

Main article: [Facial symmetry § Aurofacial asymmetry](/wiki/Facial_symmetry#Aurofacial_asymmetry "Facial symmetry")

The aurofacial asymmetry expresses the position of the face (eyes, nose, mouth) with respect to the plane [perpendicular](/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the axis through the ears. As shown in the graph, the asymmetry decreases until the age of 13. Since the axial twist is located between the ears and the face, it is predicted that the face grows from the left to the midline, as is indeed the case.[\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5)

### Orientation of internal organs

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Orientation of internal organs")\]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Stomach_colon_rectum_diagram-en.svg/250px-Stomach_colon_rectum_diagram-en.svg.png)](/wiki/File:Stomach_colon_rectum_diagram-en.svg)

Diagram of the human stomach, intestines, and rectum.

Main article: [Heart development](/wiki/Heart_development "Heart development")

The inner organs of the trunk are the regions on the body that are least mechanically associated with locomotion and the external body, and so are predicted by the axial twist theory to be the most asymmetric regions of the body. Other bilaterally symmetric animals such as [insects](/wiki/Insect_morphology "Insect morphology") and [annelids](/wiki/Annelids "Annelids") are bilaterally symmetric also on the inside. The asymmetric development of the heart is well-researched.[\[17\]](#cite_note-Isaac1997-17)[\[32\]](#cite_note-Kidokoro2022-32)

The question of why the heart should have a left-sided orientation, has been topic of scientific research before the axial twist theory was published, but none of the hypotheses could stand critical testing.[\[33\]](#cite_note-Voronov2004-33)

The asymmetric orientation and position of the gastrointestinal tract arises during development by rotation (see [Development of the digestive system](/wiki/Development_of_the_digestive_system "Development of the digestive system")). The lateral positions of the digestive organs are on the same side in all vertebrates [liver](/wiki/Liver "Liver") and [gall bladder](/wiki/Gall_bladder "Gall bladder") on the right side and the [stomach](/wiki/Stomach "Stomach"), [pancreas](/wiki/Pancreas "Pancreas"), [spleen](/wiki/Spleen "Spleen") and the final loop of the [colon](/wiki/Colon_\(anatomy\) "Colon (anatomy)") on the left side.[\[34\]](#cite_note-westheide2004-34)

## Relation to other theories and hypotheses

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Relation to other theories and hypotheses")\]

See also: [Contralateral brain § Theories](/wiki/Contralateral_brain#Theories "Contralateral brain")

There are no other theories or hypotheses that explain the entire spectrum covered by the axial twist theory,\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] but a number of theories and hypotheses address isolated aspects in various depths.

### Inversion hypothesis

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Inversion hypothesis")\]

Main article: [Inversion (evolutionary biology)](/wiki/Inversion_\(evolutionary_biology\) "Inversion (evolutionary biology)")

In 1822 the French zoologist [Étienne Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire](/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Geoffroy_Saint-Hilaire "Étienne Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire") noted that the organization of dorsal and ventral structures in the [crayfish](/wiki/Crayfish "Crayfish") (an [arthropod](/wiki/Arthropod "Arthropod")) is opposite that of mammals, and he proposed that mammals and other vertebrates are turned upside down.[\[35\]](#cite_note-Hilaire1822-35)[\[36\]](#cite_note-Dixon1907-36) As explained above, Marcel Kinsbourne proposed that the body (soma) but not the anterior head is inverted (hence somatic twist hypothesis).[\[2\]](#cite_note-Kinsbourne2013-2)

There is molecular evidence for the inversion hypothesis in almost all groups of deuterostomes.[\[37\]](#cite_note-NublerJung1996-37)[\[38\]](#cite_note-Arendt1994-38)

### Somatic twist hypothesis

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Somatic twist hypothesis")\]

Marcel Kinsbourne's somatic twist hypothesis is most closely related to the axial twist theory. Both theories were presented as an improvement to the [dorsoventral inversion hypothesis](/wiki/Inversion_\(evolutionary_biology\) "Inversion (evolutionary biology)") by the early 19th-century [naturalist](/wiki/Naturalist "Naturalist") [Étienne Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire](/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Geoffroy_Saint-Hilaire "Étienne Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire")[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)[\[2\]](#cite_note-Kinsbourne2013-2) In contrast to the axial twist, the somatic twist does not address the developmental aspects. Also, it only explains the contralateral brain but not the orientation of the internal organs.[\[23\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2015-23)

### Asymmetry of the spine

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Asymmetry of the spine")\]

No hypotheses have been published for the asymmetry of the spine. There is no other theory to explain the left position of the heart or the asymmetric orientation of the bowels and related organs.[\[25\]](#cite_note-Kouwenhoven2006-25)[\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5)

### Contralateral forebrain

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Contralateral forebrain")\]

Several hypotheses have proposed a function for (certain aspects of) the [contralateral brain](/wiki/Contralateral_brain "Contralateral brain") (i.e. the left forebrain representing mostly the right body and the right forebrain representing mostly the left body). Most prominent is Cajal's.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

#### Cajal's visual map theory

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Cajal's visual map theory")\]

The [visual map theory](/wiki/Contralateral_brain#Visual_map_theory_by_Cajal "Contralateral brain") by Santiago Ramón y Cajal proposes that the optic chiasm restores the retinal image on the visual cortex.[\[7\]](#cite_note-Cajal-7)[\[8\]](#cite_note-Llinas2003-8) Even though the theory is still supported,[\[4\]](#cite_note-Mora2019-4) several studies have pointed out the serious flaws in the theory.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)[\[2\]](#cite_note-Kinsbourne2013-2)[\[39\]](#cite_note-Victor2000-39) Most importantly, the loop of the [optic radiation](/wiki/Optic_radiation "Optic radiation") undoes the potential repair of the optic projection on the cortex, which is the central idea of the theory. Also, the theory does not hold for important groups of vertebrates such as sharks because their cerebrum has an ipsilateral visual representation but a contralateral somatosensory and motor representation.[\[30\]](#cite_note-Luiten1981a-30)[\[31\]](#cite_note-Luiten1981b-31)

#### Other functional hypotheses

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Other functional hypotheses")\]

Functional[\[40\]](#cite_note-Bertin-40)[\[41\]](#cite_note-Loeb1918-41) or topological[\[42\]](#cite_note-Shinbrot-42) explanations fail because same-side connections are just as important as crossing ones.[\[43\]](#cite_note-Braitenberg1984-43) Moreover, these explanations leave open why the [brainstem](/wiki/Brainstem "Brainstem") and the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum") have a same-side organization of the body.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)

#### Brain lateralization

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Brain lateralization")\]

The Yakovlevian torque is often thought to reflect the lateralization of specific functions in the brain.[\[24\]](#cite_note-Toga2003-24)

The parcellation theory proposes that an increasing brain size can conserve coincidental contralateral organization,[\[26\]](#cite_note-Ebbesson1980-26) but does not explain the optic chiasm, nor that most vertebrates have a rather small forebrain, especially the early forms.[\[44\]](#cite_note-Janvier1996-44)

## Open questions

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Axial_twist_theory&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Open questions")\]

The axial twist theory is a novel [scientific](/wiki/Science "Science") discipline and very few [scientific papers](/wiki/Scientific_literature "Scientific literature") have presently addressed it directly.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)[\[2\]](#cite_note-Kinsbourne2013-2)[\[23\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2015-23)[\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5) Although a considerable volume of research exists on the genetic and embryological mechanisms of asymmetric development, an open question is how the twist is initiated and how the inversion of the left-right and up-down axes in the anterior head region is established.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

The embryology of the twisting has been addressed only rudimentarily in the chick and the zebrafish.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1) The differences in timing and mechanisms across the vertebrate clades are completely unknown.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

The evolution of the axial twist is an open question. The founders of the axial twist idea (de Lussanet & Osse, and Kinsbourne) agree that the axial twist is universal in vertebrates and probably is a feature of all chordates.[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1)[\[2\]](#cite_note-Kinsbourne2013-2) Although the asymmetric development of other chordates such as the [Lancelet](/wiki/Lancelet "Lancelet") has been [studied in detail](/wiki/Lancelet#Model_organism "Lancelet"), no study has analysed this development in the light of the axial twist theory. Moreover, even other deuterostomes, i.e. the [echinoderms](/wiki/Echinoderms "Echinoderms") ([sea stars](/wiki/Sea_stars "Sea stars"), [sea lilies](/wiki/Sea_lilies "Sea lilies"), etc.) show a marked asymmetric development and even an axial twist.[\[45\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2011-45) This twist has remarkable similarities to that in vertebrates, but no study has addressed this at present. Lastly, the asymmetric and twisted development is well known from gastropods and the relation to asymmetric development in vertebrates is an important question.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

It has been proposed that problems in the axial twist development may play a central role in developmental malformations such as holoprosencephaly[\[1\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2012-1) and skoliosis[\[5\]](#cite_note-Lussanet2019-5) but these have not been looked into.\[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

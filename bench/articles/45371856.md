# The Long Strange Trip from Silica to Smartphone

## 

Three continents, 30,000 kilometers, and 2,000-degree reactors

[Samuel K. Moore](https://spectrum.ieee.org/u/samuel-k-moore)

23 Sep 2025

5 min read



This article is part of our special report [The Scale Issue](https://spectrum.ieee.org/special-reports/scale/).



**If you want to** get a sense of the truly global scale of the electronics industry, look no further than your smartphone. The processor that powers it started as a humble rock, and by the time it found its way into your device, it had probably seen more of the world than you have. Along the way it was subjected to some of the most technologically sophisticated and closely guarded processes on the planet. Come along as we retrace that incredible 30,000-kilometer ride.

### 1\. Quartz


Your smartphone processor began its journey in the northwest corner of [Spain](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/spain), at [Mina Serrabal](https://www.ferroglobe.com/about-ferroglobe/industrial-footprint/serrabal), a [quartz](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/quartz) mine near the city of Santiago de Compostela. Quartz—or more technically, [silicon dioxide](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/silicon-dioxide) or silica—is the main component of sand. But at Serrabal it can come in huge pieces twice the width of a smartphone. Mine operator [Ferroglobe](https://www.ferroglobe.com/about-ferroglobe) runs an automated system to sort the silica by size. After the pieces are washed and treated, the big ones head to the Atlantic coast for the next step in the journey.

**Fact:** According to consultant [Thunder Said Energy](https://thundersaidenergy.com/downloads/global-polysilicon-production-capacity/), 350 million tonnes of silica was mined in 2024.

### 2\. Silicon Metal


After [an hour by truck](https://www.google.com/maps/dir/A+Coru%C3%B1a,+Spain/Mina+Serrabal,+San+Pedro+de+Vilanova,+S%2FN,+15885+Vedra,+A+Coru%C3%B1a,+Spain/@43.0740788,-8.7223238,10z/data=!3m1!4b1!4m14!4m13!1m5!1m1!1s0xd2e7cfcf174574d:0x6a47350d095cdfee!2m2!1d-8.4103923!2d43.3637626!1m5!1m1!1s0xd2fabdeec48a31f:0xd78bba10e8f3c241!2m2!1d-8.4198474!2d42.7914123!3e0?entry=ttu&g_ep=EgoyMDI1MDYxMS4wIKXMDSoASAFQAw%3D%3D), the quartz mini-boulders arrive at [Sabón](https://www.ferroglobe.com/about-ferroglobe/industrial-footprint/sab%C3%B3n/), Ferroglobe’s 125,000-square-meter factory in the coastal province of A Coruña. Here the quartz will be mixed with dehydrated wood chips and heated to 1,500 to 2,000 °C in a trio of electric-arc furnaces that use massive electrodes [invented at this plant in the 1990s](https://pyrometallurgy.co.za/InfaconVIII/389-Javier.pdf). Inside the furnace, a reaction takes place that rips the oxygen from the silica and sticks it to the carbon from the wood. The result is silicon metal and [carbon monoxide](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/carbon-monoxide).

**Fact:** 3.8 million tonnes of silicon metal was produced in 2023, according to the [U.S. Geological Survey](https://pubs.usgs.gov/periodicals/mcs2024/mcs2024-silicon.pdf).

### 3\. Purified Polysilicon


The resulting silicon metal is about 98 percent pure, and that’s not good enough. It will need to be at least 99.9999999 percent pure to become a [microprocessor](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/microprocessor), which will require some pretty powerful chemistry. So it’s off to [Wacker Chemie](https://www.wacker.com/cms/en-us/products/product-groups/polysilicon/polysilicon.html), in Burghausen, [Germany](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/germany). Here, the metal undergoes what’s called the [Siemens](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/siemens) process: It’s bathed in hydrochloric acid and reacts to form [hydrogen](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/hydrogen) gas and a liquid called trichlorosilane. Any impurities will be in the liquid, which is then run through a multistep distillation process that separates the pure trichlorosilane from anything unwanted. Once the needed purity is reached, the reaction is reversed: At 1,150 °C, the trichlorosilane is reacted with hydrogen to deposit multiple crystals of silicon, called polysilicon, and the resulting hydrochloric acid gas is sucked away. The polysilicon forms thick rods around heating elements. Once it’s cooled and removed from the reaction chamber, the polysilicon is smashed up for shipping.

**Fact:** According to consultant [Thunder Said Energy](https://thundersaidenergy.com/downloads/global-polysilicon-production-capacity/), 1.7 million tonnes of polysilicon was produced in 2024, most of that for solar-cell production.

### 4\. Silicon Wafers


The ultrapure silicon is made up of many crystals at different orientations. But [microprocessors](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/microprocessors) must be made from a single crystal. So the material might migrate to Sherman, Texas, where GlobalWafers recently opened a US $3.5 billion silicon-wafer plant. Here the polysilicon is put through what’s called the [Czochralski (Cz) method](https://spectrum.ieee.org/modern-civilization-relies-on-this-crystalgrowing-method). In a high-purity quartz crucible, the polysilicon is heated to about 1,425 °C and melts. Then a seed crystal with a [precise crystal orientation](https://spectrum.ieee.org/silicon-crystal) is dipped into the melt, slowly drawn upwards, and rotated. Do all that __exactly__ right, and you will pull up an ingot of pure, crystalline silicon that’s 300 millimeters across and several meters tall. Specialized saws then slice this pillar of semiconducting purity into wafers less than 1 millimeter thick. The wafers are cleaned, polished, and sometimes further processed, before heading to wafer fabs.

**Fact:** According to industry association SEMI, manufacturers have shipped nearly 43 million square meters of silicon wafers in the last five years. That’s enough to cover two-thirds of the island of Manhattan.

### 5\. Processed Wafers


Now it’s off to Tainan, in southern [Taiwan](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/taiwan), where [TSMC’s Fab 18](https://www.tsmc.com/english/aboutTSMC/TSMC_Fabs) will turn these wafers into the latest smartphone [processors](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/processors). It’s an exceedingly intricate process, involving some of the most complex and expensive equipment on the planet, including [EUV lithography systems](https://spectrum.ieee.org/high-na-euv) that can cost upward of $300 million each. In Fab 18, each wafer will go through months of exquisitely precise torture to produce the [transistors](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/transistors) and wiring that make up the processors. [Extreme ultraviolet](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/extreme-ultraviolet) [radiation](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/radiation) will print patterns onto it, hot ions will ram into its surface, precision chemical reactions will build up some parts one atomic layer at a time, acids will etch away nanometer-scale structures, and metals will electrochemically plate parts and be polished away in others. The result: a wafer full of identical processors.

**Fact:** The maximum size of a silicon die is 858 mm2. Within a chip, there are more than 160 kilometers of wiring. Apple’s M3 Max processor contains 92 billion transistors.

### 6\. Packaged Chips


As amazing as these processors are, you can’t use them in this form. They first need to be packaged. For our silicon, that’s going to happen at [ASE’s facility in Penang](https://ase.aseglobal.com/press-room/asem-launch-p5/), [Malaysia](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/malaysia). A package provides the chip with mechanical protection, a way for heat to be removed, and a way of connecting the chip’s micrometer-scale parts to a circuit board’s millimeter-scale ones. To do this, the wafers are first diced into chips. Then tiny balls of solder, some only tens of micrometers across, are attached to the chips. The solder bumps are aligned to corresponding parts of the package, and the two parts are melted together. It’s becoming more common for multiple pieces of silicon to be integrated within the same package, either stacked on top of each other or positioned next to each other on a separate piece of silicon called an interposer. Other steps to the process follow, and the packaged part is now ready for its next step.

**Fact:** Stacking multiple chips within a package could lead to [GPUs](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/gpus) with 1 trillion transistors by 2033

### 7\. Smartphones


Our packaged chip arrives next in southern [India](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/india), at [Foxconn’s new $2.56 billion assembly plant](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/business/india-business/apples-biggest-contract-manufacturer-foxconn-readies-300-acre-iphone-making-campus-in-india-with-dorms-for-30000-employees/articleshow/121332453.cms) on the outskirts of Bengaluru. The 1.2-square-kilometer site includes dormitories to house 30,000 workers, who will turn the chip, printed circuit board, touchscreen, battery, and a multitude of other components into an iPhone—one of some [25 million the company expects to produce per year at this and three other plants](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/technology/tech-news/apples-biggest-manufacturing-partner-foxconn-expands-india-operations-25-million-iphones-30000-dormitories-and-/articleshow/121341390.cms).

**Fact:** Processors and other [logic chips](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/logic-chips) made up 12 percent of the $463 billion cost of [smartphones](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/smartphones) and other [mobile devices](https://spectrum.ieee.org/tag/mobile-devices) in 2022, according to the Yole Group
